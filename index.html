Fortune / Anthropic Research

Anthropic Maps AI Job Exposure: A "Great Recession for White-Collar Workers" Is Now Possible — and Young Graduates Are Most at Risk

📊
  • Anthropic's landmark "Labor Market Impacts of AI" report introduces "observed exposure" — a new metric comparing theoretical AI capability against real-world Claude usage data — finding AI is barely scratching the surface of what it could automate
  • For computer and math workers, large language models can theoretically handle 94% of tasks, yet Claude currently covers only 33% in observed professional use — the gap between "blue area" (possible) and "red area" (actual) is vast and closing fast
  • The most AI-exposed workers are 16 percentage points more likely to be female, earn 47% more on average, and are nearly four times as likely to hold a graduate degree — the lawyer, financial analyst, and software developer face more disruption than the warehouse worker
  • A 14% drop in the job-finding rate in AI-exposed fields since ChatGPT's launch signals a hiring slowdown rather than mass layoffs — young workers aged 22–25 saw a 16% fall in employment in AI-exposed occupations
  • The February jobs report showed employers shed 92,000 jobs, with unemployment rising to 4.4% — Block (Jack Dorsey) cut nearly 4,000 workers citing AI-enabled "smaller and flatter teams"
  • Researchers name the scenario plainly: a "Great Recession for white-collar workers" — during the 2007–2009 financial crisis, U.S. unemployment doubled from 5% to 10%; a comparable doubling in top AI-exposed occupations from 3% to 6% is now measurable in their framework
  • 30% of workers have zero AI exposure — cooks, mechanics, bartenders, dishwashers — jobs requiring physical presence that no large language model can replicate, reinforcing a growing divide between knowledge-economy and physical-economy workers

Fortune / Pew Research Center

Pew Survey: 57% of Teens Use AI for Schoolwork — But 64% of Parents Don't Know the Full Picture

57%
  • New Pew Research Center survey of 1,458 U.S. teens and parents (Sept.–Oct. 2025) finds 57% of teens use AI to search for information and 54% use it specifically to help with schoolwork
  • 4 in 10 teens use AI chatbots to summarize articles, books, and videos — including texts they may never read in full; 1 in 10 admits to using AI for most or all of their schoolwork
  • A striking "perception gap": 64% of teens report using chatbots, but only 51% of parents believe their teen uses AI — nearly 3 in 10 parents say they simply don't know if their child uses these tools
  • 64% of parents approve of AI for article and book summaries; 58% approve for homework help — parental acceptance is higher than many educators expected
  • A Brookings report released alongside the survey calls generative AI the "fast food of education," warning it provides "frictionless" outcomes while subtracting from the learning experience
  • About 25% of teens remain concerned about AI's societal impact — most commonly citing fears of over-reliance, loss of creativity, and erosion of critical thinking skills

KSL / Deseret News — Utah Legislature

Utah Passes SB322: A First-of-Its-Kind "AI Regulatory Sandbox" for K-12 Classrooms — Not a Ban, Not a Blank Check

⚖️
  • Utah's Senate approved SB322, sponsored by Sen. John Johnson (R-North Ogden), creating an "Educational Technology Regulatory Sandbox" — a structured, time-limited pilot program to test AI tools in real classrooms before any statewide commitment
  • Participation is entirely voluntary for schools, educators, parents, and students — but vendors must complete "red teaming" adversarial testing before any AI system can enter the pilot
  • No AI system under SB322 can assign final grades, make placement decisions, or override a teacher's professional judgment without human review and approval — the "human in the loop" requirement is a condition of pilot authorization, not a suggestion
  • AI systems are prohibited from simulating romantic or personal relationships with students — a direct response to a 2025 Common Sense Media finding that 1 in 3 teens have used AI companions for social interactions
  • Students must be informed when interacting with AI, can request human-educator review of AI-generated decisions, and cannot be penalized for declining AI tools
  • Johnson: "This bill does not ask us to trust that AI works. It requires proof." — Independent evaluators must perform pre- and post-tests to verify that AI systems actually produce measurable learning results before statewide adoption
  • Utah already operates the country's first State Office of Artificial Intelligence Policy; SB322 now awaits a final House vote to become the nation's most detailed K-12 AI governance framework

Iowa Capital Dispatch — University of Northern Iowa

University of Northern Iowa Launches Two New AI Bachelor's Degrees — One in Business, One in Mathematics

🎓
  • The University of Northern Iowa is launching two new undergraduate AI majors: one in the Wilson College of Business focused on how AI supports strategy, operations, and problem-solving inside organizations; and one in the College of Humanities, Arts and Sciences focused on the mathematics of machine learning and deep learning
  • The business AI major covers machine learning, predictive analytics, ethics, governance, and accountability — preparing graduates for roles as business intelligence analysts, AI architects, product managers, and data scientists in finance, insurance, and manufacturing
  • The mathematics of AI major covers calculus, linear algebra, discrete mathematics, statistics, and computer programming alongside machine learning theory — preparing students for graduate study or careers in technology, health care, and government
  • UNI is also launching a new certificate in Applied AI for Business, focusing on practical AI applications for professionals who need AI fluency without a full degree
  • Mathematics Department head Douglas Mupasiri: "A mathematically trained AI professional can diagnose model failures, understand uncertainty and risk, and adapt to new technologies as the field evolves. That adaptability is what employers are looking for"
  • UNI joins a national wave of universities — including Brown, Penn GSE, and LSU — racing to build structured AI education portfolios that serve students from foundational literacy to advanced technical and leadership roles

University of the Incarnate Word / NSF

NSF Awards $203K Grant to Build AI Fluency in Middle Schools Across Texas and New York

🔬
  • The University of the Incarnate Word (UIW), in collaboration with UTSA and the University at Albany (SUNY), has received a National Science Foundation grant of $203,066 to build AI fluency among middle school teachers and students in San Antonio, TX and Schenectady, NY
  • Funded through NSF's Computer Science for All (CSforAll) program, the two-year project (Fall 2025–Fall 2027) partners with Northside Independent School District and Schenectady City Schools — reaching students in required core subjects, not just elective tech classes
  • Rather than treating AI as an add-on, the project embeds AI fluency into authentic learning experiences across core middle school subjects — teachers build their own AI understanding first, then design and implement classroom activities aligned to course goals
  • Each semester, teachers participate in structured learning cycles with real-time virtual meetings for lesson design, peer feedback, and implementation — leaving with usable classroom resources, not just theoretical knowledge
  • The project directly addresses the teacher training gap identified in multiple 2026 studies: 71% of educators using AI tools report receiving no formal institutional training before implementation
  • Lead researcher Dr. Lucretia M. Fraga (UIW): the project is designed to help teachers and students develop "meaningful, responsible fluency with artificial intelligence" — one of today's most urgent educational needs

Business Insider / NYU

NYU Professor Buys Students $1 Notebooks to Restore Classroom Trust Eroded by AI

📓
  • Ayşe Baltacıoğlu-Brammer, an assistant professor of history and Middle Eastern and Islamic Studies at New York University, has replaced typed online submissions with handwritten $1 Amazon notebooks — a direct response to AI eroding trust between students and faculty
  • The professor had maintained a no-tech policy for six years due to screen distraction; the move to handwritten journals is the next step — eliminating AI-generated submissions without requiring constant suspicion or surveillance of students
  • Results so far: answers are more authentic, with visible scribbles and crossed-out words; 90% of submissions have been "a joy to read"; students have even asked to decorate notebook covers — a post about the approach received over 11,000 likes on X
  • The professor's framing: "Even if a couple of students feed the questions to the AI and then write the generated answers by hand, that's a win for me — they are still spending much more time thinking and writing"
  • The approach has added about 2 hours to her workweek but has made teaching more enjoyable — and has inspired colleagues to consider similar implementations, despite concerns about class size and handwriting legibility
  • Her broader view: "AI has democratized cheating" — before AI, only a small group could pay for ghostwritten work; generative AI has made this problem universal, requiring educators to rethink assessment from the ground up

Vinod Khosla: "All Education Should Be Free" — AI Will Replace 80% of Jobs and Make the Four-Year Degree Optional

80%
  • Vinod Khosla, founder of Sun Microsystems and Khosla Ventures, told Fortune that AI will replace two-thirds to 80% of all jobs by 2030 — including physicians, radiologists, accountants, chip designers, and salespeople — making the traditional four-year degree "a real question"
  • "All education should be free," Khosla said — predicting that when everything can be learned through technology, attending college will be more like a hobby than a necessity for most careers

  • His prediction raises a fundamental question for educators: "Do you pay a farmworker the same as an oncologist, because they happen to have the same expertise, which is the expertise of AI?" — challenging the entire value proposition of professional credentials

KQED MindShift

College Students and Professors Are Writing Their Own AI Rules — and They Don't Always Agree

40%
  • EDUCAUSE's 2025 AI Landscape Study found that fewer than 40% of higher education institutions had formal acceptable-use policies for AI as of November 2024 — leaving faculty and students to negotiate AI boundaries course by course
  • English professor Dan Cryer compares using AI to write a college essay to "bringing a forklift to the gym" — the tool does the work, but the student misses the entire point of the exercise and the education they paid for
  • Professor Isabelle Clement takes the opposite approach: she allows students to use AI for outlines, feedback on ideas, and comparing sources — her goal is for students to "interrogate" AI tools, not just use them
  • Students are also drawing their own lines: some use AI only for proofreading and checking against rubrics; others use it for outlining but insist on writing the actual text themselves; a small share use it for everything
  • The governance vacuum has created a new burden for both sides: professors must determine whether work is authentic; students must navigate conflicting rules across different courses and instructors — often without institutional guidance
  • The fragmented, course-by-course approach to AI policy is increasingly seen as unsustainable — institutions that fail to develop coherent, institution-wide frameworks risk deepening confusion and inequity among students who receive inconsistent guidance

Key Themes Emerging

AI Is Reshaping the Value of Education Itself

Vinod Khosla's prediction that "all education should be free" and that the four-year degree is "a real question" is no longer fringe — it reflects a growing consensus among technologists and a measurable shift in public opinion. With only 35% of Americans saying college is "very important" (a record low), and Anthropic's research showing AI can theoretically handle 94% of tasks in high-skill knowledge occupations, the fundamental question for educators is no longer how to integrate AI, but what education is for in a world where AI provides expertise on demand.

Young Workers Are the First to Feel the Hiring Slowdown

Anthropic's landmark labor market report reveals that AI's impact on employment is not arriving through mass layoffs but through a quieter, more insidious mechanism: a 14–16% drop in hiring rates for young workers in AI-exposed fields. Recent graduates entering the job market in 2026 face a structural disadvantage — the entry-level roles that once served as on-ramps to professional careers are being absorbed by AI before new graduates can fill them. This is the defining career challenge for the Class of 2026.

State Legislatures Are Stepping In Where Institutions Have Not

Utah's SB322 — creating a first-of-its-kind AI regulatory sandbox for K-12 classrooms with mandatory red-teaming, human-in-the-loop requirements, and student due process protections — represents a new phase in AI education governance. With fewer than 40% of higher education institutions having formal AI policies and West Virginia's superintendent testifying before Congress that AI regulation should remain at the state level, the policy landscape is fragmenting. States are becoming the primary arena for AI education governance, with dramatically different approaches emerging across the country.

The Trust Crisis Is Forcing Radical Assessment Redesign

The NYU professor who bought her students $1 notebooks to restore classroom trust is not an outlier — she is a leading indicator. As AI makes typed submissions unreliable as evidence of student learning, educators across the country are redesigning assessments from the ground up: handwritten journals, oral examinations, in-class demonstrations, and portfolio-based evaluations. The question is no longer whether AI can write a student's essay — it clearly can — but what kinds of assessment tasks remain meaningful when AI can complete most of them.

Teacher Training Remains the Critical Bottleneck

The NSF's $203,066 grant to UIW for middle school AI fluency — and the structured, teacher-first approach it embeds — reflects a growing recognition that AI adoption in education will succeed or fail at the teacher level. Multiple 2026 studies converge on the same finding: 71% of educators using AI tools received no formal training before implementation. The schools and districts that invest in structured, ongoing teacher AI education — not one-time workshops but embedded professional learning cycles — will produce measurably better outcomes than those where adoption remains self-directed and informal.

University AI Programs Are Multiplying — But Coherence Is Lacking

The University of Northern Iowa's launch of two new AI bachelor's degrees — one in business, one in mathematics — is part of a national wave of university AI program expansion. But the proliferation of AI majors, certificates, and executive courses raises a new challenge: coherence. Students, employers, and institutions are struggling to distinguish between AI programs that build genuine capability and those that are credential-washing — attaching the "AI" label to existing curricula without substantive redesign. The universities that build the most rigorous, outcomes-linked AI pathways will define the standard for the field.
