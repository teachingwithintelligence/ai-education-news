Pew Research Center

Pew Research: 54% of U.S. Teens Use AI for Schoolwork — 59% Say AI Cheating Is Common at Their School

54%
  • New Pew Research Center survey of teens ages 13–17 finds 57% use chatbots to search for information, while 54% specifically use AI for help with schoolwork
  • About 30% of teens report using AI chatbots daily — a significant shift from occasional experimentation to routine academic integration
  • A majority of teens (59%) say AI cheating is a regular occurrence at their school, with about a third saying it happens "extremely or very often"
  • One-in-ten teens report doing all or most of their schoolwork with AI's help; 21% do "some" and 23% do "a little" — leaving just 45% who haven't used AI for schoolwork
  • About 25% of all teens say chatbots have been "extremely or very helpful" for completing schoolwork, with another 25% saying "somewhat helpful"
  • Teens are more likely to view AI's impact on their own lives positively (36%) than negatively (15%), though concern rises when asked about AI's effect on society broadly (26% negative)
  • AI literacy awareness is high — over 95% of teens have heard at least a little about AI chatbots — but confidence in using them varies widely

Virginia Mercury

Virginia Lawmakers Propose AI Guardrails for Schools — Bills Would Restrict Chatbot Use in Classrooms

⚖️
  • Virginia lawmakers are advancing legislation to regulate AI use in K-12 classrooms, citing concerns over student safety, critical thinking, and learning outcomes
  • House Bill 1186 would require school boards to prohibit students from being "required, encouraged, or permitted" to use AI chatbots for instruction or assignments
  • Senate Bill 394 would establish a pilot program for practical AI use in public schools, requiring the Board of Education to publish guidance on "safe, ethical, and equitable" AI use
  • Del. Sam Rasoul called the gap between AI advancement and education's ability to keep pace "an educational crisis" requiring "prompt and significant measures"
  • Sen. Stella Pekarsky, a former school board member, warned against allowing AI to "proliferate unchecked" while acknowledging its potential value for students and teachers
  • Both bills are on track to pass the General Assembly and are likely to be revised before final approval
  • Virginia joins a growing list of states scrambling to craft AI policies as federal guidance from Congress remains limited

ISTE+ASCD / Google

Google & ISTE+ASCD Launch Largest-Ever AI Teacher Training Initiative — 6 Million Educators to Be Trained

6M
  • ISTE+ASCD and Google announced a three-year partnership to provide free AI literacy training to 6 million K-12 teachers and higher education faculty across the United States
  • Described as the largest coordinated effort to prepare educators to use AI, the program will cover pedagogy, ethics, critical thinking, and responsible use
  • Training will include hands-on exploration of Google's Gemini models and NotebookLM, with certifications recognizing demonstrated understanding
  • More than 60% of K-12 teachers already used AI tools in classrooms in 2025 — nearly double the share from two years prior — but only half have received any formal training
  • Anthropic, Microsoft, and OpenAI separately pledged $23 million to the American Federation of Teachers to develop a "National Academy for AI Instruction" for 400,000 teachers
  • Critics warn that industry-sponsored teacher training amounts to "customer acquisition" campaigns, raising concerns about corporate influence in education
  • Education experts testifying before Congress called for federal "guidance and guardrails" on AI, noting teachers currently rely on a "grab bag" of advice from states, tech companies, and even TikTok

College Board Research

College Board: 92% of Faculty Fear AI Plagiarism — 84% Say AI Reduces Critical Thinking in Students

92%
  • College Board survey of 3,000+ U.S. college faculty finds 92% are concerned about plagiarism or dishonesty facilitated by AI — a near-universal level of alarm
  • More than 84% of faculty agree that AI reduces students' critical thinking, originality, and deep engagement with course material
  • 74% of faculty report students are using AI to write essays or papers; 67% say students use it to paraphrase or rewrite content
  • Faculty sentiment skews negative overall: 45% report a negative view of AI in higher education vs. 34% positive — yet 77% have used AI themselves in their professional role
  • Faculty at more selective institutions report higher levels of student AI use and greater concern, while faculty at open-enrollment colleges are more likely to view AI as a practical instructional tool
  • Writing-intensive disciplines (English, history, humanities) report the highest classroom disruption; STEM and business faculty are more likely to use AI themselves and view it positively
  • Only 21% of faculty feel "very confident" guiding AI use in their classrooms; 79% say they are still beginning to explore what's needed or need more guidance

Valparaiso University Launches AI Practitioner Certificate Open to All Majors — Fall 2026

🎓
  • Valparaiso University will launch a 12-credit, four-course AI Practitioner Certificate program in fall 2026, open to undergraduate students of all majors and disciplines
  • Program covers both technical AI knowledge and ethical, productive use in the modern workforce — starting with "Fundamentals of AI" followed by electives in machine learning, data mining, and creative coding
  • Students complete a capstone project demonstrating AI skills applicable to any field — from chemistry research to creative writing to business analysis
  • Dean Gregg Johnson: "There's an expectation that employers demand new employees understand and be able to use AI — but also do it in a way that doesn't produce gibberish or hallucinations"

  • Prof. Cukurtepe: "You won't lose your job to AI, but you may lose your job to people who know how to use AI. We're trying to educate students to use AI ethically, responsibly, and usefully"

Investopedia

AI Cuts Entry-Level Jobs — Graduate School Applications Surge as Recent Grads Face 5.6% Unemployment

5.6%
  • As of November 2025, 5.6% of recent college graduates were unemployed, compared to 4.2% of all workers — a widening gap driven by AI displacement of entry-level roles
  • Entry-level positions are being disproportionately replaced by AI, with many young workers feeling unprepared to enter a workforce increasingly advancing with the technology
  • Law school admissions during the 2024–2025 cycle reached the highest level in over a decade, as bachelor's degree holders seek alternative paths amid the slowing labor market
  • Graduate school is becoming a more attractive option — delaying loan repayment and typically leading to higher-paying roles than bachelor's-level positions
  • Fields projected to have the highest job openings for graduate degree holders: mental health counselors, lawyers, and career counselors — roles requiring advanced human judgment
  • A Harvard Business School report warns AI-based automation could make nearly 18 million entry-level jobs in the U.S. obsolete — about 12% of the total workforce
  • Computer science graduates bucking the trend: despite "job apocalypse" warnings, CS class of 2026 is on track to earn $81,000 right out of college, per Fortune

Key Themes Emerging

Teen AI Use Reaches Mainstream Scale

Pew Research confirms that over half of U.S. teens now use AI chatbots for schoolwork, with 30% using them daily. The majority of teens believe AI cheating is common at their schools, yet most also view AI positively for their own futures — creating a complex tension between academic integrity and practical skill-building that educators and policymakers are still struggling to address.

State-Level Policy Race Intensifies

Virginia's proposed legislation — including a bill that would ban schools from requiring or encouraging AI chatbot use — reflects a national scramble to regulate AI in K-12 before federal guidance arrives. With Congress still seeking "guardrails," states are moving independently, creating a patchwork of policies that could significantly vary student AI experiences across the country.

Massive Push for Teacher AI Literacy

Google's partnership with ISTE+ASCD to train 6 million educators — combined with a $23 million AFT initiative backed by Anthropic, Microsoft, and OpenAI — signals that Big Tech is moving aggressively into the teacher training space. While the scale is unprecedented, critics warn that industry-led professional development may prioritize product adoption over genuine pedagogical improvement.

Faculty Alarm at Near-Universal Levels

The College Board's survey of 3,000+ faculty reveals a profession in crisis: 92% fear AI plagiarism, 84% believe AI undermines critical thinking, yet 77% use AI themselves. The divide between writing-intensive and STEM disciplines is sharpening, with humanities faculty experiencing the most disruption while remaining the least equipped with institutional support or clear guidance.

Universities Launching AI Programs for All Majors

Valparaiso University's new AI Practitioner Certificate — open to all disciplines, not just computer science — reflects a growing consensus that AI literacy must be embedded across the entire curriculum. From English majors to nursing students, universities are recognizing that AI competency is becoming a baseline employment expectation, not a specialized technical skill.

Job Market Reshaping Higher Education Choices

With recent graduate unemployment at 5.6% and AI eliminating entry-level roles at scale, students are making strategic pivots — law school applications are at a decade high, and graduate programs in human-centered fields like counseling are seeing surging demand. The job market is effectively forcing higher education to reckon with what skills remain distinctly human in an AI-augmented economy.
