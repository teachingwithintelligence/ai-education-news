Government Technology / New York Daily News — NYC Department of Education

NYC Schools Release Landmark AI Guidelines — "Traffic Light" Framework Governs Nation's Largest School District

Traffic Light
  • New York City Public Schools — the nation's largest school district — released long-awaited AI guidance built around a "traffic light" framework: red (prohibited), yellow (proceed with caution), and green (approved) use cases for teachers and students
  • Red-light prohibitions include using AI to make decisions about student discipline, graduation, promotion, or placement in advanced classes; AI cannot be used to develop IEPs for students with disabilities, nor to surveil or counsel students
  • Green-light approvals include teachers using AI for lesson planning, unit planning, scheduling, resource planning, and drafting non-essential communications — while students may use AI for "research, exploration, and creative projects" under yellow-light caution
  • Student data cannot be used to train AI models; a 10-step data privacy review process is required before any AI tool can be used in a public school — though the current process does not yet evaluate for algorithmic bias
  • Schools Chancellor Kamar Samuels: "AI can be used as a powerful tool to make the work of our educators more efficient, giving them more time to focus on supporting our students as they develop essential critical thinking skills"
  • A more comprehensive AI "playbook" is expected in June 2026 after 45 days of public comment — with multiple school boards having already passed resolutions calling for an AI moratorium of up to two years

Government Technology / The Times-Picayune — Louisiana Board of Regents

LSU Launches First AI Bachelor's Degree in Louisiana — Plus New 3-Year Accelerated Degree Track

BS in AI
  • The Louisiana Board of Regents approved a Bachelor of Science in Artificial Intelligence at LSU — the first AI bachelor's degree in the state — focused on training students to be AI engineers, not just AI users
  • Curriculum includes rigorous mathematics, computer science, and AI theory, with an industry capstone course where students consult in teams on AI solutions for area employers in energy, petrochemical, health care, defense, and logistics sectors
  • The Board also created a new degree classification: the 3-year accelerated bachelor's degree (90 credit hours vs. the standard 120), dubbed the "AccB" — first offered in information technology and bioinformatics at LSU Alexandria
  • Deputy Commissioner Tristan Denley: "It is a program which is not to teach people about AI, but it is a program to teach people how to be AI engineers" — aligning with Louisiana's push to shape higher education around labor market demand
  • The accelerated degree model will get students into the workforce faster and at lower cost, with Louisiana lawmakers also advancing bills to prohibit state funds for degrees with low earning potential

U.S. National Science Foundation — TechAccess: AI-Ready America Initiative

NSF Launches "AI-Ready America" — $1M Per State to Build Nationwide AI Literacy Infrastructure

$1M/State
  • The National Science Foundation launched TechAccess: AI-Ready America, a nationwide effort to boost AI readiness in every U.S. state and territory — envisioning a future where every individual, community, and business thrives in an AI-driven economy
  • State-level Coordination Hubs will receive $1 million per year for three years each, with an initial round funding 10 states and subsequent expansion to all 50 states, territories, and the District of Columbia
  • Three funding pathways: Coordination Hubs (state-level networks), a National Coordination Lead (up to $3M/year for 5 years), and AI-Ready Catalyst Award competitions ($5M–$10M per competition) targeting critical AI readiness gaps
  • Partners include the USDA National Institute of Food and Agriculture, the Department of Labor Employment Training Administration, and the Small Business Administration — signaling a whole-of-government approach to AI workforce readiness
  • The initiative is informed by America's AI Action Plan and prioritizes hands-on implementation support, workforce upskilling, experiential learning through internships, and collaboration with community colleges and universities

New Roles
  • AI certification for educators is rapidly becoming a professional differentiator, with credentialed teachers gaining access to emerging roles — AI integration specialist, innovation coach, digital learning leader, curriculum redesign facilitator, and data ethics team member — that did not exist five years ago
  • Districts across the country are moving from "curiosity to urgency" on AI implementation, with superintendents asking: "How many people in our system can evaluate an AI tool for instructional alignment, bias, and data privacy?" — and finding critical gaps in human capacity

Education Week — EdWeek Research Center

EdWeek FAQ: 9 in 10 Educators Say AI Has Changed Teaching — But Cheating Fears May Be Overblown

9 in 10
  • 9 in 10 educators say that artificial intelligence has changed the job of teaching at least a little, according to a December 2024 EdWeek Research Center survey — with more than a third of teachers already using AI to plan lessons
  • Despite widespread concern, some research suggests students are not cheating more than before AI was widely available — and AI-detection tools have significant false positive rates, with a higher likelihood of incorrectly flagging the work of English language learners
  • Soft skills — communication, collaboration, critical thinking, and adaptability — are increasingly identified by teachers and business leaders as the key differentiator for students in an AI-integrated workforce, as entry-level tech jobs face displacement pressure
  • Teachers are using AI for lesson planning, differentiating schoolwork to match student skill levels, grading, and writing messages to families — but experts stress that AI tools should be an aide, not a replacement, requiring teachers to verify accuracy and check for bias
  • Schools are responding to AI's academic integrity challenges with oral exams, in-class writing with structured reflection, and annotation requirements — asking students to document how they used AI tools rather than simply prohibiting their use

Pilot Online — Higher Education AI Response

Colleges Turn to Oral Exams to Combat AI — Growing Movement Rethinks Assessment in the Age of ChatGPT

Oral Exams
  • A growing number of U.S. college instructors are turning to oral exams as a primary strategy to combat what educators are calling an "AI crisis" in higher education — where written assignments can be completed by AI tools in seconds
  • Oral examinations require students to explain, defend, and extend their thinking in real time — a format that AI tools cannot replicate on behalf of a student, making them a natural integrity safeguard in an era of generative AI
  • The shift reflects a broader reassessment of what assessment is for: if the goal is to measure what a student knows and can do, then formats that AI can complete on their behalf are no longer valid measures of student learning
  • The movement is part of a wider reimagining of higher education assessment that includes in-class writing, portfolio-based evaluation, process documentation, and AI annotation requirements — moving away from take-home written work as the default format
  • The trend aligns with research suggesting that AI raises, not lowers, the academic bar for K-12 students when used with structured reflection — with districts shifting from traditional essays to in-class writing where students must annotate their AI use

Key Themes Emerging

Municipal AI Governance Comes of Age

NYC's "traffic light" AI framework — released nearly three years after its short-lived ChatGPT ban — represents a new maturity in K-12 AI governance. By distinguishing between green-light uses (lesson planning), yellow-light uses requiring oversight (student research), and red-light prohibitions (grading, discipline, IEPs), NYC is providing the operational granularity that schools have been waiting for. The framework's 10-step data privacy review and prohibition on using student data to train AI models sets a standard that other large urban districts are likely to follow as the White House's national AI framework takes shape.

Universities Racing to Build AI Degree Infrastructure

LSU's new Bachelor of Science in AI — the first in Louisiana — joins a national wave of universities moving from AI electives to full degree programs designed from the ground up for the AI economy. LSU's approach is notable for its focus on producing AI engineers rather than AI users, with rigorous mathematics, computer science, and industry capstone courses. The simultaneous creation of a 3-year accelerated degree track signals that universities are also rethinking the economics of education — getting students into the workforce faster and at lower cost, aligned with labor market demand.

Federal AI Literacy as National Infrastructure

The NSF's TechAccess: AI-Ready America initiative — funding state-level Coordination Hubs at $1 million per year — represents a pivotal reframing of AI literacy as foundational national infrastructure. Combined with the White House's National AI Policy Framework released March 21 and the Department of Labor's "Make America AI-Ready" text-message course, the federal government is now treating AI readiness as essential as roads or broadband. The whole-of-government approach, spanning NSF, DOL, USDA, and SBA, signals that AI education is no longer a niche workforce development program but a core national competitiveness strategy.

Teacher AI Certification Shifts from Optional to Strategic

The emergence of AI certification as a career differentiator for educators reflects a deeper shift in how districts think about human capacity. As EdCircuit reports, the challenge is not access to AI technology but the ability of educators to evaluate tools for instructional alignment, bias, and data privacy — a gap that is widening faster than traditional professional development can close. Districts that build credentialed cohorts of AI-literate educators will be able to move from isolated experimentation to coherent implementation, while those that don't risk falling behind as AI adoption accelerates across every subject and grade level.

Assessment Reinvention in the Age of Generative AI

The growing adoption of oral exams at U.S. colleges — alongside in-class writing, portfolio assessment, and AI annotation requirements — signals a fundamental rethinking of what assessment is for in an age when AI can complete most written assignments in seconds. Rather than treating AI as a cheating tool to be detected and punished, the most forward-thinking institutions are redesigning assessment formats that are inherently AI-resistant: formats that require students to think, explain, and defend their reasoning in real time. This shift may ultimately improve the quality of education by forcing a return to assessment formats that measure genuine understanding.

The AI School Spectrum: From Prohibition to AI-as-Teacher

The simultaneous emergence of NYC's AI prohibitions for grading and discipline and Alpha School's model of AI as the primary classroom teacher illustrates the extraordinary breadth of approaches to AI in education. On the same day that the nation's largest school district was drawing red lines around AI use, a new school was announcing that AI would replace traditional instruction entirely for core subjects. This spectrum — from cautious governance to radical reimagination — reflects the genuine uncertainty about AI's role in learning, and suggests that the next several years will be a period of intense experimentation whose outcomes will shape education for decades.
