46% of High Schoolers Now Use AI in College Search — Up from 26% Just One Year Ago

46%
  • A new EAB survey of 5,000+ high school students finds 46% now use AI tools like ChatGPT during their college search, a dramatic jump from 26% in spring 2025 — signaling AI has moved from novelty to mainstream
  • Nearly 1 in 5 students (18%) removed a college from consideration based on information surfaced through AI-generated search results — meaning AI is now shaping enrollment decisions before colleges make first contact
  • More than half of students said they would react negatively to college recruitment messages they perceived to be AI-generated, underscoring demand for authentic human communication
  • 43% of students say AI will influence the career they pursue; 38% believe AI will reduce the number of jobs requiring a college degree
  • 39% of students say AI is pushing them to consider alternatives to college entirely, including starting a business or entering an apprenticeship program
  • EAB VP Madeleine Rhyneer: "AI is now shaping first impressions, helping families narrow their options, and influencing real enrollment decisions — often before a college ever has direct contact with a prospective applicant"

Ellucian

Ellucian Survey: Higher Ed Shifts from AI Experimentation to Institutional Strategy — 66% of Colleges Now Leveraging AI

66%
  • Ellucian's 3rd Annual Higher Education AI Survey of 779 faculty and administrators from 300+ institutions finds AI adoption has accelerated sharply: 66% of institutions are now leveraging AI, up from 49% year over year
  • 88% of respondents expect institutional AI use to increase over the next two years; 90% of individuals report using AI themselves (up from 84% the prior year)
  • Nearly half (43%) say their institution's strategic plan now includes a focus on AI; nearly two-thirds of executive leaders say their institution already allocates budget specifically for AI
  • The share who say AI does "more good than harm" for student learning declined to 45% from 55% year over year — reflecting growing skepticism as AI becomes more embedded in academic settings
  • Data security and privacy remain the leading barrier at both personal (61%) and institutional (56%) levels; environmental impact is a rising new concern, cited by more than 1 in 5 respondents
  • Concerns about AI-related role elimination are rising: cited by 14% of respondents this year, up from 7% the prior year
  • Ellucian CEO Laura Ipsen: "The question now is how institutions scale AI responsibly and strategically — protecting data, building trust, and ensuring AI strengthens, not replaces, the people at the heart of higher education"

11K

Key Themes Emerging

The Governance Gap Widens

The Coursera report's finding that only 20% of U.S. universities have a formal AI policy — despite 95% of students and educators using AI — captures the defining tension in higher education today. As Dartmouth races to integrate AI campus-wide and Saint Leo mandates IBM AI credentials for every graduate, the gap between institutional adoption and institutional governance has never been wider. The question is no longer whether AI will be used, but who is setting the rules.

AI Is Reshaping the College Search

EAB's finding that 46% of high schoolers now use AI in their college search — up from 26% in just one year — signals a structural shift in how the next generation evaluates higher education. With 18% removing colleges from consideration based on AI-generated results and 39% considering alternatives to college altogether, AI is not just changing how students learn; it is changing whether they enroll at all. Colleges that fail to adapt their outreach and curricula risk being filtered out before they ever make contact.

Universities Move from Experimentation to Strategy

Ellucian's survey documents a decisive inflection point: 66% of institutions now leverage AI (up from 49%), and nearly half have incorporated AI into their strategic plans. But the same survey reveals a sobering countertrend — the share of leaders who believe AI does "more good than harm" for student learning dropped from 55% to 45% in a single year. As AI becomes embedded in institutional operations, optimism is giving way to a more complex, cautious reckoning with its actual effects on learning.

New Degree Programs Meet Workforce Demand

UNI's launch of two new AI undergraduate majors — one in business applications, one in mathematical foundations — reflects a broader national trend of universities redesigning curricula to meet surging employer demand. With 11,000 AI-tied entry-level jobs posted in Iowa and surrounding states in a single year, and Saint Leo making IBM AI credentials a graduation requirement across all majors, the message from industry is clear: AI fluency is no longer a specialization, it is a baseline expectation for every graduate entering the workforce.

Parental Concerns Reach a Tipping Point

A new Count on Mothers report finds that while 43% of mothers report their child uses AI for school, only 6% see AI as an unambiguous positive force. Two-thirds worry about exposure to unsafe content, more than half fear children cannot distinguish AI-generated "fake" from real, and nearly half are anxious about AI's impact on learning and child development. With 39% of mothers either unaware of how their child's data is collected or lacking the knowledge to protect it, parental oversight of AI in K-12 education remains critically underdeveloped.

Teacher Training Remains the Weakest Link

Across multiple surveys released this week, one finding is consistent: teacher training is the most urgent unmet need in AI education. The independent EssayShark study found 71% of educators who use AI received no formal institutional training. Ellucian found 83% of Financial Aid staff need AI training. The Coursera report found only 27% of educators feel confident identifying AI-generated content. As AI reshapes every corner of campus life, the educators responsible for guiding students through it are, in the majority, navigating without a map.
