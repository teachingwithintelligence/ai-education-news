eSchool News — RAND Corporation American Youth Panel Survey

RAND Study: Student AI Homework Use Surges from 48% to 62% — While 67% Fear It Is Eroding Their Critical Thinking

62%
  • A nationally representative RAND Corporation survey of over 1,000 students ages 12–29 found that AI homework use rose sharply from 48% in May 2025 to 62% by December 2025 — driven largely by middle and high schoolers, while college student use remained relatively steady
  • Despite the surge in usage, 67% of students said using AI for schoolwork harms critical thinking skills — up from 54% earlier in the year — with concern even higher among non-users (78%) than among active AI users (60%)
  • ChatGPT remains the most widely used tool at 53%, while Google Gemini more than doubled its student user base between May and December 2025, reaching 28% — signaling a rapidly shifting AI tool landscape in K-12 and higher education
  • Students most often use AI to get better explanations of assignments (38%), brainstorm ideas (35%), look up facts (33%), and draft or revise writing (33%) — with most students not considering these uses to be cheating, except for getting direct homework answers (45% call that cheating)
  • Only one-third of students reported their school had a clear, schoolwide AI policy — the rest said rules varied by teacher or were absent entirely, leaving students to navigate AI ethics largely on their own
  • RAND researcher Heather Schwartz: "Students are clearly embracing AI as a learning tool, but they're also conflicted about what it means for their own learning. The question is whether schools can help them use AI in ways that deepen, rather than erode, their critical thinking."

Chalkbeat New York — NYC Department of Education

NYC Releases Preliminary AI School Guidelines — "Traffic Light" Framework Governs 800,000 K-12 Students

Traffic Light
  • New York City's Education Department unveiled preliminary AI guidelines for its 800,000 K-12 students, structured around a "traffic light" framework: green for approved uses like lesson plan brainstorming, yellow for supervised uses like translating for bilingual learners, and red for strictly prohibited uses like AI-generated grading or discipline decisions
  • The guidance — released nearly three years after NYC's short-lived ChatGPT ban — requires all AI tools to pass a data privacy and security vetting process before classroom use, and prohibits student personal information from being used to train AI models or generate revenue
  • Schools Chancellor Kamar Samuels: "AI is here, and our responsibility is to put strong systemwide safeguards in place" — framing the guidance as a protective infrastructure rather than a technology endorsement
  • The AI Task Force that wrote the guidance included advisory input from Google, OpenAI, and other companies — drawing pushback from a parent coalition that has gathered 1,500 signatures calling for a two-year moratorium on AI use in schools
  • Public feedback will be collected through May 8, with a comprehensive final playbook expected in June 2026 — leaving key questions about student homework use and personal AI accounts still unresolved in the preliminary version

Education Week — EdWeek Research Center / National Academy for AI Instruction

Teachers Double AI Tool Use in One Year — $23M AFT Partnership with Anthropic, Microsoft & OpenAI to Train 400,000 Educators

2× Growth
  • The share of teachers using AI-run tools nearly doubled from 2024 to 2025, according to a national EdWeek Research Center survey — with 6 in 10 teachers now reporting AI use in their practice, though most still rely on it for basic lesson plans and administrative tasks rather than instructional improvement
  • The National Academy for AI Instruction — a five-year, $23 million partnership between the American Federation of Teachers and Anthropic, Microsoft, and OpenAI — is actively training teachers in New York City and other cities to move beyond surface-level AI use toward deeper pedagogical integration
  • As AI models advance, teachers increasingly need training not just on how to use AI tools, but on how to leverage their own professional judgment and expertise when working alongside AI — a shift from tool literacy to pedagogical wisdom
  • The partnership aims to train 400,000 teachers nationwide, representing a significant portion of the U.S. teaching workforce — and reflecting a recognition by major AI companies that educator preparation is essential to responsible classroom AI adoption
  • Experts warn that without deeper training, the current pattern of surface-level AI use risks reinforcing existing inequities: teachers in well-resourced schools are more likely to receive professional development, widening the gap in AI-enhanced instruction quality

Center on Reinventing Public Education — State AI Early Adopter Database

CRPE: Only 13 States Offering AI Guidance for Schools — But Early Adopters Show Bipartisan "Cautious Exploration" Pattern

13 States
  • A new CRPE report finds that AI is already disrupting education, but only 13 states are currently offering guidance for schools — leaving the majority of U.S. districts without official direction as AI adoption accelerates in classrooms
  • Among the 20 early adopter states documented in CRPE's State AI Early Adopter Database, a consistent pattern of "cautious exploration over sweeping mandates" has emerged — with states issuing non-binding guidance, investing in professional learning, and piloting tools before scaling
  • Critically, politics is not the main divider: early AI strategies look similar across red, blue, and divided states — suggesting AI education policy is emerging as a bipartisan infrastructure issue rather than an ideological battleground
  • Nearly all early adopter states are prioritizing AI literacy and educator capacity-building as their first investment — while about half are testing AI tools through pilots, reflecting a "learn before you leap" approach to technology adoption
  • CRPE identifies growing pressure on states to clarify expectations, strengthen procurement and data privacy guardrails, and address infrastructure gaps — particularly as AI adoption accelerates faster than governance frameworks can keep pace

U.S. Department of Labor — Make America AI-Ready Initiative

DOL Launches "Make America AI-Ready" — Free AI Literacy Course Delivered by Text Message, No Internet Required

Text "READY"
  • The U.S. Department of Labor launched "Make America AI-Ready," a free AI literacy course accessible to any American by texting "READY" to 20202 — designed to be maximally inclusive, including for those without laptops or reliable internet access
  • The course delivers bite-sized learning content and daily challenges entirely over text message, completable in seven days at just 10 minutes per day — developed through a public-private partnership between the Labor Department and education technology company Arist
  • Content aligns with DOL's AI Literacy Framework across five areas: understanding AI principles, exploring AI uses, directing AI effectively, evaluating AI outputs, and using AI responsibly — providing a structured foundation for workforce readiness
  • Secretary of Labor Lori Chavez-DeRemer: "The 'Make America AI-Ready' initiative is designed to ensure every American worker has the chance to learn foundational skills so they can benefit from the opportunities that the AI economy presents"
  • The initiative is part of the White House's Pledge to America's Youth and advances the Trump Administration's America's Talent Strategy and AI Action Plan — framing AI literacy as an economic equity issue essential to national competitiveness

Key Themes Emerging

The Critical Thinking Paradox

The RAND Corporation's finding that 67% of students fear AI is harming their critical thinking — even as 62% now use it for homework — captures a profound paradox at the heart of AI in education. Students are simultaneously the most worried and the most dependent users of AI tools, caught between cognitive instincts and frictionless convenience. The challenge for K-12 institutions is to design learning environments where AI serves as a scaffold for deeper engagement rather than a shortcut around it. RAND researcher Heather Schwartz frames the question as whether schools can help students use AI in ways that deepen, rather than erode, critical thinking — a distinction that requires explicit policy, not just prohibition.

City-Level AI Policy Comes of Age

New York City's "traffic light" AI framework — released nearly three years after its short-lived ChatGPT ban — represents a new maturity in municipal AI governance. By distinguishing between green-light uses (lesson plan brainstorming), yellow-light uses requiring professional oversight (student data analysis, translation for bilingual learners), and red-light prohibitions (AI-generated grading or discipline decisions), NYC is providing the kind of granular, operational guidance that schools have been waiting for. The framework's requirement that all AI tools pass data privacy vetting before classroom use, and its prohibition on using student data to train AI models, sets a standard that other large urban districts are likely to follow.

Universities Race to Build AI Degree Infrastructure

RIT's new Bachelor of Science in AI — joining UW-Whitewater, Eastern University's online AI Business Strategy master's, and a wave of other new programs — signals that universities are moving from AI electives and minors to full degree programs designed from the ground up for the AI economy. RIT's approach is notable for its integration of co-op work experience, ethics coursework, and an AI minor open to all majors — reflecting an understanding that AI literacy must permeate the entire campus, not just the computer science department. The simultaneous launch of an AI minor alongside the BS degree suggests a two-track strategy: deep expertise for AI specialists and foundational literacy for every graduate.

Teacher AI Adoption Doubles — But Depth Lags

The near-doubling of teacher AI tool use from 2024 to 2025 is a striking indicator of how rapidly AI has entered the classroom — but the EdWeek Research Center's finding that most teachers still use AI for basic lesson plans and administrative tasks, rather than instructional improvement, reveals a critical gap between adoption and transformation. The $23 million AFT–Anthropic–Microsoft–OpenAI partnership to train 400,000 teachers is the most ambitious attempt yet to close that gap, moving educators from surface-level tool use to pedagogically sophisticated AI integration. The success of this initiative may determine whether AI becomes a genuine force for instructional improvement or remains a productivity tool that leaves the core of teaching unchanged.

AI Equity Research Enters the Academy

Penn State's AI Justice Fellows initiative — funding doctoral research on AI's effects on multilingual learners, graduate writing, education policy, and counselor education — represents the institutionalization of AI equity research as a serious scholarly field. By centering questions of fairness, accountability, and responsibility in AI education research, Penn State is helping to build the evidence base that policymakers and practitioners will need as AI adoption accelerates. The program's focus on how AI tools affect marginalized students — including multilingual learners and international graduate students — addresses a gap that mainstream AI education research has largely overlooked.

Federal AI Literacy as Workforce Infrastructure

The Department of Labor's "Make America AI-Ready" initiative — delivering AI literacy via text message to ensure access for workers without laptops or reliable internet — represents a pivotal reframing of AI education as an economic equity imperative. By partnering with Arist to deliver a seven-day, 10-minutes-per-day course through the most universally accessible technology available, the DOL is acknowledging that the digital divide and the AI literacy gap are the same problem. Combined with the NSF's $11 million teacher training grant and the White House's AI Action Plan, this signals that the federal government is now treating AI literacy as foundational national infrastructure — as essential as roads or broadband — rather than a niche workforce development program.
