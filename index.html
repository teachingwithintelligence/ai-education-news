Stanford University / Government Technology — SCALE Report

Stanford Researchers Find Thin Evidence Behind AI Classroom Tools — Gains Fade When Technology Is Removed

~20
  • Stanford's AI Hub for Education reviewed more than 800 studies on AI in education but found only approximately 20 high-quality causal studies that can determine whether AI tools directly drive changes in student outcomes — revealing a stark gap between rapid adoption and rigorous evidence
  • Students consistently perform better while using AI tools, but those gains often weaken or disappear once the technology is removed — a phenomenon researchers call "cognitive offloading," where students rely on AI to do thinking for them rather than developing their own understanding
  • Senior researcher Lily Fesler: "If students are really relying on AI tools to just do the assignment for them, they're thinking a little less, engaging less, during the task. Their performance can improve … but they're not necessarily internalizing and learning from that experience"
  • AI tools can reduce teacher time on grading, lesson planning, and feedback by as much as 30% without lowering lesson quality — but researchers note that time saved does not mean teachers are working less; they reinvest it in other student-support activities
  • Co-author Chris Agnew, managing director of the AI Hub for Education: "The headline is mixed, and it requires real intention and much further research. I think the headline is, it's still too early to know if this is doing school faster … or if it's reimagining"
  • Researchers caution district leaders against replacing core elements of teaching with technology: "AI, being a tool that has promise, does not mean it's better for kids to spend more time just in front of computers" — relationships and in-person learning remain central to effective education

Jobs for the Future (JFF) — National Learner Survey

JFF Survey: 69% of Learners Now Receive AI Training — But Students Still Learn About AI More From Social Media Than Schools

69%
  • A new national survey released today by Jobs for the Future (JFF) finds nearly 7 in 10 learners — defined as people over 16 currently enrolled in education or training — report AI is incorporated into their coursework, up from 57% in 2024
  • The number of learners receiving formal AI training from their institution has risen more than 20 percentage points since last year to 69% — yet learners are still more likely to seek AI information from social media (48%), news articles (38%), and friends or family (30%) than from their school (23%)
  • AI policies across education providers remain uneven: 31% of learners report their institution fully permits AI use, while 11% say it bans AI entirely and 13% say they do not know their school's AI policy — creating widespread confusion
  • 40% of learners report spending more time collaborating with peers as a result of AI, while 41% report spending less — reflecting AI's mixed and polarizing impact on the social fabric of learning environments
  • Learners most commonly use AI to complete assignments more efficiently (44%), enhance learning and understanding (38%), and access AI-driven tutoring (35%) — but 11% say AI has not significantly supported their education at all
  • Ben Pring, JFF Vice President: "Increased use does not automatically translate into increased efficacy. If schools want to realize AI's potential, they must build trust, provide clear guidance, and ensure that AI augments relationships and classroom experience"

eSchool News — AI Pedagogy Research

New Research: AI Tools That Guide Student Reasoning Outperform Answer-Generating Tools — Pedagogy Matters More Than Content

⚙️
  • New analysis published today in eSchool News finds that the AI tools most likely to shift student outcomes are those built on pedagogical foundations — not simply those with the most advanced AI models or the broadest content libraries
  • Tools that guide students through reasoning processes — asking follow-up questions, prompting reflection, and scaffolding problem-solving — consistently produce better learning outcomes than tools that generate complete answers outright

  • Special education teachers are among the most active adopters of AI pedagogical tools: 57% reported in 2025 using AI to help develop individualized education programs (IEPs) for students with learning disabilities

Key Themes Emerging

Evidence Gap Threatens AI Adoption Credibility

Stanford's SCALE report — finding only ~20 rigorous causal studies among 800+ reviewed — exposes a fundamental problem: schools are adopting AI tools at scale without adequate evidence that those tools improve lasting learning. The "cognitive offloading" phenomenon, where students perform better with AI but worse without it, raises the uncomfortable question of whether AI is teaching students or substituting for learning. District leaders are now being urged to demand causal evidence from vendors, not just performance metrics, before committing to large-scale deployments.

Teacher AI Adoption Outpaces Institutional Support

With 85% of K-12 teachers now using AI — nearly double the rate of two years ago — and only 35% of districts having provided students any formal AI training, the gap between adoption and preparation has become a structural crisis. The JFF survey released today reinforces this: learners are more likely to learn about AI from social media than from their schools. The convergence of congressional testimony demanding federal guardrails, Google's 6-million-teacher training initiative, and the $23 million National Academy for AI Instruction signals that teacher support has finally become a national policy priority.

Gen Z Job Market Crisis Deepens as AI Agents Scale

ServiceNow CEO Bill McDermott's warning that Gen Z graduate unemployment could reach the mid-30s within two years aligns with data showing 58% of 2024–2025 graduates still searching for their first job, a 32% drop in U.S. job postings since ChatGPT's launch, and 92,000 jobs shed in February 2026. The convergence of AI agent deployment, reduced entry-level hiring, and a generation entering the workforce with primarily "codifiable knowledge" creates a structural challenge that universities must address through curriculum redesign — not just career services.

AI Degree Programs Proliferate at Elite Institutions

The simultaneous launch of AI programs at Dartmouth, Rose-Hulman, University of New Haven, and Boston University — alongside Northwestern's AI major announced last week — reflects a higher education sector racing to build academic infrastructure for AI at scale. Dartmouth's framing is particularly significant: as the birthplace of AI, its emphasis on ensuring tools "amplify human judgment rather than replace it" sets a philosophical tone for responsible AI education. Universities are no longer just training AI users — they are training AI architects, ethicists, and governance professionals.

K-12 Policy Matures from Prohibition to Structured Integration

The wave of formal AI policies adopted by school boards — from Sonoma Valley, California to Utah's statewide legislation embedding AI in computer science standards — reflects a national maturation of K-12 AI governance. Districts are moving away from blanket bans and ad hoc teacher decisions toward structured frameworks covering acceptable use, academic integrity, data privacy, and grade-level distinctions. The parent survey finding that 80% want more guardrails, combined with only 45% of principals having any AI policy, creates both the political mandate and practical urgency for districts to act before the 2026–2027 school year.

Pedagogy, Not Technology, Determines AI Learning Outcomes

Today's research consensus — from Stanford's SCALE report to eSchool News analysis — converges on a single insight: the design of AI tools matters far more than their underlying technology. Tools that guide students through reasoning, prompt reflection, and scaffold problem-solving produce lasting learning gains; tools that generate answers outright improve task performance without improving understanding. This distinction has profound implications for how districts evaluate and procure AI tools, how teachers are trained to deploy them, and how students are taught to use them as cognitive partners rather than cognitive replacements.
