Pearson Launches "Foundations of AI for PK-12 Educators" — 250,000 Free Vouchers for U.S. High School Teachers

250K
  • Pearson, the world's lifelong learning company, launched a new self-paced online course — "Foundations of AI for PK-12 Educators" — to help pre-K through grade 12 teachers use AI confidently and responsibly in the classroom
  • The 6.5-hour course covers AI fundamentals, effective prompt design, ethics and safety (including bias, privacy, and misuse), and hands-on classroom use cases with ChatGPT and Gemini — earning participants a verifiable Credly digital badge

  • Dr. E. Wyatt Gordon, Pearson SVP: "To make AI a useful asset in the classroom, we have to focus on the person leading it — the teacher. This is about building their confidence with practical skills to use AI responsibly and effectively"

Instructure / InnovateEDU — SXSW EDU 2026

Instructure's 2026 Evidence Report: Only 2% of Consumer Classroom Tech Has Verified Proof of Impact on Student Learning

2%
  • Instructure (maker of Canvas LMS) released its 2026 Evidence Report at SXSW EDU, analyzing 150 of the most widely used classroom technologies against federally recognized research standards — finding stark gaps between purpose-built edtech and consumer tools
  • Only 2% of consumer technologies used in classrooms have identifiable evidence aligned to the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA), compared to 40% of purpose-built edtech tools — a 20x difference that has major implications for AI tool procurement
  • Privacy protections remain inconsistent: purpose-built edtech products are more likely to hold recognized data privacy certifications, but many classroom technologies — including AI tools — still lack verified safeguards for student data
  • Only 33% of the most-accessed classroom technologies have achieved a recognized interoperability certification (OneRoster, LTI, or Project Unicorn), limiting seamless integration into school ecosystems
  • 70% of the 100 most-accessed classroom technologies include at least one accessibility indicator, yet only 15% have achieved a recognized usability certification — a gap that disproportionately affects students with disabilities
  • Melissa Loble, Instructure Chief Academic Officer: "The conversation is shifting from what a tool can do to whether it measurably improves learning outcomes. Evidence aligned to ESSA, strong student privacy protections and seamless interoperability are no longer differentiators — they are baseline requirements"

Government Technology / NJ.com

New Jersey to Use AI to Score Standardized Writing Tests for 1.3 Million Students — Raising Accuracy and Equity Concerns

$58.7M
  • New Jersey will use AI to score most student writing on its new NJSLA-Adaptive standardized tests, debuting this spring for grades 3–10 — part of a $58.7 million, two-year contract with Cambium Assessment
  • The AI system will grade essays and short answers on the English Language Arts section; only responses flagged as "unusual" or "borderline" will be routed to human scorers — with Cambium assuming 25% of responses will require handscoring
  • In Texas, AI grading controversies offer a cautionary tale: Dallas ISD sent thousands of AI-scored tests for human rescoring, and nearly 40% came back with higher scores — with 85% of third-grade AI scores improved when humans reviewed them
  • In Massachusetts, AI grading errors were blamed for 1,400 incorrect MCAS scores last year — raising the stakes for New Jersey's rollout affecting nearly all of the state's 1.3 million public school students
  • Steve Beatty, NJEA president: "If a student fails the AI-scored sections of the exams, there should be a plan to have the writing reassessed by a human. They should go back to a person to be verified"
  • The New Jersey case is part of a broader national trend: AI is being embedded into high-stakes assessment infrastructure at scale, often ahead of the evidence base needed to validate its accuracy and fairness across student populations

Key Themes Emerging

Faculty Resistance Is Becoming Organized and Vocal

The Guardian's reporting today captures a shift in the faculty response to AI: from individual frustration to coordinated resistance. The American Association of University Professors has published formal warnings about uncritical AI adoption; faculty unions are incorporating AI protections into contracts; and professors are developing creative pedagogical workarounds — oral exams, analog journals, in-class demonstrations — to preserve the learning outcomes that AI threatens to short-circuit. This organized resistance is a new force in the AI-in-education debate, and it will shape how universities implement AI policies in 2026 and beyond.

Teacher Training Is Finally Getting Serious Institutional Backing

Pearson's launch of 250,000 free AI training vouchers for U.S. high school teachers — backed by the White House's Pledge to America's Youth — represents a meaningful escalation in the scale and seriousness of teacher AI professional development. For too long, AI training for educators has been ad hoc, underfunded, and disconnected from classroom realities. The Pearson course's emphasis on practical, classroom-focused guidance — including when not to use AI — reflects a more mature understanding of what teachers actually need. The question now is whether this scale of investment can close the gap between student AI adoption and teacher AI readiness.

Evidence Standards for AI Tools Are Becoming Non-Negotiable

Instructure's 2026 Evidence Report — released at SXSW EDU — is a landmark document for the edtech procurement conversation. The finding that only 2% of consumer classroom technologies have ESSA-aligned evidence of impact, compared to 40% of purpose-built edtech, gives district leaders a clear framework for evaluating AI tools: evidence, privacy, and interoperability are baseline requirements, not differentiators. As federal relief dollars expire and state budgets tighten, the pressure to justify technology spending with outcome data will only intensify — and AI tools that cannot demonstrate measurable impact on student learning will face increasing scrutiny.

AI Scoring of High-Stakes Tests Is Expanding — and Contested

New Jersey's decision to use AI to score writing for 1.3 million students on its new adaptive standardized tests is a significant expansion of AI's role in high-stakes assessment. The cautionary data from Texas — where nearly 40% of AI-scored tests came back with higher scores when reviewed by humans, and 85% of third-grade AI scores were improved by human review — underscores the accuracy risks of deploying AI in consequential evaluation contexts. As more states adopt AI scoring for efficiency and cost reasons, the equity implications for students whose writing may be systematically underscored by AI systems trained on non-representative data deserve urgent attention.

AI Majors Are Expanding Beyond Engineering Into Business and Humanities

The University of Northern Iowa's addition of AI majors in both the Wilson College of Business and the College of Humanities, Arts and Sciences — announced the same week as Northwestern's new engineering AI major — signals that AI education is moving beyond its technical origins. The recognition that business leaders, writers, educators, and social scientists need structured AI training as much as engineers reflects a more sophisticated understanding of how AI will reshape work across every sector. Universities that offer AI training only within engineering schools risk producing graduates who lack the cross-disciplinary judgment needed to deploy AI responsibly in complex organizational and social contexts.

AI Job Displacement Is Being Systematically Undercounted

Fortune's reporting on the 75% unemployment insurance non-application rate — combined with Harvard Business School's estimate of 18 million at-risk entry-level jobs — reveals a structural blind spot in how AI's labor market impact is being measured and communicated. If the first wave of AI displacement shows up in hiring freezes and rising entry-level requirements rather than mass layoffs, official unemployment statistics will understate the true scale of disruption. For educators preparing students for this labor market, the implication is clear: AI literacy is not just a competitive advantage — it is increasingly a prerequisite for labor market entry in a growing number of fields.
