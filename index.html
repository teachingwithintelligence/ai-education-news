K-12 Dive — RAND Corporation American Youth Panel Survey

RAND Study: Middle & High Schoolers Rapidly Increasing AI Homework Use — While Fearing Its Impact on Critical Thinking

63%
  • A new RAND Corporation report released today finds the share of high schoolers using AI for homework jumped from 49% to 63% between May and December 2025 — while middle schoolers rose from 30% to 46% in the same period
  • Despite surging use, a growing majority of students now believe AI use for schoolwork harms their critical thinking skills — up more than 10 percentage points from ten months earlier, signaling deepening self-awareness about AI dependency
  • Only about 1 in 3 middle and high school students reported their school had a schoolwide rule about AI use for homework — and just 9% of middle schoolers and 7% of high schoolers said their schools permit AI for homework at all
  • Stanford University's SCALE Initiative analyzed over 1,100 research papers on AI in K-12 education and found only 20 actually evaluated real impact — highlighting a critical evidence gap as schools rush to adopt AI tools
  • AI tools with guardrails — such as tutoring programs offering hints or guided reasoning — show more promising results than generative AI chatbots that simply give students direct answers
  • RAND recommends schools explicitly inform students when AI can and cannot be used, and facilitate conversations about how AI tools will impact their development and critical thinking skills

Inside Higher Ed — Jobs for the Future (JFF) National Survey

JFF Survey: 70% of College Students Now Use AI Weekly — But Most Learn About It From Social Media, Not Schools

70%
  • A new national survey by Jobs for the Future finds 70% of learners use AI on a daily or weekly basis for their education — up from 59% in 2024 — and 69% say AI tools are incorporated into their lessons, up from 57% in 2024
  • Despite increased institutional training, students still prefer informal channels: 48% used social media, 38% used news articles, and 30% asked friends and family — while only 23% sought guidance from their school or training program
  • The most common education use for AI in 2025 was completing assignments more efficiently (44%), followed by enhancing learning and understanding (38%), and accessing AI-driven tutoring (35%)
  • AI policies remain deeply uneven: 31% of learners report their institution fully permits AI use, 11% say it bans AI entirely, and 13% do not know their school's AI policy at all
  • Formal AI training from institutions rose more than 20 percentage points in one year to 69%, but only 35% of learners rated those trainings as highly effective
  • JFF Vice President Ben Pring: "Increased use does not automatically translate into increased efficacy. Schools must build trust, provide clear guidance, and ensure AI augments relationships and classroom experience"

Times of India — Stanford ADP Payroll Study

Stanford Study: Young Workers Ages 22–25 See 6% Employment Decline in AI-Exposed Jobs as Entry-Level Roles Shrink

5.7%
  • A Stanford study using ADP payroll records tracking millions of workers found that in the most AI-exposed occupations, workers ages 22–25 saw a 6% decline in employment from late 2022 to September 2025 — while older workers in the same roles recorded 6–9% growth
  • Federal Reserve Bank of New York data shows unemployment among recent college graduates rising from 4.0% in Q4 2022 to 5.7% in Q4 2025 — a troubling trend concentrated in AI-exposed fields
  • The entry-level squeeze is structural: new graduates typically handle the "first draft, basic analysis, routine coding, and research clean-up" — exactly the tasks AI is now automating most efficiently
  • Where AI augments human work rather than replaces it, early-career employment holds up better — close to 70% of occupations in the lowest AI-exposure group recorded rising early-career employment versus less than half in the highest-exposure group
  • The real damage is often invisible: not mass layoffs, but smaller trainee batches, paused junior hiring, and decisions to "stretch the existing team a little further" — quietly eliminating the entry point for new graduates
  • Experts warn that as beginner roles shrink, companies risk eating their own future talent pipeline — creating a skills shortage in 3–5 years when experienced workers retire without trained successors

Yahoo News — Brookings Institution & RAND K-12 AI Report

85% of K-12 Teachers Now Use AI — But Brookings Warns Risks Overshadow Benefits as Policies Lag Behind Adoption

85%
  • An estimated 85% of K-12 public school teachers used AI during the 2024–2025 school year — primarily for curriculum and content development — while 86% of K-12 students have used AI in general
  • The Brookings Institution's 2026 AI and K-12 education report warns that the risks of generative AI in education overshadow its benefits, citing weakened student-teacher relationships and student safety concerns
  • Only 35% of school district leaders provided students with any AI training in 2025, and just 45% of principals reported having school or district policies on AI use — revealing a massive governance gap
  • 71% of K-12 teachers report that when students use AI for schoolwork, it is hard to determine whether the work is genuinely their own — a major academic integrity challenge
  • 57% of special education teachers use AI to help develop individualized education programs (IEPs) for students with learning disabilities — one of the clearest documented benefits of AI in K-12 settings
  • Stanford research found that when students use AI and are then told they can no longer use it, they actually perform worse than students who never used AI — raising serious concerns about long-term learning dependency

Epsom College — AI in Education Certified Framework

Epsom College Becomes First UK Independent School to Achieve AI in Education Certification — Setting a New Standard for Responsible AI Use

🏆
  • Epsom College has become the first independent school in the UK to achieve Explorer status within the AI in Education Certified Framework — recognition that the school is among national leaders in embedding AI responsibly into teaching and learning
  • The certification, awarded by AI in Education (the national body ensuring AI develops in the interests of young people), recognizes schools that engage critically with the technology and embed its use in ways that are safe, ethical, and educationally meaningful
  • At Epsom, AI is being used to enhance lesson design, enable more personalized feedback, and help pupils engage more deeply with complex ideas — not as a replacement for excellent teaching, but as a means of strengthening it
  • Head Mark Lascelles: "We may not be able to predict exactly how AI will have shaped the world by the time our pupils leave us, but we can ensure they engage with it thoughtfully, think critically about its implications, and develop the confidence to harness its potential"
  • The certification provides parents with concrete assurance that their children's use of AI is guided, purposeful, and grounded in strong educational values — addressing growing parental concerns about unchecked AI use in schools
  • The AI in Education framework was founded in 2023 and is rapidly expanding as schools seek formal recognition for responsible AI integration practices

Key Themes Emerging

Student AI Adoption Outpaces School Policy

Today's RAND data reveals a stark reality: high schoolers using AI for homework jumped from 49% to 63% in just seven months, yet only 1 in 3 students say their school has any AI policy at all. Students are not waiting for institutional guidance — they are already deeply embedded in AI-assisted learning. The critical challenge for schools is not whether to allow AI, but how to structure its use so that it deepens rather than erodes the cognitive skills students need for lifelong learning. RAND's recommendation to make AI policies explicit and involve students in shaping them offers a practical starting point.

Critical Thinking Paradox: Students Use AI More While Trusting It Less

A striking paradox emerges from today's research: students are using AI more than ever while simultaneously growing more skeptical of its effects on their own thinking. The RAND finding that a growing majority of students believe AI harms their critical thinking — even as they continue using it — suggests that awareness alone is insufficient. Structural interventions such as AI-free assessment periods, guided reasoning tools, and flipped classroom models are needed to preserve cognitive development. The Brookings Institution's warning that risks overshadow benefits adds urgency to designing AI integration that actively builds, rather than replaces, human reasoning capacity.

Entry-Level Job Market Squeeze for New Graduates

The Stanford payroll study's finding of a 6% employment decline for workers ages 22–25 in AI-exposed occupations — while older workers in the same roles grew 6–9% — reveals a structural shift in how companies view junior talent. The entry-level role has historically served not just as a productivity input but as a training ground where competence is built. As AI makes it easier to automate the beginner layer of white-collar work, companies face a temptation to trim junior hiring that could create a serious talent pipeline crisis within a decade. Universities must redesign curricula to emphasize the judgment, creativity, and contextual reasoning that AI cannot replicate.

AI Literacy Certification and Standards Emerge

Epsom College's achievement of the UK's first AI in Education certification marks the beginning of a formal credentialing ecosystem for responsible AI use in schools. As more institutions seek to demonstrate that their AI integration is safe, ethical, and educationally sound, certification frameworks will become increasingly important signals of quality for parents, students, and regulators. The parallel development of the NSF's NAIRR AI Education Fellows program in the US — selecting 25 educators from 23 states — suggests that both formal certification and peer-to-peer knowledge networks are emerging as complementary mechanisms for raising the floor of AI education quality nationwide.

Workforce Readiness Requires AI Fluency, Not Just Awareness

UA Little Rock's framing — "move from fear to fluency" — captures the shift in how universities are approaching AI workforce preparation. The warning that 60% of jobs will face task changes within four years, combined with the finding that half the current workforce has had zero AI training in the past year, creates an urgent mandate for higher education to produce graduates who can not only use AI tools but integrate them into real workflows and decision-making processes. The distinction between passive AI use and active AI fluency — knowing how to prompt, evaluate, and build with AI — is becoming the defining competency gap in today's labor market.

Guidance Gap: Institutions Must Become Trusted AI Educators

The JFF survey's finding that students still prefer social media over their schools for AI guidance is a wake-up call for educational institutions. When 48% of learners turn to social media and only 23% turn to their school for AI information, it signals a credibility and relevance gap that institutions must urgently address. Schools that fail to become authoritative, trusted sources of AI knowledge risk ceding that role to commercially motivated platforms with no obligation to prioritize student wellbeing or learning outcomes. Building institutional credibility on AI — through clear policies, effective training, and honest communication about both benefits and risks — is now a core function of educational leadership.
