National Science Foundation — AI Professional Development for K-12

NSF Invests $11M to Train Thousands of K-12 Teachers in AI and Computer Science Nationwide

$11M
  • The U.S. National Science Foundation announced an $11 million award to the Computer Science Teachers Association (CSTA) to launch "AI Professional Development Weeks: CS Foundations for Creating with AI" — a multistate initiative to prepare thousands of K-12 educators to teach foundational AI at scale
  • The program will operate over two years in Indiana, South Carolina, Minnesota, New Jersey, Iowa, Illinois, and at least three additional states — directly supporting approximately 2,500–3,000 teachers and reaching an estimated 500,000–600,000 students
  • NSF Acting Director Brian Stone: "American students must be prepared not just to use AI, but to understand it and create with it. This investment will equip thousands of educators with the tools needed to bring AI and computer science into the classroom"
  • CSTA Executive Director Jake Baskin: "AI is arriving in classrooms faster than AI literacy — and that gap is growing. CS teachers are uniquely positioned to move students beyond passive AI use toward deeper understanding, critical thinking, and meaningful creation"
  • The initiative stems directly from the executive order on "Advancing Artificial Intelligence Education for American Youth" and represents a coordinated federal push to close the gap between AI adoption and AI understanding in K-12 schools
  • Teachers will deepen knowledge of core CS concepts — data, algorithms, abstraction, and systems — and increase confidence in designing age-appropriate lessons where students use, build, and critique AI systems

RAND Corporation — American Youth Panel Survey

RAND Report: 62% of Students Now Use AI for Homework — and 67% Fear It Harms Their Critical Thinking

62%
  • A new RAND Corporation report based on a nationally representative survey of 1,214 youth (ages 12–29) finds the share of students using AI for homework rose from 48% in May 2025 to 62% in December 2025 — with middle and high schoolers driving the sharpest increase
  • 67% of students now endorse the statement that "the more students use AI for schoolwork, the more it will harm their critical thinking skills" — up more than 10 percentage points from ten months earlier, signaling growing self-awareness about AI dependency
  • Most students do not consider AI use cheating — except when using AI to get direct answers for homework; other uses such as brainstorming, editing, and research assistance are widely viewed as acceptable
  • Students in higher grade levels are more likely to believe their teachers are checking homework for AI use and worry about being falsely accused of cheating — creating anxiety that complicates productive AI integration
  • RAND recommends schools adopt the "flipped classroom" model — where students first engage with content at home and then practice independently during AI-free class time — to preserve cognitive development while allowing AI-assisted learning
  • Clarity and schoolwide consistency in AI policies is essential: explicit rules about when AI can and cannot be used could reduce student anxiety and dampen misuse, even if enforcement remains imperfect

Fortune — BlackRock CEO Larry Fink at 2026 Infrastructure Summit

BlackRock CEO Warns AI Is Creating a Job Market "Crisis" for Class of 2026 — Highest Graduate Unemployment in Years

5.6%
  • BlackRock CEO Larry Fink warned at the 2026 Infrastructure Summit that "when this year's college graduates enter the workforce, we could see the highest unemployment rate among them in years — even without a recession," citing AI's rapid displacement of entry-level white-collar roles
  • Unemployment among recent college graduates ages 22–27 now sits at 5.6% — near levels not seen since 2013 excluding the pandemic — while job postings on Handshake fell more than 16% between August 2024 and August 2025 and average applications per role jumped 26%
  • Fink: "The speed at which AI is changing, we're not adapting our society fast enough. Really post World War II, the pathway to a white-collar job was a college education, and AI is going to disrupt many of those types of jobs"
  • More than half of employers view the job market for the class of 2026 as "poor" or "fair" — the most pessimistic reading from the National Association of Colleges and Employers since the start of the pandemic
  • BlackRock committed $100 million to skilled-trade programs to help address the gap, aiming to reach 50,000 workers over five years in roles like electricians, HVAC technicians, plumbers, and ironworkers — fields where AI is creating demand, not eliminating it
  • AMD CEO Lisa Su, set to deliver MIT's 2026 commencement address, offered a contrasting view: "The Class of 2026 will be graduating at an exciting time, as AI transforms our world and expands what is possible"

Education Week — National Academy for AI Instruction

6 in 10 Teachers Now Use AI — But Most Still Rely on Basic Tasks as $23M National Training Initiative Scales Up

60%
  • A national survey by the EdWeek Research Center finds the share of teachers using AI-run tools nearly doubled from 2024 to 2025 — with 6 in 10 teachers now using AI in their practice, though most still rely on it for basic lesson plans and administrative work rather than instructional improvement
  • The National Academy for AI Instruction — a five-year, $23 million partnership between the American Federation of Teachers and Anthropic, Microsoft, and OpenAI — is training 400,000 teachers on how to leverage AI for more sophisticated instructional uses
  • AFT President Randi Weingarten: "We're in this race for teachers to get this knowledge of more meaningful use of AI. There is a real demand from educators to learn so that they are in the driver's seat for AI as opposed to the companies or districts or the technology itself"
  • Teachers are now learning to develop "agentic" AI tools — autonomous software systems that can perform complex, multi-step tasks involving reasoning — such as stress-testing lesson plans for content gaps or generating differentiated materials for special education students
  • Data privacy remains a major concern: teachers report uncertainty about what student data AI tools collect and share, with some switching platforms after high-profile incidents — highlighting the urgent need for federal guardrails on student data protection
  • Weingarten: "The more someone uses AI, the less fearful they are of using it — but there's still a lot of fear in the absence of federal guardrails on privacy, safety, disinformation, and academic freedom"

Stanford Report — Stanford Accelerator for Learning

Stanford Experts Debunk Five Myths About AI in Education — From Teacher Replacement to Creativity and Cheating

5
  • Stanford University's Accelerator for Learning convened five events with leading researchers and educators to challenge the most persistent myths about AI in classrooms — concluding that human connection, not AI, remains the irreplaceable core of learning
  • Myth 1 — AI will replace teachers: Stanford experts say the opposite is true; in an increasingly digital world, the classroom is more vital than ever as a space for human relationships, peer communities, and the social dimensions of learning that AI cannot replicate
  • Myth 2 — AI makes cheating too easy: ETS CEO Amit Sevak reframed this as an opportunity to rethink assessment entirely — moving toward formative, scenario-based, and AI-powered personalized tests that measure synthesis, analysis, and adaptive reasoning rather than rote knowledge
  • Myth 3 — AI stifles creativity: Stanford CS chair Mehran Sahami argued that AI can lower the floor for creation and raise the ceiling of what is technically feasible — but Brazilian middle school research showed AI assistance led to worse performance on subsequent tasks when removed, underscoring the risk of dependency
  • Myth 4 — More AI tools means better learning: Stanford's Susan Athey warned that the democratization of AI tool creation has produced "too many pilots and not enough effective implementations" — grounding AI development in learning science and iterative design is essential
  • Myth 5 — AI policies can wait: Stanford researchers emphasized that the absence of clear institutional guidance is itself a policy choice — one that cedes the AI education agenda to commercial platforms with no obligation to prioritize student wellbeing or learning outcomes

Penn State Launches AI Justice Fellows Initiative — Funding Doctoral Research on Equity, Fairness, and Accountability in AI-Driven Education

9

  • Fellows are investigating topics including AI-assisted policy analysis across Pennsylvania's 499 school districts, differential AI feedback for multilingual graduate students, Socratic questioning in the age of generative AI, and the ethics of AI-mediated academic work for international students
  • Associate Dean Leah P. Hollis: "The depth and rigor of the proposals affirmed that our graduate students are approaching AI not as a novelty but as a serious site of scholarly inquiry — asking important questions about how these technologies influence fairness, accountability and responsibility in education systems"

  • Penn State's program joins a growing ecosystem of university AI ethics initiatives, including similar programs at MIT, Stanford HAI, and the University of Michigan, signaling that AI justice is becoming a recognized academic field

Key Themes Emerging

Federal Investment in AI Teacher Training

The NSF's $11 million award to the Computer Science Teachers Association marks a significant escalation in federal commitment to closing the AI literacy gap in K-12 education. By targeting 2,500–3,000 teachers across 10+ states and projecting a reach of 500,000–600,000 students, the initiative represents a coordinated national strategy rather than isolated local experiments. The program's emphasis on moving students from passive AI use to active understanding — building, critiquing, and creating with AI — reflects a maturing policy consensus that AI literacy requires foundational computer science knowledge, not just tool familiarity.

The Critical Thinking Paradox

RAND's finding that 67% of students believe AI harms their critical thinking — even as usage climbs to 62% — reveals a profound tension at the heart of AI adoption in education. Students are not unaware of the risks; they are caught between the immediate convenience of AI assistance and a growing sense that it may be eroding the very cognitive capacities they need for long-term success. Stanford's research reinforces this concern, showing that AI assistance can lead to worse performance when removed. The policy implication is clear: schools must design AI integration that builds cognitive skills, not just productivity — using tools like flipped classrooms and guided reasoning scaffolds to ensure AI augments rather than replaces student thinking.

Graduate Job Market Under Pressure

BlackRock CEO Larry Fink's warning that the class of 2026 may face the highest graduate unemployment in years — without a recession — crystallizes a structural shift that labor economists have been tracking for months. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York data showing graduate unemployment at 5.6% confirms that AI's displacement of entry-level white-collar work is no longer theoretical. The challenge for universities is not simply to teach AI skills, but to redesign curricula around the judgment, creativity, and contextual reasoning that AI cannot replicate — the very competencies that will differentiate human workers in an AI-augmented economy.

AI Majors Surge as CS Enrollment Falls

The simultaneous decline of general computer science enrollment and surge in AI-specific programs reflects a sophisticated student response to labor market signals. As LinkedIn data shows a 143% increase in AI engineer postings and median salaries approach $170,000, students are making rational bets on specialization over breadth. Northwestern's new AI major — joining programs at over a dozen institutions launching in 2026 — signals that higher education is finally catching up to industry demand. The inclusion of ethics and societal impact courses in leading programs suggests that the field is maturing beyond pure technical training toward a more holistic conception of AI expertise.

Teacher AI Adoption Outpaces Training Quality

The EdWeek finding that 6 in 10 teachers now use AI — nearly double the 2024 rate — is encouraging, but the qualifier matters: most still use AI for basic administrative tasks rather than instructional improvement. The $23 million National Academy for AI Instruction's shift toward "agentic" AI tools — systems that can perform complex, multi-step instructional tasks — represents the next frontier of teacher AI adoption. The persistent data privacy concerns teachers report, combined with the absence of federal guardrails, suggest that scaling sophisticated AI use in classrooms will require not just training but regulatory clarity that gives teachers confidence to experiment without fear of exposing student data.

AI Justice and Equity in Higher Education

Penn State's AI Justice Fellows initiative signals an important evolution in how universities are framing AI research: moving beyond "how to use AI effectively" toward "how AI affects fairness, accountability, and who gets to learn." As AI tools become embedded in grading, advising, feedback, and policy analysis, questions of algorithmic bias and differential impact on multilingual students, international students, and underrepresented groups become urgent scholarly and ethical priorities. The emergence of AI justice as a recognized field — with dedicated funding, fellowships, and publication pathways — suggests that higher education is beginning to take seriously its responsibility to ensure that AI serves equity rather than undermining it.
