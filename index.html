HEPI — Student Generative AI Survey 2026

AI Use Now "Near Universal" Among Undergraduates — 94% Use Generative AI for Assessed Work, HEPI Survey Finds

94%
  • The Higher Education Policy Institute (HEPI) published its Student Generative AI Survey 2026 today, finding that 95% of undergraduates use AI in at least one way and 94% use generative AI to help with assessed work — up dramatically from just 3% in 2024
  • The proportion of students directly including AI-generated text in assessed work has risen to 12%, up from 8% in 2025 and 3% in 2024 — a fourfold increase in two years that signals a fundamental shift in how students approach academic writing
  • 65% of students say assessment has changed significantly in response to AI, and a minority express anxiety about false accusations of academic misconduct — reflecting the stress of navigating rapidly shifting institutional policies
  • 49% of students believe AI has improved their student experience, particularly by saving time, improving understanding, and providing instant support; however, a minority feel AI has worsened their experience, citing concerns about fairness, skill erosion, and social isolation
  • 15% of students report using AI for companionship, advice, or to address loneliness — a striking finding that extends AI's role in student life well beyond academic tasks into emotional and social support
  • 68% of students believe AI skills are essential to thrive in today's world, yet fewer than half (48%) feel their teaching staff are helping them develop these skills for their future careers — with Arts and Humanities students particularly likely to feel under-supported
  • Only 36% of students feel encouraged by their institution to use AI, and only 38% say they are provided with AI guidance — a significant gap between student AI adoption and institutional readiness to support it responsibly

Lehi Free Press — Utah K-12 Policy

Utah Passes Landmark AI Classroom Law — Bans Student AI Use Without Teacher Authorization, Limits K-3 Screen Time

🏛️
  • Utah's HB273 passed the legislature on March 6, 2026 — the final day of the session — and takes effect July 1, 2026, establishing the nation's most comprehensive state-level framework for AI and technology use in public schools
  • Students cannot use generative AI to complete academic work unless a teacher specifically authorizes it for an instructional purpose — a blanket prohibition that shifts the default from permissive to restrictive for all K-12 students statewide
  • AI tools are prohibited from independently grading student work or making high-stakes academic decisions; teachers must maintain professional judgment and instructional responsibility when using AI tools in their classrooms
  • For kindergarten through grade 3, the law largely prohibits classroom screen time except for basic computer science standards or state assessments, and bans school-issued devices from going home — emphasizing hands-on learning and printed materials
  • Grades 4–6 may use technology but must balance it with traditional instruction; middle schoolers may take devices home only with written parental consent; high schoolers may take devices home unless parents opt out
  • Schools must notify parents if generative AI is used in instruction, and the Utah State Board of Education must create model AI and technology policies that all districts must align with — Utah passed nine total AI bills in the 2026 legislative session

eSchool News — K-12 Assessment Design

The AI-Resistant Classroom Is a Myth — Educators Must Redesign Assessments That Assume AI Is Already Present

🧠
  • A major analysis published today in eSchool News argues that attempts to construct "AI-proof" assignments through surveillance software or detection systems are proving unreliable, inconsistent, and counterproductive — the AI-resistant classroom is a myth
  • AI detection tools remain problematic at best: false positives undermine student trust, false negatives create complacency, and as generative models improve, detection becomes increasingly unreliable — detection is not an instructional strategy
  • The analysis recommends five shifts: (1) from product-based to process-based assessment; (2) embedding metacognition as a graded component; (3) designing for judgment, not product; (4) incorporating structured oral components; and (5) clarifying AI disclosure expectations
  • Process-based assessment re-centers evaluation on the intellectual journey: annotated drafts showing revision decisions, source selection explanations, AI use reflections, and short oral defenses of written work — making the invisible cognitive work visible
  • AI excels at generating plausible text but struggles with contextual judgment, synthesis across lived experience, and dynamic application — assessments should prioritize localized case analysis, real-time problem solving, and comparative critique of AI-generated alternatives
  • The emerging goal: not to eliminate AI from student workflows, but to ensure human thinking remains central — "If AI is present, what does rigorous learning look like now?" is the more productive question for educators

UIC Today — Teacher AI Tools Research

UIC Professor Builds "SkillTree" — AI Platform That Helps Teachers Give Personalized Feedback Without Replacing Their Judgment

🌳
  • Jeremy Riel, assistant professor at the University of Illinois Chicago College of Education, is developing SkillTree — a prototype AI-enabled platform designed to help teachers give timely, personalized feedback to students at scale
  • SkillTree organizes course skills visually (like video game skill trees), starting with basics and branching into advanced abilities; students see personalized reports showing what they already know and what they still need to work on
  • When students submit an assignment, the system uses AI to check whether the work demonstrates certain skills, then sends the teacher a simple summary report — allowing teachers to make final decisions about student learning
  • "The AI doesn't do any grading. It's just providing teachers with an extra source of information so the teacher can instead spend time working directly with students," Riel said — positioning AI as a teacher's assistant, not a replacement
  • SkillTree addresses the AI "black box" problem by connecting AI evaluations directly to the visual skill structure, enabling teachers and researchers to verify the system's reasoning and validate that student evaluation aligns with established frameworks
  • UIC College of Education Dean Kathryn Chval: "The ways Jeremy Riel is applying AI to enhance teachers' feedback have the potential to personalize learning, support more equitable outcomes for students and ensure teachers have the time they need to spend with students"

Key Themes Emerging

Student AI Adoption Has Reached a Tipping Point

The HEPI 2026 survey's finding that 94% of undergraduates use generative AI for assessed work — up from just 3% in 2024 — marks a fundamental shift in how students approach academic work. The question is no longer whether students use AI, but how well they use it and how effectively institutions support responsible use. With 12% of students now directly including AI-generated text in assessed work and only 36% feeling encouraged by their institutions to use AI, higher education faces an urgent credibility gap between student behavior and institutional policy.

State Legislatures Are Moving to Regulate AI in Schools

Utah's HB273 — which bans student AI use without teacher authorization and limits K-3 screen time — represents a new phase in AI education policy: state-level legislation rather than district-by-district guidelines. With Utah passing nine AI bills in its 2026 session, and other states watching closely, the era of voluntary school AI policies may be giving way to statutory frameworks. The challenge for legislators is writing rules durable enough to guide practice without being so rigid that they become obsolete as AI technology evolves.

Higher Education's AI Literacy Gap Is a Workforce Crisis

The Validated Insights finding that only 1% of U.S. colleges have made AI literacy an institution-wide priority — while 88% of employees expect to use AI by 2028 and 55% of recent graduates say their schools failed to train them — frames the higher education AI gap as a workforce crisis, not just a curriculum question. With AI demand growing 5X faster than the U.S. economy and a significant wage premium already emerging for AI-literate workers, institutions that fail to act are not just missing an opportunity — they are actively disadvantaging their graduates.

Assessment Design Must Evolve Beyond AI Detection

The eSchool News analysis that "the AI-resistant classroom is a myth" captures a growing consensus among education researchers: the focus must shift from policing AI use to redesigning assessments that assume AI is present and still measure meaningful learning. Process-based assessment, metacognitive reflection, oral defenses, and judgment-focused tasks are emerging as the practical tools for this redesign. The deeper implication is that AI has exposed what was already broken in assessment design — the over-reliance on easily reproducible outputs rather than genuine understanding.

AI Is Entering Student Wellbeing and Emotional Life

The revelation that 15% of university students are using AI for companionship, advice, or to address loneliness opens a new dimension in the AI education conversation that goes beyond academic integrity and learning outcomes. As AI becomes embedded in students' emotional and social lives, universities face new questions about the boundaries of AI-based support, the risks of AI dependency, and the relationship between AI use and genuine human connection. Student services, mental health professionals, and institutional leaders will need to engage with this dimension of AI adoption as urgently as they engage with academic integrity.

Teacher-Centered AI Tools Are Emerging as a Promising Model

UIC's SkillTree platform — which uses AI to help teachers give personalized feedback without replacing their judgment — represents a design philosophy that is gaining traction: AI as a teacher's assistant, not a teacher's replacement. By addressing the "black box" problem and keeping teachers in the decision-making role, SkillTree offers a model for how AI can reduce teacher workload while preserving the human relationships that are central to effective learning. The UAlbany AI Plus Symposium's focus on teacher-centered AI applications reflects a broader shift in the field toward tools that augment rather than automate the work of educators.
