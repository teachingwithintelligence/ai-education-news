National Law Review — White House AI Policy Framework

White House Unveils National AI Policy Framework — Education Section Draws Praise and Criticism Over K-12 Gaps

7 Pillars
  • On March 20, 2026, the White House released its long-awaited "National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence," organized around seven pillars emphasizing U.S. innovation dominance, workforce development, and AI-ready education — while calling on Congress to enact federal preemption of state AI regulations
  • The education section calls for AI training in existing workforce programs, federal research on task-level workforce changes, and support for land-grant institutions — but critics note it contains no mention of K-12 schools, AI literacy mandates, or protections against cognitive risks in early education
  • AI for Education advocates responded: "The 2025–2026 school year needs to be the year of AI literacy for students — we are encouraged to see workforce language, but the absence of K-12 guidance is a significant gap that states and districts will have to fill themselves"
  • The framework urges Congress to prevent states from enacting AI regulations that conflict with federal strategy, framing AI development as "an inherently interstate phenomenon with key foreign policy and national security implications" — a move that could preempt California's emerging AI education mandates
  • The framework calls for industry-led standards rather than a new federal AI agency, and recommends grants, tax incentives, and technical assistance to help small businesses deploy AI tools — signaling a deregulatory approach that contrasts sharply with the EU AI Act's rights-based framework
  • Legal analysts note that while the framework represents the Trump administration's clearest AI policy statement to date, congressional action remains uncertain — meaning states like California, New York, and Colorado will likely continue shaping AI education policy in the near term

JD Journal / Reuters — California State Bar AI Proposal

California Poised to Become First State to Require Law Schools to Teach AI — 89% of Deans Support the Move

89%
  • A proposal under review by the California State Bar's Committee of Bar Examiners would require the roughly two dozen California-accredited law schools to integrate mandatory AI training into their curricula — potentially making California the first U.S. state to formally require AI education as part of a law degree
  • Surveys of California-accredited law school leaders show 89% of deans believe students should learn about AI before entering legal practice — though fewer support a statewide mandate, preferring institutional flexibility in curriculum design
  • Proposed training would cover how AI systems operate, practical applications in legal work (research, contract drafting, document review, litigation analysis), ethical responsibilities, and the limitations of AI including inaccurate outputs, data bias, and over-reliance risks
  • The proposal follows a wave of court sanctions against attorneys who submitted AI-generated briefs containing fabricated case citations — highlighting the urgent need for lawyers to understand AI's limitations before deploying it in high-stakes legal contexts
  • The rule would apply only to California-accredited law schools, not ABA-accredited institutions — though many ABA schools have already begun voluntarily integrating AI courses, legal technology labs, and innovation programs into their curricula
  • Legal experts say California's move could set a national precedent: as the state that pioneered online legal research requirements decades ago, its adoption of AI training mandates may prompt other state bars and the ABA to follow suit

RAND Corporation — American Youth Panel Survey

RAND Study: AI Homework Use Jumps to 62% — But 67% of Students Fear It Is Harming Their Critical Thinking

62%
  • A new RAND Corporation report based on a nationally representative survey of 1,214 youth (ages 12–29) finds the share of students using AI for homework rose sharply from 48% in May 2025 to 62% in December 2025 — with middle and high schoolers driving the steepest increase
  • 67% of students now endorse the statement "the more students use AI for schoolwork, the more it will harm their critical thinking skills" — up more than 10 percentage points from ten months earlier, revealing a growing self-awareness about AI dependency even as usage climbs
  • Most students do not consider AI use cheating — except when using it to get direct answers for homework; brainstorming, editing, and research assistance are widely viewed as acceptable, creating inconsistent norms that complicate school policy
  • Students in higher grade levels are more likely to believe their teachers are checking homework for AI use and to worry about being falsely accused of cheating — creating anxiety that complicates productive AI integration in classrooms
  • RAND recommends schools adopt the "flipped classroom" model — where students first engage with content at home and then practice independently during AI-free class time — to preserve cognitive development while allowing AI-assisted learning outside school
  • The report calls for schoolwide consistency in AI policies: explicit rules about when AI can and cannot be used could reduce student anxiety and dampen misuse, even if enforcement remains imperfect in practice

Penn State University — AI Transformation Initiative

Penn State Launches University-Wide AI Literacy Framework — Four Core Competencies for All Students, Faculty, and Staff

4
  • Penn State has launched the first component of its AI Transformation Initiative: a university-wide AI Literacy Framework establishing a shared foundation for how all students, faculty, academic administrators, and staff engage responsibly and effectively with AI technologies
  • The framework defines four core elements of AI literacy: Technical Knowledge (how AI systems work and their limitations), Ethical Awareness and Social Responsibility (bias, privacy, transparency), Critical Thinking (evaluating AI outputs and knowing when AI use is appropriate), and Practical Use (applying AI tools intentionally in academic and professional settings)
  • Provost Fotis Sotiropoulos: "Students will graduate with foundational knowledge of AI tools, applications, limitations and societal impact. Equally important, we are investing in opportunities for our faculty and staff to deepen their own understanding and use of these technologies"
  • Four working groups — covering undergraduate students, graduate students, faculty, and staff — provided coordinated input to shape the framework, ensuring it addresses the distinct responsibilities and decision-making contexts across all university roles
  • The framework is designed to be adaptable across disciplines and roles, with foundational AI learning modules launching for all Penn State students, faculty, and staff — supported by University Libraries, Teaching and Learning with Technology, and the Schreyer Institute for Teaching Excellence
  • Penn State's initiative joins a wave of university-wide AI literacy efforts in 2026, including similar frameworks at American University, University of Arizona, and dozens of other institutions responding to the growing expectation that graduates enter the workforce AI-ready

Key Themes Emerging

Federal AI Policy Leaves Education Gaps

The White House's new National AI Policy Framework represents the Trump administration's most comprehensive AI governance statement to date — but its near-silence on K-12 education, AI literacy for students, and cognitive development risks has drawn sharp criticism from educators and advocates. By framing AI policy primarily around innovation, workforce development, and federal preemption of state regulations, the framework leaves schools to navigate AI integration without federal guidance. This vacuum is being filled by states like California, New York, and Colorado — and by institutions like Penn State and American University — creating a patchwork of AI education policies that varies dramatically by geography and institution type.

The Critical Thinking Paradox Deepens

RAND's finding that 67% of students believe AI harms their critical thinking — even as usage climbs to 62% — reveals a profound tension at the heart of AI adoption in education. Students are not unaware of the risks; they are caught between the immediate convenience of AI assistance and a growing sense that it may be eroding the very cognitive capacities they need for long-term success. The policy implication is clear: schools must design AI integration that builds cognitive skills rather than replacing them — using approaches like flipped classrooms, guided reasoning scaffolds, and AI-free practice time to ensure AI augments rather than substitutes for student thinking.

AI as a Teacher Retention Tool

The emerging evidence that AI teaching assistants can improve teacher retention by 40% or more reframes the AI-in-education debate in an important way: AI is not just a student tool or a curriculum challenge — it is increasingly a workforce solution for a profession in crisis. With 44% of teachers leaving within five years and $2.2 billion lost annually to turnover, the administrative burden that drives burnout is a structural problem that AI tools are uniquely positioned to address. The key insight from successful implementations is that AI must be deployed as a teacher-empowerment tool, not a cost-cutting measure — with teacher input, comprehensive training, and ongoing support determining whether adoption succeeds or fails.

University AI Governance Matures

The simultaneous launch of AI literacy frameworks at Penn State, new AI guidelines at American University, and an OpenAI enterprise contract at CU Boulder signals that higher education is moving from ad hoc AI experimentation to structured institutional governance. The challenge is that governance frameworks are arriving unevenly: research universities with dedicated AI centers and working groups are developing sophisticated policies, while smaller institutions and community colleges lag behind. The University of North Texas's decision to cut 70+ academic programs amid this transition underscores the financial pressures reshaping higher education — with AI both driving disruption and offering potential solutions to the efficiency and enrollment challenges institutions face.

Professional Schools Lead AI Curriculum Reform

California's proposal to require law schools to teach AI skills reflects a broader trend: professional schools — in law, medicine, business, and engineering — are increasingly at the forefront of AI curriculum reform, driven by the direct and immediate impact of AI tools on their graduates' professional practice. Unlike general AI literacy initiatives, professional school AI training is grounded in specific use cases, ethical obligations, and regulatory contexts that give it practical urgency. The 89% of law school deans who support AI training, combined with courts already sanctioning attorneys for AI-generated errors, creates a compelling case that professional AI competence is becoming a baseline requirement — not an elective enhancement.

Student AI Governance: Real Data Replaces Surveys

The emergence of real-time behavioral data from school AI platforms — such as Securly's analysis of 1.2 million student interactions across 1,300+ districts — marks an important shift in how schools understand student AI use. Rather than relying on self-reported surveys (which consistently undercount problematic use), schools now have access to actual behavioral patterns: what students are asking AI, when they are violating policies, and how AI use varies by grade level, subject, and time of day. This data infrastructure is essential for evidence-based AI policy — allowing schools to move from blanket bans or unrestricted access toward nuanced, context-specific guidance grounded in what students are actually doing with AI tools.
