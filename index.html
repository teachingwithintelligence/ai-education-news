U.S. National Science Foundation — AI Teacher Training Initiative

NSF Invests $11 Million to Expand AI Professional Development for K-12 Teachers Nationwide

$11M
  • The U.S. National Science Foundation announced today an $11 million award to the Computer Science Teachers Association (CSTA) to launch "AI PD Weeks: CS Foundations for Creating with AI" — a multistate initiative directly tied to the executive order on "Advancing Artificial Intelligence Education for American Youth"
  • The program will operate in Indiana, South Carolina, Minnesota, New Jersey, Iowa, Illinois, and at least three additional states over the next two years, directly supporting approximately 2,500–3,000 K-12 teachers
  • Based on conservative estimates of 200 students per teacher, this investment is expected to expand enhanced AI and computer science learning opportunities to 500,000–600,000 students nationwide
  • Teachers will deepen knowledge of core CS concepts — including data, algorithms, abstraction, and systems — and learn how they underpin AI tools, with the goal of moving students beyond passive AI use toward deeper understanding and meaningful creation
  • NSF acting director Brian Stone: "This investment will equip thousands of educators with the tools needed to bring AI and computer science into the classroom, turning the Executive Order into action and preparing the next generation to become innovators, builders and leaders"
  • CSTA executive director Jake Baskin: "AI is arriving in classrooms faster than AI literacy — and that gap is growing. Computer science provides the foundation students need to truly understand AI, and CS teachers are uniquely positioned to close that gap"

RAND Corporation — American Youth Panel Survey

RAND Study: 62% of Students Now Use AI for Homework — and 67% Believe It Harms Their Critical Thinking

62%
  • A new RAND Corporation report drawing on nationally representative surveys of 1,214 American youth ages 12–29 finds the share of students using AI for homework jumped from 48% in May 2025 to 62% in December 2025 — with middle and high schoolers driving the overall increase
  • 67% of students now endorse the statement "The more students use AI for their schoolwork, the more it will harm their critical thinking skills" — up more than 10 percentage points from ten months earlier, signaling growing self-awareness about AI dependency
  • With the exception of using AI to get direct answers for homework, most students do not feel that other AI-assisted schoolwork constitutes cheating — revealing a significant gap between student perceptions and educator concerns about academic integrity
  • Students in higher grade levels are more likely to say their school's AI rules depend on the specific teacher, believe teachers are checking homework for AI use, and worry about being falsely accused of AI-assisted cheating
  • More female than male students believe AI use harms critical thinking skills and think their teachers are actively checking homework for AI-generated content
  • RAND recommends schools adopt the "flipped classroom" model — where students first engage with new content at home, then do independent practice during AI-free, teacher-led class time — as a structural solution to cognitive offloading concerns

Fortune — BlackRock CEO Larry Fink Warning

BlackRock CEO Larry Fink Warns AI Is Creating a Job Market 'Crisis' for the Class of 2026 Graduates

5.6%
  • BlackRock CEO Larry Fink warned at the 2026 Infrastructure Summit that "when this year's college graduates enter the workforce, we could see the highest unemployment rate among them in years — even without a recession," citing AI's rapid displacement of entry-level white-collar roles
  • The unemployment rate among recent college graduates ages 22–27 already sits at 5.6% — near levels not seen since 2013, excluding the pandemic — while job postings on Handshake fell more than 16% between August 2024 and August 2025
  • More than half of employers view the job market for the class of 2026 as "poor" or "fair," according to the National Association of Colleges and Employers — the most pessimistic reading since the start of the pandemic
  • Fink argued the traditional college-to-career pipeline is fracturing: "Really post World War II, the pathway to a white-collar job was a college education, and AI is going to disrupt many of those types of jobs"
  • To address the gap, BlackRock committed to invest $100 million in skilled-trade programs, aiming to reach 50,000 workers over five years in roles like electricians, HVAC technicians, plumbers, and ironworkers — fields being fueled by AI infrastructure expansion
  • AMD CEO Lisa Su offered a counterpoint, saying the Class of 2026 is "graduating at an exciting time, as AI transforms our world and expands what is possible" — urging graduates to lean into the technology rather than fear it

Dartmouth College — AI Program Expansion

Dartmouth — Birthplace of AI — Launches Comprehensive Suite of New AI Programs Across Engineering, Business, and Medicine

🎓
  • Dartmouth, which hosted the 1956 Summer Research Project that coined the term "artificial intelligence," announced a sweeping expansion of AI programs across its schools — including new degree tracks at Thayer Engineering, AI-integrated MBA electives at Tuck, and medical AI courses at Geisel School of Medicine
  • Thayer School of Engineering students can now choose an AI track within the Master of Engineering program and a new undergraduate concentration in AI — covering machine learning, high-dimensional sensing, optimization, and reinforcement learning through hands-on, project-based courses

Jobs for the Future (JFF) — National Learner Survey

JFF Survey: AI Usage in Education Is Growing Rapidly, But Guidance Gaps Persist — Students Still Learn About AI More From Social Media Than Schools

69%
  • A new national survey by Jobs for the Future (JFF) finds nearly 7 in 10 learners now report AI is incorporated into their coursework — up from 57% in 2024 — with formal AI training from institutions rising more than 20 percentage points in one year to 69%
  • Despite the rise in institutional AI training, learners are still more likely to seek AI information from social media (48%), news articles (38%), and friends or family (30%) than from their school (23%) — highlighting a persistent credibility gap in formal AI education
  • AI policies across education providers remain deeply uneven: 31% of learners report their institution fully permits AI use, while 11% say it bans AI entirely and 13% say they do not know their school's AI policy at all
  • 40% of learners report spending more time collaborating with peers as a result of AI, while 41% report spending less — reflecting AI's polarizing and unpredictable impact on the social dynamics of learning environments
  • Learners most commonly use AI to complete assignments more efficiently (44%), enhance learning and understanding (38%), and access AI-driven tutoring (35%) — but 11% say AI has not significantly supported their education
  • JFF Vice President Ben Pring: "Increased use does not automatically translate into increased efficacy. If schools want to realize AI's potential, they must build trust, provide clear guidance, and ensure that AI augments relationships and classroom experience"

World Economic Forum — AI Cognitive Infrastructure Policy

World Economic Forum: As AI Becomes 'Cognitive Infrastructure,' Policymakers Must Govern It Like Utilities — or Risk Societal Dependency

⚠️
  • A new World Economic Forum analysis published today argues that AI has crossed a threshold to become "cognitive infrastructure" — as foundational to modern life as electricity or the internet — requiring governance frameworks that treat it as a public utility rather than a private product
  • The report warns that without "cognitive-aware" design and literacy frameworks, AI risks replacing rather than reinforcing human capability — a concern directly applicable to education, where students increasingly outsource reasoning to AI tools
  • Policymakers are urged to prioritize AI literacy as a foundational skill at all education levels, ensuring students understand not just how to use AI but how it works, where it fails, and how to maintain independent cognitive capacity
  • The analysis draws parallels to historical infrastructure transitions: just as societies had to develop "electricity literacy" to safely use electrical systems, today's students need "AI literacy" to navigate an AI-saturated world without becoming cognitively dependent
  • The report identifies education systems as the primary lever for building societal AI resilience — arguing that schools that fail to teach AI literacy are producing graduates who will be disproportionately vulnerable to AI-driven labor displacement
  • The WEF calls for international coordination on AI education standards, noting that countries with strong AI literacy programs will hold significant competitive advantages in the workforce of the 2030s

Key Themes Emerging

Federal Investment Accelerates K-12 AI Teacher Training

Today's NSF $11 million investment in CSTA's AI PD Weeks program marks a significant escalation of federal commitment to K-12 AI education, directly tied to the White House executive order on AI education for American youth. With 2,500–3,000 teachers targeted across at least 10 states, the initiative represents a model for scaling AI literacy from the classroom up — recognizing that teacher preparation is the critical bottleneck in bringing meaningful AI education to students. The program's emphasis on moving students "beyond passive AI use toward deeper understanding and meaningful creation" sets a new pedagogical standard for what AI education should accomplish.

Student AI Dependency and Critical Thinking Concerns Intensify

The RAND Corporation's latest American Youth Panel data reveals a troubling paradox: as more students use AI for homework (62%, up from 48% in just seven months), a growing majority (67%) believe it is harming their critical thinking skills. This self-awareness among students has not translated into reduced use — suggesting that structural interventions, not individual willpower, are needed. RAND's recommendation to adopt flipped classroom models — where AI-free in-class practice follows at-home AI-assisted exploration — offers a practical framework for schools seeking to preserve cognitive development while embracing AI's learning benefits.

Class of 2026 Faces Unprecedented AI-Driven Job Market Disruption

BlackRock CEO Larry Fink's warning that the class of 2026 could face the highest graduate unemployment in years — even without a recession — is backed by data showing 5.6% graduate unemployment, a 16% drop in entry-level job postings, and more than half of employers rating the market as "poor" or "fair." The convergence of AI agent deployment, reduced entry-level hiring, and a generation entering the workforce with primarily codifiable knowledge creates a structural challenge that universities must address through curriculum redesign. BlackRock's $100 million investment in skilled-trade programs signals that the AI economy will create new jobs — but not the ones traditional four-year degrees have historically prepared graduates for.

University AI Degree Programs Proliferate at Historic Pace

The simultaneous launch of AI programs at Dartmouth, University of New Haven, Georgetown, Olivet Nazarene, and Vanderbilt — alongside the proposed NSF AI Education Act of 2026 authorizing AI scholarships — reflects a higher education sector racing to build academic infrastructure for AI at scale. Dartmouth's framing is particularly significant: as the birthplace of AI, its emphasis on ensuring tools "amplify human judgment rather than replace it" sets a philosophical tone for responsible AI education. Universities are no longer just training AI users — they are training AI architects, ethicists, and governance professionals who will shape how the technology develops.

State and Federal AI Policy Races to Keep Pace With Classroom Reality

Nevada's "hurry up, but don't rush" legislative debate captures the dilemma facing policymakers nationwide: AI is already in classrooms, but governance frameworks are still being written. The Brookings Institution's warning that risks overshadow benefits, combined with the Guinn Center's finding that there is "no reliable, long-term data" on AI's educational effectiveness, creates a policy environment where urgency and caution are in direct tension. The proposed NSF AI Education Act, Nevada's pending legislation, and New York City's forthcoming AI guidelines all signal that 2026 will be a pivotal year for formalizing AI governance in education at every level of government.

AI as 'Cognitive Infrastructure' Demands New Literacy Frameworks

The World Economic Forum's framing of AI as "cognitive infrastructure" — as essential and potentially as dangerous as electricity or the internet — elevates AI literacy from a nice-to-have skill to a foundational civic competency. The JFF survey's finding that students still learn about AI more from social media than from schools underscores the urgency: if educational institutions do not become authoritative sources of AI knowledge, students will form their understanding of the technology from unvetted, commercially motivated sources. Countries and institutions that build robust AI literacy programs now will produce graduates with the cognitive resilience and critical judgment needed to thrive in — and responsibly govern — an AI-saturated world.
