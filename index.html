Education Week — K-12 Research

Real-Time Data Reveals How Students Use AI on School Technology — 1 in 5 Interactions Flagged as Problematic

42%
  • New real-time analysis by Securly — covering more than 1.2 million student AI interactions on school technology — found that roughly 1 in 5 (20%) involved cheating, self-harm, bullying, or other problematic behaviors
  • Roughly 1 in 50 student-AI interactions were flagged as potential red flags for violence, cyberbullying, or self-harm — representing more than 24,000 troubling queries in the dataset, including students asking AI how to draft messages about suicidal thoughts
  • ChatGPT is by far the most popular AI tool among students, accounting for 42% of interactions; Securly's own AI Chat made up 28%; Google's Gemini comprised 21%; and purpose-built edtech tools (MagicSchool, SchoolAI, BriskTeaching) comprised 9%
  • Nearly all deflected student queries — 95% — were from students trying to get AI tools to complete their schoolwork for them, confirming that academic integrity remains the dominant AI challenge for K-12 schools
  • Securly detected a higher percentage of potentially unsafe AI interactions (2%) than potentially unsafe student internet searches (0.4%) — suggesting AI tools present a distinct and elevated safety challenge compared to general internet use
  • The data underscores an urgent need for schools to move beyond simple AI blocking toward nuanced policies that distinguish between productive and harmful AI use — and invest in real-time monitoring and intervention systems

Department of Labor — AI Policy

Department of Labor Issues "AI Literacy" Framework — Calls on Schools, Employers, and States to Build AI-Ready Workforces

🏛️
  • The U.S. Department of Labor issued its new "Artificial Intelligence Literacy Framework" on March 9, 2026, defining AI literacy as "a foundational set of competencies that enable individuals to use and evaluate AI technologies responsibly"
  • The framework's six foundational content areas cover: understanding what AI is and its limits; applying AI across industries; directing AI through effective prompting; evaluating AI outputs for accuracy; and using AI responsibly to protect confidential information
  • For educators and job trainers, the DOL seeks to "guide curriculum development" to integrate AI literacy — stressing collaboration between training programs and local employers to provide hands-on experience with AI tools most relevant to regional labor market needs
  • The DOL emphasizes "experiential learning" through direct, hands-on AI use for real-work tasks — embedding AI tool uses relevant to an individual's job or industry, rather than treating AI literacy as a standalone academic subject
  • For employers, the DOL encourages building AI literacy across the entire workforce — providing clear internal guidance on appropriate AI use and identifying roles that may require deeper non-AI proficiency to ensure equitable access to AI tools
  • The framework positions AI literacy as a workforce equity issue: consistent AI training across all workers gives every employee the same entry point to access AI in the workplace, helping re-energize employees who may feel alienated by new technology

Spectrum News / McKinsey — Job Market

New Grads Navigate Tighter Job Market as AI Cuts Entry-Level Roles — Job Postings Down 35%, McKinsey Warns of "Triple Whammy"

35%
  • College seniors report fierce competition as AI reshapes hiring, with new entry-level job postings down 35% compared to prior years — while the number of applicants per position has surged, intensifying competition for a shrinking pool of roles
  • McKinsey analysts warn young graduates potentially face a "triple whammy": a general labour-market slowdown, a sharper decline in graduate-level job openings, and increased competition from AI tools that can perform many entry-level tasks
  • Anthropic's new labor market research finds that for every 10 percentage point increase in a job's AI coverage score, projected employment growth drops by 0.6 percentage points — with high AI-coverage roles showing consistently lower growth trajectories
  • Younger workers are increasingly pursuing careers in skilled trades — jobs considered more insulated from AI-driven cuts — with CNBC reporting a notable shift among recent graduates toward electricians, plumbers, and HVAC technicians
  • The first major labor market impact of AI is appearing in hiring patterns rather than mass layoffs: companies are raising productivity requirements for entry-level roles, making it harder for new graduates to break in even when positions technically exist
  • For educators, the implication is clear: graduates need AI skills not just to be competitive, but to understand the structural forces reshaping their career options — and universities that don't integrate AI into career preparation risk failing their students

Washington Post — Opinion / K-12

Schools Are Teaching AI — and Making a Massive Mistake: Teach Kids Agency Over Technology, Not Just How to Use It

🧠
  • A Washington Post opinion piece published March 10, 2026 argues that schools rushing to teach AI usage are making a fundamental pedagogical error: they are training students to be proficient users of AI rather than critical agents who can evaluate, question, and direct it
  • The distinction matters enormously: a student who knows how to use ChatGPT is a consumer of AI; a student who understands when to use it, when not to, and how to evaluate its outputs is an agent capable of working with AI responsibly in complex real-world contexts
  • Inside Higher Ed's concurrent analysis argues that AI is "not replacing learning — it's exposing where learning was thin to begin with," suggesting that the AI cheating crisis is a symptom of deeper problems with how schools have been assessing learning versus outputs
  • Pew Research data published this week shows teens most commonly use AI to search for information (57%), homework help (54%), and entertainment (47%) — suggesting that student AI use is largely passive and consumption-oriented rather than creative or analytical
  • An international study across five countries found that virtually all students engage with generative AI, and 36% said they are using AI to complete more than half of their study tasks — raising urgent questions about what students are actually learning
  • The emerging consensus among education researchers: the goal should not be AI fluency as a technical skill, but AI agency — the capacity to make deliberate, informed choices about when and how to use AI tools in service of genuine learning and human judgment

Key Themes Emerging

AI Is Transforming STEM Learning at Unprecedented Scale

OpenAI's launch of interactive visual learning modules for 140 million weekly ChatGPT users represents a step-change in how AI is being deployed for STEM education. By making abstract math and science concepts manipulable and visual — rather than simply providing answers — OpenAI is attempting to address the longstanding critique that AI tools undermine deep learning. The simultaneous announcement of OpenAI Certifications, the Learning Outcomes Measurement Suite, and campus-wide ChatGPT Edu deployments at dozens of universities signals that AI companies are now competing on learning outcomes, not just engagement metrics.

University AI Programs Are Expanding Rapidly Across Disciplines

Northwestern's new undergraduate AI major — the first in its new BSE degree — is part of a national wave that now spans engineering, business, humanities, and technical colleges. The University of Northern Iowa, Michigan Tech, Penn College of Technology, and the University of San Diego all announced new AI programs this week alone. The expansion of AI majors and minors beyond engineering schools into business and humanities colleges reflects a growing recognition that AI literacy is a general professional competency, not a technical specialty — and that universities failing to offer structured AI education risk graduating students unprepared for the labor market they will enter.

Teacher AI Training Is Becoming a National Priority

Pearson's launch of 250,000 free AI training vouchers for U.S. high school teachers — backed by the White House — represents a meaningful escalation in the scale and seriousness of teacher AI professional development. The DOL's new AI Literacy Framework simultaneously calls on educators to integrate AI literacy into curriculum development and collaborate with local employers on hands-on AI training. The convergence of federal policy, private sector investment, and institutional commitment to teacher AI training suggests that 2026 may be the year when teacher AI readiness finally begins to catch up with student AI adoption.

Student AI Use Is Widespread but Largely Passive and Risky

Securly's real-time data — covering 1.2 million student AI interactions — paints a sobering picture: 1 in 5 interactions involved problematic behaviors, 95% of deflected queries were students trying to get AI to complete their schoolwork, and ChatGPT dominates student AI use with 42% of interactions. Pew Research data showing teens primarily use AI for information search (57%) and homework help (54%) confirms that student AI use is largely passive and consumption-oriented. The challenge for educators is not simply preventing misuse but cultivating the kind of active, critical AI engagement that builds genuine understanding rather than outsourcing it.

AI Policy Is Moving from School-Level to Federal and State Frameworks

The DOL's new AI Literacy Framework and California's ongoing school-level debates represent two ends of a rapidly evolving policy spectrum. The DOL framework provides a national vocabulary for AI literacy — covering understanding, application, prompting, evaluation, and responsible use — that educators and employers can align around. California's experience illustrates the complexity of implementation: districts vary widely in their approaches, researchers urge caution, and the LAUSD chatbot failure serves as a cautionary tale about rushing to scale. The emerging consensus is that flexible, guidance-based approaches — like West Virginia's checklist model — are more durable than rigid mandates in a rapidly evolving technological landscape.

AI Is Reshaping the Graduate Job Market in Ways Official Data Misses

The 35% decline in new entry-level job postings — combined with McKinsey's "triple whammy" warning and Anthropic's finding that higher AI coverage correlates with lower employment growth — suggests that AI's labor market impact is already significant and accelerating. The shift toward skilled trades among younger workers reflects a rational response to AI-driven disruption in white-collar entry-level roles. For universities, the implication is urgent: career preparation must now include explicit AI skills training, not as a competitive differentiator, but as a baseline requirement for labor market entry in a growing number of fields.
