Pew Research: 57% of Teens Now Use AI Chatbots for Schoolwork — 10% Do All or Most Homework with AI

57%
  • New Pew Research Center survey of teens ages 13–17 finds 57% use chatbots to search for information, while 54% specifically use AI for "help with homework"
  • A striking 10% of all teen respondents report using AI for "all or most of their homework" — students who don't use AI for homework are now the minority at 45%
  • Equity gap emerging: 20% of teens in households earning under $30,000/year do "all or most" homework with AI, compared to just 7% in households earning over $75,000
  • Black and Hispanic teens are 12% more likely than white counterparts to do all or most schoolwork with AI chatbots
  • About 25% of teens who use AI for school say chatbots are "extremely" or "very helpful" for completing schoolwork
  • AI companies are actively working their way into teachers' unions and classrooms, raising concerns about the pace of adoption outrunning safeguards
  • Researchers warn of "cognitive and social effects of AI dependence in young students" as the technology becomes normalized in academic settings

Colleges Turn to AI to Decide Who Gets In — Scanning 250,000 Essays in Under an Hour

50%+
  • Admissions offices nationwide are deploying AI to read essays and review transcripts, aiming to streamline workloads amid higher education funding pressures
  • Virginia Tech's AI system scans 250,000 essays in under an hour — compared to a human reader averaging two minutes per essay — saving at least 8,000 hours
  • Both AI and a human reader score each essay; discrepancies trigger an additional human review, creating a hybrid model
  • As early as 2023, about 50% of colleges were using AI in admissions review — a figure that has likely increased significantly since
  • MIT economics professor warns: if many universities rely on the same AI models, they could end up making similar decisions and favoring a narrow type of applicant
  • Students are cautioned against using AI to write their essays, yet colleges may use AI to evaluate those same essays — raising fairness concerns
  • Quote from admissions expert: "Ten years from now, all bets are off. I'm guessing AI will be admitting students."

AI Cuts Entry-Level Jobs — Graduate School Applications Surge as Recent Grads Face 5.6% Unemployment

5.6%
  • As of November 2025, 5.6% of recent college graduates were unemployed, compared to 4.2% of all workers — a widening gap driven by AI displacement of entry-level roles
  • Entry-level positions are being disproportionately replaced by AI, with many young workers feeling unprepared to enter a workforce increasingly advancing with the technology
  • Law school admissions during the 2024–2025 cycle reached the highest level in over a decade, as bachelor's degree holders seek alternative paths
  • Graduate school is becoming a more attractive option — delaying loan repayment and typically leading to higher-paying roles than bachelor's-level positions
  • Fields projected to have the highest job openings for graduate degree holders: mental health counselors, lawyers, and career counselors
  • Tariffs stifling many companies' abilities to hire are compounding the AI-driven displacement effect on new graduates
  • Experts advise students to pursue graduate degrees in fields requiring advanced human judgment, creativity, and domain expertise that AI cannot easily replicate

The Cavalier Daily / University of Virginia

UVA Panel: Mandatory AI Literacy Course Proposed — Experts Reject University-Wide AI Policy as Too Restrictive

🎓
  • UVA's Honor Committee hosted a panel on Feb. 20 to discuss AI's future in academia, featuring faculty, students, and the Dean of Libraries
  • Panel unanimously agreed that a University-wide AI policy would NOT be the best approach — concern it would constrain academic freedom and limit students' ability to develop valuable AI skills
  • Proposal for a mandatory AI literacy course for all students covering both usage and ethics, including AI's societal and political impacts on education
  • Dean of Libraries Leo Lo: "I have never seen something like this disrupting education in my lifetime — calculators, internet, computers — I don't think any of them can compare"
  • Panelists recommend a "culture of documentation": if students use AI, they should own it, acknowledge it, and document it transparently
  • AI detection tools criticized as inaccurate and fostering distrust and anxiety — panelists recommend prohibiting faculty from using them
  • Concern raised that AI corporations' interests do not align with higher education institutions — universities urged to leverage their procurement power to make demands of tech companies

Key Themes Emerging

AI Homework Use Reaches Tipping Point

Pew Research confirms that students who don't use AI for homework are now the minority. With 10% of teens doing all or most assignments via AI, and a stark equity gap emerging along income and racial lines, the normalization of AI-assisted schoolwork is raising urgent questions about learning outcomes, fairness, and what education is actually preparing students for.

AI in College Admissions: A Double Standard?

More than half of U.S. colleges now use AI to evaluate applications — yet students are warned not to use AI to write them. Virginia Tech's system scans 250,000 essays in under an hour, saving 8,000 hours of human labor. Critics warn this creates a fairness paradox and risks homogenizing admitted classes if institutions rely on the same AI models.

Universities Accelerating AI Integration

UNC Charlotte's new AI Accelerator — with 1,060+ faculty already trained — reflects a national wave of universities moving from reactive policy debates to proactive AI infrastructure. With new AI programs launching in fall 2026 and 176 institutions joining the AAC&U AI pedagogy initiative, higher education is shifting from asking "should we use AI?" to "how do we use it responsibly?"

Graduate School as an AI-Era Refuge

As AI eliminates entry-level roles and recent graduate unemployment hits 5.6%, law school applications have reached a decade high. Graduate school is emerging as a strategic response — not just for career advancement, but as a hedge against AI displacement. Fields requiring advanced human judgment, such as counseling and law, are projected to remain in high demand.

AI Literacy: From Optional to Essential

Cambridge's reimagined K-8 curriculum and UVA's proposed mandatory AI literacy course reflect a growing consensus: AI literacy is no longer a supplemental skill but a core educational requirement. The emphasis is shifting from "how to use AI" to "when and whether to use it" — developing the judgment and critical thinking that AI itself cannot replicate.

Policy Vacuum vs. Institutional Action

UVA's panel unanimously rejected a top-down AI policy as too restrictive, favoring classroom-level dialogue and a culture of transparent documentation. This mirrors a broader national tension: federal guidance remains absent, yet institutions are increasingly taking matters into their own hands — through accelerators, literacy curricula, and faculty training — to fill the governance gap.
