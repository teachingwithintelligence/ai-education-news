Education Week — Bipartisan Policy Center Commission

Bipartisan Commission Demands Bold National Action to Prepare Students for AI-Fueled Workforce Disruption

🏛️
  • A new report from the Bipartisan Policy Center's workforce commission — co-chaired by former governors Deval Patrick (D-MA) and Bill Haslam (R-TN) — calls for a White House talent advisory council to form a unified, cross-government strategy for training workers in the AI era
  • The commission recommends a federal "K-12 scorecard" mapping the gaps between states' definitions of academic proficiency and performance on the National Assessment of Educational Progress — and calls on states to dramatically raise standards to align with AI-era workforce demands
  • States and districts must rethink high school entirely, providing programs that allow students to graduate with credentials and skills sought by future employers — with federal incentives for competency-based education and new secondary education models
  • The commission calls for a national "beating the odds" incentive program providing increased Title I funding to states that show accelerated growth for English learners, students with disabilities, and other populations on college and career readiness metrics
  • Former U.S. Secretary of Education Margaret Spellings: "There will never be a substitute for teaching students to read and be numerate at some level. Period. With or without AI" — framing literacy as the non-negotiable foundation for any AI-era education strategy
  • Former Republican Governor Bill Haslam: "No matter where you are on the economic spectrum, this discombobulation is going to affect you. The strategy should be national, but the tactics should be state and local" — acknowledging the bipartisan urgency of AI workforce preparation
  • LinkedIn data cited at the AI+Education Summit (March 11, 2026) shows 70% of job skills are expected to change by 2030 due to AI — making the commission's call for bold, immediate action a matter of national competitiveness, not just education policy

The Daily Northwestern — New AI Major

Northwestern University Launches Undergraduate AI Major This Fall — With Required Ethics Course for All Students

🎓
  • Northwestern University announced a new undergraduate AI major launching fall 2026, housed in the McCormick School of Engineering's Department of Computer Science — joining Carnegie Mellon and MIT in expanding dedicated AI academic programs
  • About 60 students had already filled out McCormick's interest form within hours of the announcement — signaling strong demand from students who see AI specialization as critical to their career prospects
  • Coursework will cover machine learning, natural language processing, and the mathematical concepts underlying modern AI technologies — with all students required to take a course on the societal impact of AI
  • "Because of the image and impact AI tools are having on society, having a component of ethics for students to have a deeper understanding and appreciation for what the tools that they are building could be used for — we felt that was an important element," said CS Department Chair Samir Khuller
  • The program is available to all undergraduate NU students, requiring 18 units for students outside McCormick and 48 units for students already enrolled in McCormick, including the school's core requirements
  • Weinberg junior Callie Berthold: "People who can master developing fields are the ones that are going to become famous for it or have the most opportunities. A place like Northwestern, being able to spot that early on and then provide resources to students is really critical"

Gothamist — NYC Education

NYC Plans New AI-Focused High School in Lower Manhattan — Even as City's AI Rules Remain Delayed

🏙️
  • New York City's Department of Education is proposing the Next Generation Technology High School in Lower Manhattan — a selective school designed to prepare students for careers in cybersecurity, computer science, robotics, and advanced math, making students "builders as well as ethical users of AI"
  • A public hearing is scheduled for April 14 ahead of a vote by the Panel for Educational Policy — but the proposal has sparked debate as the city has still not delivered promised AI guidance that was due in early February 2026
  • Carnegie Mellon University would offer students priority access to its summer technology research program, and Google and OpenAI "have been at the table" for partnerships — signaling strong industry interest in shaping the school's curriculum
  • Some parents and educators are alarmed: "It's clear that they see AI as the new 'next big thing' and they don't want to be left behind. But social media was the new next big thing a few years ago and we are still coming to terms with its effects on our students," said one community education council member
  • Supporters argue the school is a timely response to a booming field: "There's a misconception because it was initially referred to as the 'AI high school,' that somehow this is a school where kids are just going to be on AI all day long. And that is absolutely not the case," said Deborah Alexander of the Citywide Council on High Schools
  • The debate mirrors national tensions between preparing students for an AI-saturated economy and ensuring adequate safeguards, policies, and understanding of AI's impact on learning and social-emotional development are in place first

Digital Education Council — Global AI Student Survey 2026

92% of Students Now Use AI — But 65% Fear It Makes Learning Too Shallow, New Global Survey Finds

92%
  • The Digital Education Council's AI in Higher Education Latin America Survey 2026 finds that AI use among students is nearly universal at 92% — a 6% increase from the 2024 Global AI Student Survey, where adoption stood at 86%
  • Despite near-universal adoption, 65% of students feel concerned that AI may make learning too shallow and discourage critical thinking and creativity — a striking tension between widespread use and deep ambivalence about its educational value
  • 67% of students report using AI daily or weekly, with ChatGPT emerging as the most widely used tool (88% of frequent AI users), alongside Google Gemini, NotebookLM, Grok, and Claude
  • 73% of students foresee themselves using AI in their future jobs — suggesting that AI use during studies is closely connected to how students expect to work, making AI literacy a career preparation issue as much as an academic one
  • 56% of students express concern about data privacy when using AI tools, and 56% worry about the fairness of AI in evaluating their work — pointing to governance gaps that institutions must address to maintain student trust
  • While nearly half of students (50%) support teacher use of AI to provide feedback on assignments, they remain cautious about AI being used to grade or evaluate their performance — suggesting a clear boundary students want institutions to respect

HigherEdJobs / The EduLedger — EAB Survey

46% of High School Students Now Use AI to Search for Colleges — and 39% Are Reconsidering Whether to Attend at All

46%
  • A new national survey by EAB of more than 5,000 high school students finds that 46% now use AI tools such as ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity during their college searches — nearly double the 26% who reported doing so just months earlier in spring 2025
  • Nearly 1 in 5 students (18%) said they removed a college from their consideration list based on information surfaced through AI-generated search results — meaning AI is now shaping first impressions and real enrollment decisions before colleges ever have direct contact with prospective applicants
  • 62% of AI users turned to tools to find schools matching their academic profile and preferences; one in four reported having an ongoing conversation with an AI tool about their college search — with AI functioning as "a surrogate for real-world advisors"
  • 39% of students said advances in AI are pushing them to consider alternatives to college altogether, including starting a business, pursuing an apprenticeship, or entering the workforce directly — a finding that should alarm higher education leaders
  • The most common emotional response students reported about AI's effect on their futures was uncertainty (44%), followed by skepticism (33%), concern (32%), and anxiety (31%) — with one student writing: "It feels like AI is 'stealing' all the things. I am feeling concerned I won't be able to get a job later"
  • More than half of students said they would react negatively to messages they perceived as AI-generated from colleges — yet EAB's own testing data showed a 29-percentage-point increase in engagement when colleges used hyper-personalized content, suggesting personalization can overcome AI skepticism

70%

Barnard College — Responsible AI Symposium

Barnard College Hosts "Responsible AI and the Liberal Arts" Symposium — Calls for Ethics-Centered AI Literacy Framework

🔬
  • Barnard College hosted its "Symposium on Responsible AI and the Liberal Arts" on March 11, 2026, bringing together AI researchers, alumnae, faculty, and students to examine what responsible AI looks like on a college campus — with Provost Rebecca Walkowitz framing responsible AI as "designing, developing, and deploying AI systems in ways that are transparent, fair, accountable, safe, and aligned with human values"
  • Keynote speaker Kathleen McKeown, founding director of Columbia's Data Science Institute, guided attendees through the arc of AI research — from her own work in the 1980s to today's Large Language Models — emphasizing the paradigm shift LLMs have brought to research and education
  • Five Barnard alumnae across journalism, education, mental health, and product design discussed responsible AI in their fields — repeatedly returning to themes of safety, transparency, and human connection as the non-negotiable foundations of ethical AI deployment
  • Julie Scelfo, journalist and founder of Mothers Against Media Addiction: "Responsible AI is built with people in mind. Safe AI is only rolled out once we know it's safe. That there are regulations in place and transparency requirements, so that we can know what's happening inside these companies"
  • Barnard's existing AI literacy framework — a four-level pyramid covering understanding, using, analyzing, and creating AI — is being expanded to add a "responsible and ethical use" dimension, reflecting the symposium's finding that capability without responsibility is insufficient
  • The symposium featured a community showcase of AI projects across campus, including intersectional feminist approaches to AI, AI readiness transformation frameworks, and research on AI's impact on student writing — demonstrating how liberal arts institutions are integrating AI ethics across disciplines

Science|Business — Max Planck Society

Max Planck Society Launches Germany's First Cross-University Graduate School for Biomedical AI — PhD Applications Open September 2026

🧬
  • The Max Planck Society has selected the Max Planck School of Biomedical Artificial Intelligence as its fourth Max Planck School — the first initiative to systematically bring together universities and non-university institutes across Germany in a graduate programme focused on AI
  • Max Planck Society President Patrick Cramer: "The chosen subject focus addresses a particular challenge: the interface between biomedicine and artificial intelligence. This holds enormous potential for better understanding the causes of health and disease and for generating innovations in diagnostics and therapy"
  • The selection committee praised the concept's "clear scientific focus and coherent approach to the qualification of doctoral candidates" — noting its credible plans for infrastructure, computing resources, supervision model, curriculum, and an internationally visible network of fellows
  • The new School will join three existing Max Planck Schools (Cognition, Matter to Life, and Photonics) and aims to launch its first call for PhD applications on September 1, 2026 — funded through the Max Planck Society and the Dieter Schwarz Foundation
  • External reviewers included leading AI researchers from University College London, the University of Oxford's DeepMind Chair, and the Universidad Politécnica de Madrid — reflecting the international caliber of the programme's scientific advisory structure
  • The initiative reflects a broader European trend: as AI reshapes medicine and life sciences, universities are creating dedicated interdisciplinary graduate programmes that bridge technical AI expertise with domain knowledge in health, biology, and clinical research

Key Themes Emerging

AI Workforce Preparation Is Now a National Security Issue

The Bipartisan Policy Center commission and the AI+Education Summit both frame AI workforce preparation not merely as an education challenge but as a matter of national competitiveness and security. With 70% of job skills expected to change by 2030 and Senator Warner pointing to rising graduate unemployment and declining entry-level hiring, the urgency has moved from academic debate to bipartisan legislative action. The proposed Economy of the Future Commission Act and the Department of Labor's new AI Workforce Hub signal that federal policy is beginning to catch up with the scale of disruption AI is already causing in the labor market.

Universities Are Racing to Launch Dedicated AI Programs

Northwestern's new AI undergraduate major — joining Carnegie Mellon, MIT, and others — reflects a broader wave of universities creating dedicated AI degree programs rather than simply adding AI modules to existing curricula. The Max Planck Society's new School of Biomedical AI in Germany signals that this trend is global, with institutions recognizing that interdisciplinary AI expertise requires purpose-built programs. Crucially, Northwestern's decision to require an ethics course for all AI majors reflects a growing consensus that technical AI education without ethical grounding is insufficient preparation for the real-world deployment of these systems.

AI Is Reshaping How Students Choose — and Question — College

The EAB survey's finding that 46% of high school students now use AI to search for colleges — and that 39% are reconsidering college altogether because of AI — represents a fundamental shift in the higher education pipeline. AI is not just changing what students learn; it is changing whether they believe a college degree is worth pursuing. For enrollment managers, this means competing in a new information environment where AI-generated summaries shape first impressions before colleges ever make direct contact. The 29-percentage-point engagement boost from hyper-personalized outreach suggests that authenticity and relevance, not volume, are the new currency of college recruitment.

Student AI Adoption Is Universal, But Confidence and Trust Are Not

The Digital Education Council's finding that 92% of students use AI but 65% fear it makes learning too shallow captures the central paradox of AI in education: students are using tools they don't fully trust, for purposes they're not sure are educationally sound, in institutions that haven't yet provided adequate guidance. The 56% who worry about data privacy and the 56% who question the fairness of AI grading point to governance gaps that institutions must urgently address. Widespread adoption without institutional support and clear ethical frameworks is not a success story — it is a risk management failure waiting to happen.

K-12 Policy Is Bifurcating Between Restriction and Innovation

The contrast between New York City's plan to open an AI-focused high school and Utah's recent law banning unauthorized student AI use illustrates a deepening bifurcation in K-12 AI policy. Some jurisdictions are racing to build AI-native learning environments; others are legislating restrictions while they figure out the risks. The Bipartisan Policy Center commission's call for a federal K-12 scorecard and national standards suggests that this patchwork of state and local responses is inadequate for the scale of disruption AI is bringing. The challenge for policymakers is creating frameworks flexible enough to enable innovation while providing the guardrails that parents, educators, and students are asking for.

Liberal Arts and Ethics Are Claiming Their Place in AI Education

Barnard College's "Responsible AI and the Liberal Arts" symposium and Northwestern's mandatory AI ethics course both signal a significant shift: the conversation about AI in education is no longer dominated solely by computer science departments. Liberal arts institutions are asserting that questions of transparency, fairness, accountability, and human connection are not add-ons to AI education — they are its foundation. As AI systems become more capable and more embedded in social, professional, and emotional life, the ability to think critically about their design, deployment, and impact is becoming as important as the ability to build them.
