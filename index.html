RAND Corporation — American Youth Panel Study

RAND Study: Student AI Homework Use Jumps to 62% — But 67% Worry It Harms Critical Thinking

62%
  • Between May and December 2025, the percentage of middle school, high school, and college students using AI for homework rose sharply from 48% to 62%, driven largely by younger students in K-12
  • 67% of students now believe AI use for schoolwork harms critical thinking skills — up from 54% earlier in 2025 — with concern even higher (78%) among students who do not personally use AI
  • ChatGPT remains the dominant tool at 53% usage, but Google Gemini more than doubled its share between May and December 2025, rising to 28% — signaling a rapidly shifting competitive landscape
  • Students most often use AI to get better explanations of assignments (38%), brainstorm ideas (35%), look up facts (33%), and draft or revise writing (33%) — with older students more likely to use AI for all purposes
  • Only about one-third of students report their school has a schoolwide AI policy; many — especially college students — say rules vary dramatically by teacher, creating widespread confusion
  • Heather Schwartz, RAND vice president: "Students are already using these tools. The question is whether schools can help them use AI in ways that deepen, rather than erode, their critical thinking" — study supported by the Gates Foundation

Education Week — National Parents Union Survey

8 in 10 Parents Want More AI Guardrails for Their Children, New Survey Finds

80%
  • A new Echelon Insights survey conducted on behalf of the National Parents Union finds 8 in 10 parents want more guardrails on AI for their children in K-12 schools
  • 86% of parents want AI chatbots to provide pop-up warnings before displaying sensitive topics related to violence, self-harm, or abuse; 85% want alerts if a child discusses anything harmful or illegal
  • 79% of parents say AI platforms should require parental permission before a minor can use the tool — a significant majority that may shape future federal and state legislation
  • 52% of parents feel there are equal benefits and downsides to AI tools used in K-12, while 40% say they know enough about AI to evaluate its use in their child's school
  • The House Energy and Commerce Committee recently advanced three bills that would protect minors' data privacy, flag harmful online activity, and require online platforms to implement safeguards
  • The federal government continues to push for AI expansion in education, with the Trump administration's executive order directing AI integration across K-12 — creating tension with parent concerns about safety and oversight

Key Themes Emerging

Student AI Use Surges — But So Does Self-Doubt About Learning

The RAND study released today reveals a striking paradox: as student AI use for homework climbs to 62%, the share of students who believe AI harms their critical thinking has risen even faster — from 54% to 67% in just seven months. Students are not naive about the trade-offs; they are using AI while simultaneously worrying about what it is doing to their ability to think independently. This self-awareness, combined with the finding that only one-third of schools have clear AI policies, points to an urgent need for structured guidance rather than blanket prohibition or uncritical adoption.

Gen Z Job Market Crisis Deepens as AI Agents Scale

ServiceNow CEO Bill McDermott's warning that Gen Z graduate unemployment could reach the mid-30s within two years is not an outlier prediction — it aligns with data showing 58% of 2024–2025 graduates still searching for their first job, a 32% drop in U.S. job postings since ChatGPT's launch, and the February 2026 economy shedding 92,000 jobs. The convergence of AI agent deployment, reduced entry-level hiring, and a generation entering the workforce with primarily "codifiable knowledge" creates a structural challenge that universities must address through curriculum redesign, not just career services.

AI Degree Programs Proliferate at Every Level

The simultaneous launch of AI majors at Northwestern, Rose-Hulman, and dozens of other institutions — alongside UGA's MSAI program ranking No. 17 nationally — reflects a higher education sector racing to build academic infrastructure for AI at scale. The World Economic Forum's projection of 11 million new AI and data science jobs provides the economic rationale, but the more significant development is the emphasis on responsible AI, safety guardrails, and ethical deployment embedded in these new programs. Universities are no longer just training AI users — they are training AI architects and governance professionals.

Teacher Training Becomes a National Priority

The convergence of Google's 6-million-teacher AI literacy initiative, the $23 million National Academy for AI Instruction backed by Anthropic, Microsoft, and OpenAI, and congressional testimony demanding federal guardrails signals that teacher AI training has moved from a peripheral concern to a central policy priority. With 60% of K-12 teachers already using AI tools — nearly double the rate of two years ago — and only half having received any formal training, the gap between adoption and preparation is widening. The question is no longer whether teachers will use AI, but whether institutions will support them in using it responsibly.

K-12 Policy Shifts from Prohibition to Structured Integration

The wave of formal AI policies being adopted by school boards — from Prince Edward County, Virginia to Bucks County, Pennsylvania — reflects a national maturation of K-12 AI governance. Districts are moving away from blanket bans and ad hoc teacher decisions toward structured frameworks covering acceptable use, academic integrity, data privacy, and grade-level distinctions. The parent survey finding that 80% want more guardrails, combined with the RAND finding that only one-third of schools have clear policies, creates both the political mandate and the practical urgency for districts to act in the 2026–2027 school year.

Skills Gap Drives Corporate AI Certification Race

Google's launch of the AI Professional Certificate on Coursera — targeting the gap between 70% of managers who want AI-trained workers and only 14% of employees who have received any training — reflects a broader corporate scramble to build AI literacy at scale. With AI spending projected to rise 44% in 2026 while training budgets grow just 5%, the private sector is increasingly stepping in to fill the gap that traditional education has not yet closed. The "Learn by Doing" model, accessible in under 10 hours without coding skills, represents a new paradigm for rapid workforce upskilling that bypasses traditional degree pathways.
