Burning Glass Institute / aiEDU

New National Research: 70% of K–12 Learning Objectives Must Be Taught Differently in the Age of AI

70%
  • The Burning Glass Institute and AI Education Project (aiEDU) released "Which Skills Matter Now?" — one of the most comprehensive analyses of how AI is reshaping K–12 education
  • Report finds 70% of K–12 learning objectives will need to be taught differently as AI automates routine and procedural tasks in the workforce
  • AI is not simply changing tools — it is fundamentally redefining what mastery looks like; employers now demand judgment, problem-solving, and collaboration with AI
  • Skills like writing, mathematical reasoning, and research are becoming harder, not easier — students must learn to direct, evaluate, and challenge AI output
  • Framework identifies competencies to be deepened, transformed, sharpened, or applied differently: ethical reasoning, creative expression, and human-centered problem solving are growing in importance
  • Report calls for rethinking how schools prioritize and assess skills, investing in teacher professional learning, and expanding access to AI tools and authentic learning opportunities
  • aiEDU has reached more than 19,000 educators and 1.14 million students since 2019; CEO warns "AI readiness is now a national priority"

54%

Cobb Courier / University of Massachusetts Boston

The Greatest Risk of AI in Higher Education Isn't Cheating — It's the Erosion of Learning Itself

⚠️
  • Prof. Nir Eisikovits and Jacob Burley of UMass Boston argue the real danger of AI in higher education is "cognitive offloading" — not academic dishonesty
  • Students, instructors, and researchers all use AI tools: for summarizing readings, building syllabuses, writing code, and scanning literature — blurring the line between assistance and replacement
  • Colleges face a fundamental choice: shape AI's impact on learning, or be redefined by it — with the enduring value of a college degree itself at stake
  • Rising AI use is forcing universities to revisit what students should learn, how to measure it, and whether traditional degree structures remain relevant
  • Researchers describe a "passenger mode" phenomenon: students physically present in class but cognitively disengaged, outsourcing the intellectual work that drives development
  • AI literacy programs are replacing outright bans at most institutions in 2026, but literacy is increasingly becoming a legal mandate, not just a suggestion
  • Experts call for a "human-centered AI shift" — prioritizing authentic learning experiences that AI cannot replicate over efficiency gains that hollow out education

Key Themes Emerging

Redefining What Students Must Learn

The Burning Glass Institute's landmark report finds 70% of K–12 learning objectives need to be taught differently. As AI automates routine tasks, skills like ethical reasoning, creative expression, and human-centered problem solving are growing in importance — not declining.

Teen AI Usage Outpaces School Policy

Pew Research confirms 54% of U.S. teens use AI for schoolwork, yet most schools are still crafting policies. With 59% of teens reporting AI cheating is routine, districts like New York City are racing to develop guidelines that balance safeguards with productive AI use.

Universities Racing to Integrate AI

Georgetown University's campus-wide Google Gemini rollout and Stevens Institute's new AI bachelor's degree reflect a broader wave of higher education institutions embedding AI into curricula, programs, and infrastructure — though faculty remain divided on pace and approach.

Entry-Level Job Pipeline Under Threat

AI is eliminating entry-level roles faster than new graduates can absorb them. With 42% of recent grads underemployed and 46% of employers citing AI as a factor in reduced hiring, experts warn of a long-term leadership pipeline crisis as the traditional career ladder loses its bottom rungs.

Cognitive Erosion vs. Cognitive Augmentation

Researchers at UMass Boston and elsewhere argue the greatest risk of AI in education is not cheating — it is the erosion of learning itself through cognitive offloading. The debate is shifting from "how to prevent AI use" to "how to ensure AI augments rather than replaces genuine intellectual development."

AI Literacy Becoming a Legal Mandate

By 2026, outright AI bans in schools have largely been replaced by AI literacy programs. But literacy is no longer just a suggestion — it is becoming a legal mandate in several states. The "human-centered AI shift" is redefining what it means to be an educated person in the age of generative AI.
