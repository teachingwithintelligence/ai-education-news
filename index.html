OpenAI / Learning Lab

OpenAI Launches Learning Outcomes Measurement Suite to Track AI's Long-Term Impact on Student Cognition

🔬
  • OpenAI introduces the Learning Outcomes Measurement Suite (LOMS) — the first standardized framework for longitudinal studies measuring how AI shapes student learning, cognition, and academic performance over time
  • LOMS is grounded in three core signals: how the model behaves, how learners respond, and what measurable cognitive outcomes result — tracking changes at both individual and cohort levels
  • The suite includes learning interaction classifiers that automatically detect "learning moments" in real de-identified interactions, and longitudinal graders that track engagement, persistence, and metacognitive strategies over time
  • Standardized cognitive instruments delivered via ChatGPT will establish baselines and measure changes in critical thinking, creativity, and memory — directly addressing concerns about AI's effect on student cognition
  • LOMS will track five key capability dimensions: Autonomous Motivation, Productive Engagement, Task Persistence, Metacognition, and Recall — providing educators with structured dashboards of how outcomes shift across cohorts
  • The initiative directly responds to growing concerns — including MIT's EEG study — that AI tools may impair cognitive development, by creating a rigorous, evidence-based mechanism to measure and improve AI's pedagogical impact
  • OpenAI's Learning Lab is now accepting institutional partners to participate in longitudinal studies; all data will be de-identified to protect student privacy

Inside Higher Ed

In-Person Classes Are No Longer Safe From AI Cheating — ASU Study Finds 45% of Course Points Vulnerable

45%
  • A preliminary audit of 21 in-person ASU biology courses finds that on average, 45% of course points can be easily earned using digital cheating methods, including AI — shattering the assumption that physical classrooms are protected
  • Students are exploiting electronic clickers to collect participation points without attending class; sharing answers via group chats; using smart glasses and watches to photograph exam questions; and using agentic AI browsers to complete entire online assignments
  • ASU biology professor Sara Brownell: "We were measuring student effort by class attendance or having them watch a video and answer questions — that was before every single student had access to ChatGPT at our university"
  • Students cite the pressure of competing against peers who cheat: "If a student is competing against another student who is cheating, they almost feel like they have to cheat to keep up"
  • 59% of U.S. teens say AI cheating happens at least somewhat often at their school; a third say it happens extremely or very often, per a new Pew Research Center survey of teens ages 13–17
  • Brownell recommends in-person, pencil-and-paper exams and active verification of participation as the most effective near-term countermeasures
  • The findings underscore the urgency of rethinking assessment design across all course formats as agentic AI tools become capable of completing entire courses autonomously

Penn GSE / Google.org

Penn GSE Expands K–12 AI Professional Development Program to 6 Districts Across PA, NJ & DE — Backed by $1M Google.org Grant

🏫
  • The University of Pennsylvania Graduate School of Education announces the expansion of its Pioneering AI in School Systems (PASS) program to six school districts and intermediate units across Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and Delaware
  • A $1 million Google.org investment funds the expansion, offered at no cost to participating districts — including Allentown PA, Neshaminy PA, Paterson NJ, and Christina DE, plus two intermediate units serving 33 additional districts
  • PASS is a three-tier professional development model supporting district leaders, school leaders, and classroom educators in building AI strategy, policy, and ethical classroom practices aligned across all roles
  • The program addresses the most urgent gap in AI education: the majority of educators using AI have received no formal training, and most districts lack coherent AI governance frameworks
  • Penn GSE Dean Katharine Strunk: "We are helping districts build the knowledge and confidence to use AI in ways that strengthen teaching and learning — creating thoughtful, human-centered approaches to AI in classrooms and communities"
  • Penn GSE is simultaneously launching a new online master's degree focused on AI in education, hiring new AI faculty, and conducting vanguard research on both the promise and risks of AI in school systems
  • The PASS expansion is designed to serve as a national model for scalable, district-based AI professional learning — with the pilot's success in Philadelphia providing a proven framework for replication

Key Themes Emerging

Measuring What AI Actually Does to Learning

OpenAI's launch of the Learning Outcomes Measurement Suite marks a pivotal shift: for the first time, there is a standardized framework to measure not just whether students use AI, but what it does to their cognition, motivation, and metacognition over time. This directly confronts the central unresolved question in AI education — whether tools that improve short-term performance are building or eroding the deeper capabilities students need for lifelong learning. The answer will shape policy for a generation.

The Classroom Is No Longer a Cheat-Free Zone

The ASU audit's finding that 45% of in-person course points are vulnerable to digital cheating — combined with Pew's data that 59% of teens say AI cheating happens at least somewhat often at their school — dismantles the assumption that physical presence ensures academic integrity. As agentic AI browsers become capable of completing entire courses autonomously, the education system faces a fundamental reckoning: not just with how to detect cheating, but with whether current assessment models are still fit for purpose.

Teacher Training Is the National Emergency

Penn GSE's $1M Google.org-backed expansion of its PASS program to six districts across three states reflects a growing recognition that teacher training — not technology — is the binding constraint on responsible AI integration. With the majority of educators using AI without formal training, and most districts lacking coherent governance frameworks, the gap between AI adoption and educator preparedness has become a systemic risk. PASS represents one of the most rigorous attempts to build that capacity at scale.

Universities Race to Build AI Pipelines

LSU's announcement of Louisiana's first AI bachelor's degree — alongside a first-in-the-nation digital twin certificate, a high school dual enrollment AI course, and an industry-sponsored AI capstone — illustrates the accelerating pace at which universities are redesigning their entire academic portfolio around AI. From UConn's agentic AI micro-credential for engineers to Penn GSE's new online master's in AI education, institutions are no longer asking whether to integrate AI — they are competing to define what that integration looks like.

The U.S. Needs a National AI Education Strategy

Digital Promise CEO Jean-Claude Brizard's Fast Company essay crystallizes a growing consensus: the U.S. is not behind on AI technology in education — it is behind on strategic coherence. While Shanghai treats AI as national educational infrastructure and other countries execute coordinated deployment at scale, the U.S. K-12 system remains fragmented, reactive, and fixated on cheating rather than opportunity. The call for governors and state leaders to build nested policy-practice alignment is the most direct challenge yet to the status quo.

The Entry-Level Job Market Has Structurally Changed

The convergence of a 35% drop in entry-level job postings, a 54,000-layoff toll tied to AI in 2025, and only 30% of graduates landing field-relevant jobs signals that the traditional college-to-career pipeline has been structurally disrupted. Universities that adapt — embedding AI fluency, early career engagement, and industry partnerships into the curriculum — are achieving strong graduate outcomes. Those that do not are sending students into a job market that no longer has room for the unprepared.
