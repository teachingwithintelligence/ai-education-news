Northwestern Engineering / McCormick School of Engineering

Northwestern Engineering Announces New AI Major — The First of Its Kind at a Top-10 Engineering School, Launching Fall 2026

🎓
  • Northwestern University's McCormick School of Engineering announces a new undergraduate AI major launching Fall 2026 — the first program in McCormick's new Bachelor of Science in Engineering (BSE) degree, administered by the Department of Computer Science
  • The curriculum spans the full breadth of AI: machine learning, natural language processing, computer vision, knowledge representation and reasoning, robotics, human-computer interaction, and AI ethics — preparing graduates to deploy AI systems using GPUs and evaluate their societal impact
  • Program Director Sara Owsley Sood: "The AI industry demands specific technical skills that differ from the core CS curriculum...This new major will span the breadth of AI, while also preparing students to deploy AI systems, build systems that interact with users, and consider their impact (ethics)"
  • CS Chair Samir Khuller: "Our new undergraduate major is designed to bridge the gap between academic computer science and applied AI. Modern AI systems, which adapt and learn strategies over time, are built on decades of evolving CS knowledge"
  • Any Northwestern student — regardless of school — may add AI as a second major while remaining in their current program, broadening access beyond engineering students to business, law, medicine, and the humanities
  • Northwestern joins a national wave of top universities building structured AI education portfolios — including Brown, Penn GSE, LSU, and the University of Northern Iowa — as demand for AI-literate graduates reaches record levels across every industry sector
  • The program's emphasis on ethics, user-centered design, and societal impact reflects a growing consensus that technical AI training without humanistic grounding produces graduates ill-equipped for the governance and trust challenges that define AI deployment in 2026

Education Week / Securly

Real-Time Data Reveals 1 in 5 Student AI Interactions on School Devices Involve Cheating, Self-Harm, or Bullying

⚠️
  • Securly analyzed nearly 1.2 million student AI interactions across 1,300+ school districts (Dec. 1, 2025 – Feb. 20, 2026), providing the most comprehensive real-time picture of how students actually use AI on school-issued technology
  • Roughly 1 in 5 (20%) student AI interactions involved cheating, self-harm, bullying, or other problematic behaviors — a rate five times higher than the 0.4% unsafe rate detected in regular student internet searches
  • 1 in 50 interactions (2%) were flagged as potential red flags for violence, cyberbullying, or self-harm — representing more than 24,000 total queries that required district-level intervention
  • Among the most alarming cases: one student asked an AI to draft an email to their mother explaining suicidal thoughts; another conducted searches on nerve locations before asking an AI how to commit suicide — both were unmasked and district officials were notified
  • The data challenges the assumption that AI misuse in schools is primarily an academic integrity issue — the mental health and safety dimensions of student AI access are emerging as an equally urgent concern for administrators and counselors
  • The findings arrive as fewer than 40% of higher education institutions have formal AI acceptable-use policies (EDUCAUSE, 2025), and as K-12 districts are still in early stages of developing frameworks for monitoring and governing student AI use

  • OpenAI's analysis finds that structured institutional access — not self-directed use — is what moves students from basic to advanced AI capability: ChatGPT Edu users show the largest gains in analysis, calculation, and learning tasks compared to free users

Education Week Research Center

Survey: Nearly 8 in 10 Educators Say High Schoolers Are Now Receiving AI Literacy Lessons — But a K-12 Gap Remains

79%
  • A nationally representative survey of 499 teachers, principals, and district leaders (December–January) finds that nearly 8 in 10 (79%) educators say high school students in their district are receiving lessons on what AI is and how to use it responsibly
  • 73% say the same for middle school students in grades 6–8 — but the gap between secondary and primary AI literacy education points to "the need to develop what we would call AI literacy learning pathways" that are cumulative, competency-based, and consistent across a student's entire K-12 journey
  • Microsoft's 2025 AI in Education report found that 76% of global education leaders view AI literacy as an essential component of basic education for every student — placing it alongside reading and mathematics as a foundational skill
  • The U.S. Department of Education has proposed including AI literacy in its grant-funding priorities, signaling a potential federal investment in standardizing AI education across school districts
  • Kelly Mills, senior director of powerful learning research at Digital Promise: AI literacy needs to be "cumulative, competency-based, and consistent across classrooms" — not a one-time lesson or elective add-on, but a structured progression built into every grade level
  • The survey data arrives as EdWeek's companion reporting shows 1 in 5 student AI interactions on school devices involve problematic behavior — underscoring that AI literacy is not just about capability, but about judgment, safety, and ethical use

Memphis Commercial Appeal — University of Memphis

University of Memphis Receives $4M Grant to Reinvent Student Assessment for the AI Age — 85% of Students Already Using AI for Coursework

$4M
  • A University of Memphis professor is leading a landmark $4 million research project to develop new student assessment frameworks for the age of AI — addressing one of higher education's most urgent and unresolved challenges
  • The central question driving the research: how should college professors evaluate their students' learning when AI models like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are available with a simple Google search and 85% of students already report using AI in some way for coursework (Inside Higher Ed/Generation Lab poll)
  • Traditional assessment methods — essays, problem sets, take-home exams — were designed for a pre-AI world; the Memphis project aims to develop evidence-based alternatives that can reliably distinguish student learning from AI-assisted output
  • The project joins a growing body of institutional responses to the assessment crisis: NYU's handwritten notebook approach, oral examinations, in-class demonstrations, and portfolio-based evaluations are all gaining traction as educators seek assessment tasks that remain meaningful when AI can complete most conventional ones
  • The $4M investment signals that foundations and funders are beginning to treat AI-age assessment as a research priority — not just a pedagogical inconvenience — with implications for accreditation, credentialing, and the fundamental purpose of higher education evaluation

$500K

Key Themes Emerging

Elite Universities Are Racing to Build Dedicated AI Degree Programs

Northwestern Engineering's announcement of a new undergraduate AI major — the first in its new BSE degree — is the latest signal that top-tier universities are moving beyond AI electives and concentrations toward full, standalone degree programs. The Northwestern major's emphasis on ethics, HCI, and societal impact alongside technical training reflects a maturing understanding of what AI education requires. As more elite institutions build these programs, the question shifts from whether to offer an AI major to what distinguishes a rigorous AI degree from a credential-washed curriculum.

Student AI Safety Is an Emerging Crisis That Extends Far Beyond Cheating

Securly's real-time data from 1.2 million student AI interactions is a watershed finding: 1 in 5 student AI queries on school devices involve problematic behavior, and 1 in 50 raise serious safety concerns including self-harm and violence. This reframes the conversation about AI in schools. The dominant policy debate has focused on academic integrity — whether students are using AI to cheat. The Securly data reveals that mental health, cyberbullying, and student safety are equally urgent dimensions of the student AI challenge, and that schools need monitoring and intervention frameworks, not just acceptable-use policies.

Gen Z Is Rewriting the Education-to-Career Equation in Real Time

A 70% surge in apprenticeship applications since 2022, a 28% jump in community college certificate enrollment in four years, and bachelor's degree growth of less than 1% in fall 2025 — these numbers tell a coherent story. Gen Z is making rational, evidence-based decisions about education and career in an era of AI-driven white-collar disruption. The skilled trades offer physical-presence job security, strong wages, and clear career pathways at a fraction of the cost of a four-year degree. Educators and policymakers who dismiss this shift as a retreat from education are misreading a generation that is adapting faster than the institutions designed to serve them.

AI Literacy Is Becoming a Universal K-12 Expectation — But Implementation Lags

The EdWeek survey finding that 79% of educators report high school students receiving AI literacy lessons is a remarkable shift from just two years ago, when AI education was largely confined to computer science electives. But the gap between secondary and primary AI literacy, and the absence of cumulative, competency-based learning pathways, means that most students are receiving fragmented exposure rather than systematic preparation. The U.S. Department of Education's proposal to include AI literacy in grant-funding priorities could accelerate standardization — but without clear frameworks, the risk is that "AI literacy" becomes a label applied to widely varying quality of instruction.

Assessment Is the Unresolved Core Problem of AI in Higher Education

The University of Memphis's $4 million research grant to develop new assessment frameworks for the AI age is an acknowledgment that higher education has not yet solved its most fundamental AI challenge. With 85% of college students using AI for coursework, the traditional essay, problem set, and take-home exam are no longer reliable measures of student learning. The Memphis project joins a growing field of institutional experimentation — handwritten exams, oral assessments, portfolio evaluations — but the absence of scalable, evidence-based alternatives means that assessment integrity remains the most consequential unresolved question in AI-era education.

State Legislatures Are Becoming the Primary Arena for AI Education Governance

With 28 states advancing K-12 education legislation in February 2026 alone — including Florida's SB 7036 authorizing AI as a federally funded instructional tool — the locus of AI education governance is clearly at the state level. Utah's AI regulatory sandbox, Illinois's new K-12 AI guidance, and Florida's Title I AI authorization represent dramatically different approaches, creating a patchwork of frameworks that will shape student AI experiences very differently depending on geography. The absence of federal AI education standards means that state-level decisions made in 2026 will define the AI education landscape for the next decade.
