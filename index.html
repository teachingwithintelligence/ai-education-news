University World News / Coursera

International Study: 95%+ of Higher Ed Uses AI, But 56% Fear Universities Are Unprepared

56%
  • Coursera survey of 4,200 educators and students across India, Mexico, the U.S., UK, and Saudi Arabia finds more than 95% now use AI tools — but a striking 56% fear higher education is unprepared to handle the technology
  • Only 26% of academics say their university has an AI use policy; 65% believe unregulated AI could undermine the value of degrees — a governance crisis hiding beneath high adoption rates
  • 80% of students report grade improvements since using AI, and 83% believe AI is having a positive impact on higher education — yet only 27% of faculty feel confident in their ability to detect AI-generated content
  • 71% of faculty use AI often or always, but only 25% are confident in their and their peers' ability to use AI to advantage in the classroom — revealing a deep confidence gap beneath surface-level adoption
  • 36% of students use AI to complete more than half of their study tasks; top uses include research (51%), writing (49%), and test preparation (46%) — with 44% using AI for time management, an unexpected finding
  • Coursera reports a 195% rise in GenAI course enrollments globally, signaling that students are actively seeking structured AI education outside their institutions
  • Report recommends four priorities for university leaders: invest in AI tools for faculty, provide targeted AI training, promote best practices, and identify AI champions to lead institutional change

USA Today / EssayShark Study

Global Study: 62% of Students Use AI Weekly — But 71% of Educators Received Zero Formal Training

71%
  • Independent study of 3,200 students and 1,150 educators across the U.S., China, Indonesia, Netherlands, and UK finds AI adoption in education is now widespread — but institutional policy and training have not kept pace
  • 62% of students use generative AI tools at least weekly for academic support: research assistance (69%), concept explanation (48%), and grammar review (41%) are the top applications
  • 58% of K-12 educators incorporate AI into lesson planning or classroom activities, while only 27% of higher education faculty report regular use — a striking gap between school levels
  • 71% of educators who use AI tools report receiving no formal institutional training prior to implementation — adoption is largely self-directed, driven by student usage patterns rather than district or university policy
  • Classrooms using structured AI-supported adaptive learning systems saw 18% improvement in assignment completion rates and 24% improvement in student-reported study efficiency compared to non-AI classrooms
  • Regional divide: in China and Indonesia, 75%+ view AI as having a net positive impact on learning; in the U.S. and Netherlands, fewer than 45% share that view — concerns center on academic integrity and critical thinking
  • Lead researcher Frederick Poche: "Our data suggests that schools with structured guidance and teacher training see more consistent outcomes than those where implementation is informal"

Fox32 Chicago

Chicago's Alpha School Opens This Fall With AI Tutors at Its Core — $55K Tuition Sparks Equity Debate

🤖
  • Alpha School — a private Pre-K through 8th grade model already operating in Austin, Miami, and New York — is opening in Chicago's Lakeshore East neighborhood this fall, with tuition set at $55,000 per year for approximately 100 students
  • The model replaces traditional classroom instruction with AI tutors for two hours of daily academic work, while human "guides" focus entirely on motivation, mentorship, and connecting students to their passions and talents
  • Co-founder Mackenzie Price: "Our AI tutor is able to very precisely pinpoint where a student is academically and then help put them into the lessons that are at their pace and their level" — distinguishing their proprietary system from general-purpose chatbots
  • DePaul University professor An Chih Cheng warns: "The computer does not think... AI models still have serious problems. These teaching materials are not necessarily accurate" — raising concerns about accuracy, privacy, and critical thinking development
  • Chicago Public Schools (CPS) took the opposite approach: on December 22, 2025, CPS officially blocked all unapproved third-party AI products from the CPS network to protect student data privacy under FERPA, COPPA, and SOPPA
  • CPS has established an AI Steering Committee, a quarterly-updated AI Guidebook, and a vetted-tools-only policy — representing a cautious, governance-first approach to AI in public education
  • The contrast between Alpha School's AI-first model and CPS's governance-first approach encapsulates the central tension in K-12 AI adoption: personalization and efficiency vs. equity, accuracy, and data privacy

Education Week / JED Foundation

New Reports: Teens Are Turning to AI for Mental Health Support — Often Instead of Professional Help

46%
  • Two new reports from the JED Foundation, drawing on surveys of 1,340 people ages 13–24, reveal that 46% of teens and young adults use generative AI daily or weekly — with a growing share turning to it for emotional and mental health support
  • 10% of teens and young adults use AI daily or weekly specifically to talk through something emotional or for mental health support — and Black youths are three times more likely (18%) than white youths (6%) to use AI for this purpose
  • 69% of teens using GenAI for mental health are relying on general-purpose tools not designed for that purpose; 37% use GenAI exclusively for mental health support rather than seeking professional help — raising serious safety concerns
  • 59% of those using GenAI for mental health said the technology encouraged them to seek professional help or crisis services — suggesting AI can serve as a bridge to care when designed thoughtfully
  • 21% of teens and young adults have never used GenAI, citing ethical, autonomy, privacy, or environmental impact concerns — a significant and often overlooked segment of the youth population
  • 47% of LGBTQ+ respondents either never used AI or identify as "low-use anxious skeptics" — their disengagement shaped by concerns around safety, control, trust, and long-term impact
  • Dr. Laura Erickson-Schroth (JED Foundation): "Many young people are turning to general-purpose AI tools to address mental health concerns, especially when they face barriers to care. These systems were not designed for this purpose and often serve as endpoints rather than pathways to support"

Brown University Launches Human-Centered AI Executive Course — Pairing Ethics and Leadership With Technical Skills

🏛️
  • Brown University's School of Professional Studies is launching "Human-Centered AI: Integrating Technology & Human Potential" on April 14 — a six-session live online executive education course designed for leaders navigating AI-driven organizational change
  • The course complements Brown's existing "Applied AI & Data Science" offering to create a portfolio approach: technical fluency for builders, leadership capability for decision-makers — recognizing that successful AI adoption requires both

  • Designed for executives, managers, and professionals who influence how AI is introduced, governed, and communicated — not just those who build or code AI systems

  • Brown joins a wave of universities — including Penn GSE, LSU, and UConn — racing to build AI education portfolios that serve professionals at every stage of their AI journey, from foundational literacy to advanced leadership
  • The course signals a maturing of the university AI education market: institutions are no longer just launching AI degree programs, but building structured pathways that connect technical skills to real-world organizational impact

Key Themes Emerging

The AI Capability Gap Is the New Literacy Crisis

OpenAI's revelation that even advanced student users operate 90–99% below power-user capability levels reframes the AI education challenge. It is not enough that students use AI — the question is whether they are using it in ways that build genuine capability. The institutions that close this gap, through structured access, certifications, and embedded coursework, will produce graduates with a measurable competitive advantage in an AI-saturated workforce.

Teacher Training Is the Binding Constraint

Two independent studies released this week converge on the same finding: 71% of educators using AI received no formal training, and adoption is largely self-directed. The Coursera survey adds that only 25% of faculty feel confident using AI to advantage in the classroom. Until institutions treat educator AI training as a strategic priority — not an afterthought — the gap between student adoption and institutional readiness will continue to widen, with unpredictable consequences for learning outcomes.

AI-First Schools vs. Governance-First Districts

The contrast between Alpha School's $55,000-per-year AI-tutor model opening in Chicago and Chicago Public Schools' December 2025 decision to block all unapproved AI tools from its network illustrates the deepening divide in K-12 AI strategy. One approach bets on personalization and efficiency; the other prioritizes data privacy, equity, and institutional control. As AI-first private schools expand, the equity implications for public education — and the students who cannot afford $55,000 tuition — become increasingly urgent.

AI as Mental Health Infrastructure — By Default

The JED Foundation's finding that 37% of teens use AI exclusively for mental health support — relying on general-purpose tools not designed for that purpose — reveals a new and largely unaddressed dimension of AI in education. Young people are turning to AI when they face barriers to professional care, and the technology is becoming de facto mental health infrastructure for a generation. Schools and universities have yet to develop policies, training, or partnerships that address this reality responsibly.

Universities Race to Build AI Leadership Pipelines

Brown University's launch of a Human-Centered AI executive course — focused on ethics, governance, and organizational leadership rather than technical skills — signals a new phase in university AI education. Institutions are no longer just building AI degree programs; they are constructing structured portfolios that serve professionals at every stage, from foundational literacy to executive decision-making. The universities that build the most coherent pathways from AI fluency to AI leadership will define the next generation of organizational capability.

AI Governance Has Become Core Educational Infrastructure

Q1 2026 marks the moment AI governance crossed from aspiration to operational necessity in education. Districts and institutions are no longer debating whether to adopt AI — they are building governance frameworks, institution-wide licenses, and formal oversight structures that resemble earlier LMS and ERP rollouts. The Coursera survey's finding that only 26% of universities have an AI use policy underscores how far most institutions still have to go. The accountability era has arrived, and the institutions without governance frameworks are increasingly exposed.
