Education Week — American Federation of Teachers / National Academy for AI Instruction

AFT Launches $23M National Academy for AI Instruction — Training 400,000 Teachers on Agentic AI Tools

400,000
  • The American Federation of Teachers launched the National Academy for AI Instruction — a five-year, $23 million partnership with Anthropic, Microsoft, and OpenAI — with its first training session held March 18, 2026 at UFT headquarters in New York City, bringing together 50 teachers to develop "agentic" AI tools
  • Agentic AI tools are autonomous software systems capable of complex, multi-step reasoning tasks — a significant leap beyond the basic lesson planning and administrative uses that currently dominate teacher AI adoption
  • The share of teachers using AI tools nearly doubled from 2024 to 2025, with 6 in 10 teachers now reporting AI use in their practice — but most still rely on surface-level applications rather than instructional improvement
  • Teachers are developing agents to create individualized education programs for students with disabilities, stress-test lessons for content gaps, monitor student progress in real time, and draft privacy-compliant parent communications — all tailored to district guidelines and union standards
  • AFT President Randi Weingarten: "We're in this race for teachers to get this knowledge of more meaningful use of AI. This will become the most disruptive technology in our time — there is a real demand from educators to learn so that they are in the driver's seat for AI"
  • Data privacy remains a central tension: teachers are navigating what student information can be shared with AI tools, with some switching from ChatGPT to Claude following Anthropic's refusal to allow the Pentagon to use its model for domestic surveillance

Key Themes Emerging

Teacher AI Training Reaches Industrial Scale

The AFT's National Academy for AI Instruction — a $23 million, five-year partnership with Anthropic, Microsoft, and OpenAI — marks a turning point in teacher professional development. By targeting 400,000 teachers and focusing on agentic AI tools rather than basic applications, the initiative signals that the education sector is moving from AI awareness to AI mastery. The academy's teacher-trains-teacher model, combined with the ETS finding that 60% of teachers already use AI daily, suggests that the profession is approaching a tipping point where AI fluency becomes a baseline expectation rather than a differentiator.

University AI Degree Proliferation Accelerates

With 304 U.S. institutions now offering AI degree programs and a new wave of bachelor's degrees launching at RIT, LSU, Northwestern, and UW-Madison, the higher education sector is undergoing a structural transformation. The simultaneous launch of Hofstra's interdisciplinary AI ethics certificate — open to all undergraduates with no prerequisites — reflects a parallel recognition that AI literacy cannot be confined to computer science departments. The emerging consensus is that every graduate, regardless of major, needs both the technical fluency to work with AI and the ethical grounding to deploy it responsibly.

Gen-Z's AI Anxiety Is Reshaping Educational Choices

The Harvard Kennedy School finding that 59% of young adults view AI as a threat to their job prospects — combined with Stanford data showing a 16% employment decline in AI-exposed roles for 22–25 year olds — is producing measurable behavioral change. Vocational college enrollment has surged nearly 20% since 2020, with students pivoting to trades they believe are AI-resistant. This generational anxiety presents a profound challenge for higher education: institutions must simultaneously prepare students to thrive in an AI-transformed economy while honestly addressing the legitimate disruptions that AI is already causing in entry-level job markets.

Global AI Education Strategies Diverge Sharply

The Getting Smart global survey reveals that nations are pursuing fundamentally different philosophies toward AI in education. Estonia and Singapore emphasize enablement — providing tools and teacher training while trusting educators to integrate AI thoughtfully. The UAE and China emphasize mandates — requiring AI instruction for every student at every grade level. Australia and Finland emphasize governance and ethics — building frameworks for responsible use before scaling adoption. The U.S. sits uncomfortably across all three approaches simultaneously, with federal promotion of AI literacy through NSF initiatives coexisting with Heritage Foundation warnings about AI's risks to children.

Entry-Level Work Is Being Redefined, Not Just Eliminated

The WEF analysis of AI's impact on entry-level employment offers a more nuanced picture than the headline 35% job posting decline suggests. Rather than eliminating early-career roles, AI is fundamentally redefining what those roles involve — shifting from task execution to judgment, from routine processing to AI oversight, and from passive learning to active AI-assisted skill acceleration. Organizations that cut junior talent to capture short-term AI efficiency gains risk weakening succession plans, stalling knowledge transfer, and losing the digital-native workers best positioned to accelerate AI adoption. The challenge for universities is to produce graduates who understand not just how to use AI, but when to question it.

AI Ethics Moves from Elective to Core Curriculum

The Heritage Foundation's warning about AI's risks to children — arriving on the same day as Hofstra's launch of an AI ethics certificate and American University's new AI guidelines — reflects a growing consensus that ethical AI literacy cannot be treated as an add-on. As AI becomes embedded in every aspect of education, from lesson planning to student assessment to institutional decision-making, the ability to evaluate AI tools for bias, privacy, and unintended consequences is becoming as fundamental as reading comprehension. The institutions leading this shift are those treating ethics not as a constraint on AI adoption but as a prerequisite for it.
