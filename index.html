U.S. Department of Labor — March 24, 2026

DOL Launches 'Make America AI-Ready' — Free AI Literacy Course Delivered Entirely by Text Message

Free
  • The U.S. Department of Labor launched "Make America AI-Ready," a free AI literacy course accessible to any American worker by texting "READY" to 20202 — designed to reach those without laptops or reliable internet access
  • The 7-day course requires just 10 minutes per day and covers five foundational areas: understanding AI principles, exploring AI uses, directing AI effectively, evaluating AI outputs, and using AI responsibly
  • Developed through a public-private partnership with ed-tech company Arist, the initiative is part of the Trump Administration's America's Talent Strategy and White House AI Action Plan
  • Secretary of Labor Lori Chavez-DeRemer: "The initiative is designed to ensure every American worker has the chance to learn foundational skills so they can benefit from the opportunities that the AI economy presents"
  • The NSF simultaneously announced its TechAccess: AI-Ready America initiative — establishing up to 56 AI-Ready Coordination Hubs across all U.S. states and territories, investing up to $1 million per Hub annually over three years
  • Federal partners include USDA NIFA, the Department of Labor, and the Small Business Administration — targeting AI literacy, small business AI adoption, and hands-on learning pathways including internships and project-based programs

Chalkbeat New York — March 24, 2026

NYC Releases Landmark AI Guidelines for Schools — A Traffic-Light Framework for 1 Million Students

Policy
  • New York City's Education Department released preliminary AI guidance for its 1,700+ schools using a "traffic light" framework: green for approved uses, yellow for cases requiring human oversight, and red for strictly prohibited decisions
  • Green-light uses include brainstorming lesson plans and drafting non-critical communications; yellow-light cases like finding trends in student data or generating translations require a trained professional to review outputs before use with students
  • Red-light prohibitions are absolute: AI may never be used to make decisions about grading, discipline, counseling, special education plans, academic placement, or graduation — and these restrictions are not expected to change in the final June playbook
  • Student personal data can never be entered into unapproved AI tools, and under no circumstances can student information be used to train AI models or generate revenue for third parties
  • All AI tools must pass through the city's Enterprise Request Management Application data privacy vetting process before classroom use — a requirement that applies to all third-party technology vendors
  • The guidance arrives nearly three years after NYC's short-lived ChatGPT school ban; public feedback is being collected through May 8, 2026, with a comprehensive final playbook expected in June

Education Week — RAND Corporation Survey, March 23, 2026

Students Fear AI Is Eroding Their Critical Thinking — RAND Survey Shows Concern Surging to 70%

70%
  • A RAND Corporation survey finds nearly 7 in 10 middle and high school students are now concerned that using AI for schoolwork is eroding their critical thinking skills — a figure that has risen sharply since early 2025
  • Among middle schoolers, concern about AI harming critical thinking climbed from 48% in February 2025 to 68% by December 2025; among high schoolers, it rose from 55% to 65% over the same period
  • 70% of college students also indicated that AI use may be harming their critical thinking — even as homework AI use rose from 30% to 46% among middle schoolers and from 49% to 60% among high schoolers
  • A randomized trial of 1,000 high school students found those using GPT-4 performed much better on practice problems but scored 17% worse once the AI was removed — suggesting AI may create a performance dependency that undermines durable learning
  • Colleges are responding by turning to oral exams to combat AI-assisted cheating, with a growing number of instructors citing the need to assess students' genuine understanding rather than AI-generated responses

New BS

-35%

Key Themes Emerging

Federal AI Literacy Push: From Workforce to Classroom

The simultaneous launch of the DOL's "Make America AI-Ready" text-message course and NSF's TechAccess: AI-Ready America initiative marks the most coordinated federal investment in AI literacy to date. By designing the DOL course to be accessible via text message — no laptop or internet required — the administration is explicitly targeting the workers most at risk of being left behind by AI automation. The NSF's 56-Hub national network signals a long-term structural commitment to AI readiness that extends from K-12 teacher training to small business adoption, representing a significant shift from voluntary guidance to funded infrastructure.

NYC's AI Policy: A Model for Urban School Districts

New York City's traffic-light AI framework — green for approved uses, yellow for human-supervised cases, red for absolute prohibitions — offers the most detailed and operationally specific AI governance model yet released by a major urban school district. The framework's absolute prohibition on AI in grading, discipline, and academic placement decisions reflects a principled commitment to keeping consequential decisions in human hands. As the largest school system in the United States, NYC's approach will likely influence how other cities and states develop their own AI policies in the months ahead, particularly as the final comprehensive playbook is expected in June 2026.

Student Critical Thinking Anxiety: A Warning Schools Cannot Ignore

The RAND finding that 70% of college students and nearly as many K-12 students fear AI is eroding their critical thinking is not merely a data point — it is a warning signal from the students themselves that the current model of AI integration is not serving their development. The randomized trial showing 17% worse performance once AI is removed underscores the risk of creating AI-dependent learners who cannot perform independently. Schools that treat this anxiety as a reason to restrict AI entirely will lose the battle; those that use it as a design principle — building AI integration around strengthening rather than replacing cognitive skills — will produce graduates genuinely prepared for an AI-saturated world.

University AI Degree Wave: Depth vs. Credentialing Theater

The simultaneous launch of new AI bachelor's degrees at LSU, RIT, Eastern University, and Arkansas Tech — alongside Georgetown's AI certificate and Tufts' new AI minors — signals that higher education is undergoing a structural transformation in how it defines academic preparation for an AI-transformed economy. What distinguishes the strongest programs, like LSU's 20-course curriculum built around building AI systems rather than using them, is a commitment to depth, ethics, and engineering rigor that goes beyond the current generative AI hype cycle. As AI credentials multiply, the critical question becomes how employers and students will distinguish substantive preparation from credentialing theater.

Entry-Level Job Market: Redefinition, Not Just Elimination

The 35% drop in entry-level job postings over 18 months is alarming, but the more nuanced story is how the nature of early-career work is being fundamentally redefined. New graduates are increasingly expected to make judgment calls, review AI outputs, and surface insights for senior teams — rather than executing the routine tasks that AI now handles. The 68% of Gen Z workers who say AI has made the job search more competitive are right to be concerned, but the employers hiring 25,000 fresh graduates despite AI adoption are signaling that digital natives with genuine AI fluency — not just AI familiarity — remain in high demand.

Teacher Training Gap: The Most Urgent Unresolved Problem in K-12 AI

The NSF's $11 million investment in K-12 AI teacher professional development addresses what may be the most urgent unresolved problem in AI education: 68% of urban teachers have received no AI training, even as 83% already use generative AI tools for school-related activities. Teachers cannot guide students through the ethical, pedagogical, and cognitive dimensions of AI use if they have not developed those frameworks themselves. Idaho National Laboratory's hands-on workshops and the AFT's AI Academy represent promising models, but scaling sustained, human-centered professional development to every district in the country will require the kind of coordinated federal infrastructure that NSF's Coordination Hub network is designed to provide.
