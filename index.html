U.S. Department of Labor — Make America AI-Ready Initiative

DOL Launches "Make America AI-Ready" — Free AI Literacy Course Delivered Entirely by Text Message, Accessible to All Americans

Text "READY"
  • The U.S. Department of Labor launched "Make America AI-Ready," a free AI literacy course accessible to any American simply by texting "READY" to 20202 — designed to be as inclusive as possible, including for those without laptops or reliable internet access
  • The course delivers bite-sized learning content and daily challenges entirely over text message, completable in seven days at just 10 minutes per day — developed through a public-private partnership between the Labor Department and education technology company Arist
  • Content aligns with DOL's AI Literacy Framework across five foundational areas: understanding AI principles, exploring AI uses, directing AI effectively, evaluating AI outputs, and using AI responsibly — providing a structured foundation for workforce readiness
  • Secretary of Labor Lori Chavez-DeRemer: "The 'Make America AI-Ready' initiative is designed to ensure every American worker has the chance to learn foundational skills so they can benefit from the opportunities that the AI economy presents"
  • The initiative advances the Trump Administration's America's Talent Strategy and AI Action Plan — and is part of the White House's Pledge to America's Youth to advance AI education across the country, signaling a coordinated federal push toward universal AI literacy
  • Deputy Secretary Keith Sonderling: "We are seeing AI create new jobs, new levels of productivity, and new forms of entrepreneurship, and we want to make sure all Americans have the skills to share in that prosperity" — framing AI literacy as an economic equity issue

RAND Corporation — American Youth Panel Survey, December 2025

RAND Report: 67% of Students Fear AI Is Harming Their Critical Thinking — Even as Homework AI Use Surges to 62%

67%
  • A nationally representative RAND Corporation survey of 1,214 youth (ages 12–29) found that 67% of students endorsed the statement "The more students use AI for their schoolwork, the more it will harm their critical thinking skills" — up more than 10 percentage points from ten months earlier
  • Despite these fears, AI homework use surged: the share of students using AI for homework rose from 48% in May 2025 to 62% by December 2025, with middle and high schoolers driving the increase — 46% of middle schoolers and 60% of high schoolers now use AI for homework
  • RAND researcher Heather Schwartz: "That initial struggle, the blank page, the math problem that doesn't yet have any steps in it — AI might be giving you a really beautiful explanation, but it's still removing that step for you. And I think it's shortchanging your learning in the process"
  • Students' definition of cheating remains narrow: on most tasks, a majority did not consider AI use to be cheating — the one exception was using AI to get homework answers directly, which 45% of respondents called cheating
  • Only one-third of middle and high school students reported that their school had a rule for using AI in homework — the rest indicated no rule, uncertainty, or teacher-by-teacher variation, leaving students to navigate AI ethics largely on their own

K-12 Dive — White House National Policy Framework for AI

White House Releases National AI Policy Framework — Urges Congress to Mandate Child Protections on AI Platforms

Framework
  • The Trump administration released a four-page National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence calling on Congress to protect children on AI platforms — including mandating that parents be able to oversee their children's privacy settings, screen time, and exposure to AI content
  • The framework recommends "commercially reasonable, privacy protective, age-assurance requirements" for AI tools that children and teens are likely to access, and urges that existing federal child privacy protections apply to AI systems — including limits on data collection for model training and targeted advertising
  • The White House framework advises against preempting state laws protecting children online, and explicitly protects state requirements for AI in public education — including procurement safeguards, AI literacy standards, and teacher training requirements
  • The KIDS Act cleared by the House committee would require AI chatbots to disclose they are "not a natural person" when interacting with minors, and to provide crisis hotline resources if a child asks about suicide — addressing a documented safety gap in AI mental health interactions
  • Kris Hagel, CIO at Peninsula School District: "At the end of the day, we need the protections. We can't just sit and continue to wait for some of this to happen" — reflecting widespread urgency among K-12 administrators for federal action on AI child safety

EdTech Innovation Hub — NSF / Computer Science Teachers Association

NSF Awards $11M to Train 2,500–3,000 K-12 Teachers in AI — Projected to Reach 600,000 Students Across 6+ States

$11M
  • The National Science Foundation awarded $11 million to the Computer Science Teachers Association (CSTA) to launch AI Professional Development Weeks — a multi-state initiative targeting Indiana, South Carolina, Minnesota, New Jersey, Iowa, and Illinois, with additional states expected
  • The program will train between 2,500 and 3,000 teachers over two years, with projected downstream impact reaching up to 600,000 students — combining intensive summer training with ongoing support through local and state networks
  • NSF Acting Director Brian Stone: "American students must be prepared not just to use AI, but to understand it and create with it. This investment will equip thousands of educators with the tools needed to bring AI and computer science into the classroom"
  • CSTA Executive Director Jake Baskin: "AI is arriving in classrooms faster than AI literacy — and that gap is growing. Through AI PD Weeks, CSTA equips educators with the knowledge to move students beyond passive AI use toward deeper understanding, critical thinking, and meaningful creation"
  • The initiative reflects a broader shift in how AI is positioned in education — moving from tool usage toward deeper conceptual understanding, with teachers learning to help students use, evaluate, and build AI systems rather than simply consume them

Education Week — RAND American Youth Panel Survey

EdWeek: Nearly 7 in 10 Middle and High Schoolers Worry AI Is Eroding Their Critical Thinking — Concern Grows Month Over Month

7 in 10
  • Nearly 7 in 10 middle and high school students say they are concerned that using AI for schoolwork is eroding their critical thinking skills — and the worry is growing: among middle schoolers, concern rose from 48% in May 2025 to 68% by December 2025
  • College students show the highest concern at 70%, while high schoolers rose from 55% to 65% over the same period — suggesting that as students advance and rely more heavily on AI, their anxiety about its cognitive effects intensifies
  • General generative AI chatbots — ChatGPT, Claude, and Google Gemini — are far more popular than purpose-built educational tools: 49% of middle schoolers and 61% of high schoolers report using AI chatbots, dwarfing usage of tutoring or mental health AI tools
  • High school students' most common AI use is brainstorming and getting better explanations (37%), while middle schoolers most commonly use AI to look up facts (32%) — both use cases that bypass the productive struggle that builds deep learning
  • RAND researcher Heather Schwartz: "The older the student, the more kids are using it for more classes, for more purposes. My sense is that younger students are going to progressively use this more and more" — signaling that K-12 AI governance must scale with adoption

Education Week — American Educational Research Association / Stanford University

AI in Student Testing: Promise and Peril — Stanford Researcher Warns of Bias Risks as Districts Explore AI-Assisted Scoring

Balance
  • AI is showing promise in expediting assessment scoring and delivering faster feedback to students — but Stanford education professor Victor Lee warns that "if it's accelerating with a lot of risks associated, that's something we want to be really cautious about"
  • In a 2024 EdWeek Research Center survey, 36% of teachers, school leaders, and district leaders said they think AI will make standardized testing worse in five years — while only 19% believed it might improve assessments, reflecting widespread skepticism among educators
  • Arizona State University's Daniella McNamara argues districts need to establish "baseline literacy" on how AI works so educators understand its capabilities and shortcomings regardless of which new tools emerge — treating AI literacy as foundational infrastructure
  • Lee's golden rule for AI in assessment: "Use AI for things with others that you would want them to use AI on with you" — emphasizing that human review of AI-generated feedback must remain central to avoid bias and preserve the relational dimension of assessment
  • Experts agree that humans must remain actively involved in the assessment process: "Because the AI doesn't know information about students contextually, leaving the AI to make all the decisions is not a good policy," Lee said — underscoring the irreplaceable role of teacher judgment

Center on Reinventing Public Education — State AI Early Adopter Database

CRPE Report: 20 States Now Leading on AI in K-12 — Pattern of "Cautious Exploration" Emerges Across Red, Blue, and Divided States

20 States
  • CRPE's new State AI Early Adopter Database documents AI-related actions across 20 early adopter states in 2024 and 2025 — including guidance documents, legislation, professional learning initiatives, pilot programs, and industry partnerships — revealing a consistent national pattern
  • Key finding: most states are choosing "cautious exploration over sweeping mandates" — issuing non-binding guidance and updating it frequently, signaling priorities while preserving local control and avoiding premature regulatory lock-in
  • Nearly all early adopter states are investing in AI literacy and educator capacity-building as their first priority — while about half are testing AI tools through pilots rather than mandating adoption, reflecting a "learn before you leap" approach
  • Critically, politics is not the main divider: early AI strategies look similar across red, blue, and divided states — suggesting that AI education policy is emerging as a bipartisan infrastructure issue rather than an ideological battleground
  • CRPE identifies growing pressure on states to clarify expectations, strengthen procurement and data privacy guardrails, and address infrastructure gaps — particularly as AI adoption accelerates faster than governance frameworks can keep pace

ASAP

Key Themes Emerging

Federal AI Literacy as a Workforce Equity Issue

The Department of Labor's "Make America AI-Ready" initiative — delivering AI literacy via text message to ensure access for workers without laptops or reliable internet — represents a pivotal reframing of AI education as an economic equity imperative. By partnering with Arist to deliver a seven-day, 10-minutes-per-day course through the most universally accessible technology available, the DOL is acknowledging that the digital divide and the AI literacy gap are the same problem. This initiative, combined with the White House's National AI Policy Framework and the NSF's $11 million teacher training grant, signals that the federal government is now treating AI literacy as foundational infrastructure — as essential as roads or broadband — rather than a niche workforce development program.

The Critical Thinking Paradox

The RAND Corporation's finding that 67% of students fear AI is harming their critical thinking — even as 62% use it for homework — captures a profound paradox at the heart of AI in education. Students are simultaneously the most worried and the most dependent users of AI tools, caught between their own cognitive instincts and the frictionless convenience of AI assistance. RAND researcher Heather Schwartz's concept of "cognitive offloading" versus "cognitive augmentation" offers a practical framework for educators: the question is not whether students use AI, but whether that use replaces or deepens their thinking. The challenge for K-12 institutions is to design learning environments where AI serves as a scaffold for deeper engagement rather than a shortcut around it.

Child Safety Becomes the AI Policy Flashpoint

The White House's National Policy Framework for AI, the KIDS Act advancing through the House, and documented cases of AI chatbots making harmful suggestions to minors have converged to make child safety the most politically urgent dimension of AI education policy. The framework's recommendation that AI chatbots disclose they are "not a natural person" to minors, and provide crisis resources when children ask about self-harm, reflects growing recognition that AI systems designed for adult users are being accessed by children in unsupervised contexts. The bipartisan momentum behind child AI safety legislation — and the White House's explicit protection of state-level AI education requirements from federal preemption — suggests that child protection may be the policy area where federal AI governance advances fastest.

States Lead While Federal Policy Catches Up

CRPE's State AI Early Adopter Database reveals that 20 states have already moved ahead with AI education guidance, legislation, and pilot programs — often without clear federal direction. The finding that early AI strategies look similar across red, blue, and divided states challenges the assumption that AI education policy will follow partisan lines. Instead, states are converging on a common approach: non-binding guidance, professional learning investment, and cautious piloting before scale. This bipartisan consensus at the state level may prove more durable than federal frameworks, and the CRPE database provides the first systematic evidence base for understanding what is actually working — and what gaps remain — as states navigate AI amid uncertainty and limited capacity.

AI Assessment: Acceleration with Guardrails

The emerging consensus from Stanford, Arizona State University, and the American Educational Research Association is that AI can meaningfully accelerate assessment scoring and feedback — but only if human educators remain actively involved in reviewing, contextualizing, and personalizing AI outputs. The 36% of educators who believe AI will make standardized testing worse reflects legitimate concerns about bias, inaccuracy, and the loss of the relational dimension of assessment. Victor Lee's principle — "use AI for things with others that you would want them to use AI on with you" — offers a practical ethical standard that places student experience at the center of AI adoption decisions, rather than efficiency or cost reduction.

Universities Scale AI Literacy from Degrees to Free Video Series

The simultaneous launch of UW-Whitewater's Bachelor of Science in AI, the Universities of Wisconsin's free ASAP video series, and the NSF NAIRR AI Education Fellowship program illustrates how universities are building AI literacy capacity across the full spectrum — from introductory public education to advanced degree programs. The UW system's strategy of pairing new AI majors with a free statewide video series reflects an understanding that AI literacy is not just a credential but a public good. As UW President Jay Rothman framed it, extending AI knowledge "outward" to all Wisconsinites — regardless of whether they enroll in a university — represents a new model of public higher education's role in preparing communities for technological change.
