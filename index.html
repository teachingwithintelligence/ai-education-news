Chalkbeat New York — NYC Department of Education

NYC Releases Landmark AI School Policy — "Traffic Light" Framework Governs 1 Million Students

1M+
  • New York City's Department of Education released its long-awaited preliminary AI guidance on March 24, 2026 — nearly three years after a short-lived ChatGPT ban — establishing a "traffic light" framework governing AI use across the nation's largest school district, serving over one million students
  • Green-light uses include brainstorming lesson plans and drafting non-critical communications; yellow-light cases such as finding trends in student data, generating translations for bilingual learners, and adapting materials for students with disabilities require trained professional review before use with students
  • All decisions about students — including grading, special education and 504 plans, discipline, counseling, crisis intervention, and academic placement — are strictly prohibited as "red light" cases and are not expected to change in the final June 2026 playbook
  • Personal student information can never be entered into unapproved AI tools, and under no circumstances can student data be used to generate revenue or train AI models — though data privacy experts warn the guidance alone cannot guarantee protection when relying on third-party products
  • Every AI tool must pass through the department's Enterprise Request Management Application vetting process before classroom use; the city is seeking public feedback through May 8, 2026 via webinars, events, and an online survey
  • Schools Chancellor Kamar Samuels: "AI is here, and our responsibility is to put strong systemwide safeguards in place" — while critics argue that key questions about homework use and personal AI accounts remain unresolved ahead of the final June release

Education Week — RAND Corporation Survey

RAND Survey: 7 in 10 Students Fear AI Is Eroding Their Critical Thinking Skills

70%
  • A new RAND Corporation survey of middle, high school, and college students finds nearly 7 in 10 middle and high schoolers are concerned that using AI for schoolwork is eroding their critical thinking skills — a figure that has risen sharply since early 2025
  • The percentage of middle school students using AI for homework rose from 30% to 46% between May and December 2025; high schoolers rose from 49% to 60% over the same period — indicating rapid adoption even as concerns mount
  • 48% of middle school students expressed concern about AI harming their critical thinking in February 2025; that number has climbed significantly by early 2026, suggesting the worry intensifies as AI use becomes more habitual
  • Students' own perceptions that AI is undermining their cognitive development should give educators serious pause, researchers say — as the technology increasingly serves as a shortcut rather than a scaffold for learning
  • The findings arrive as schools struggle to define appropriate AI use for homework, with many districts still lacking clear policies on whether AI assistance constitutes academic support or academic dishonesty

K&L Gates / White House — National AI Policy Framework

White House Releases National AI Policy Framework — Calls for Congress to Protect Children and Integrate AI Training

Federal
  • The White House released its National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence on March 20, 2026 — outlining legislative recommendations intended to guide Congress as it considers federal AI legislation, marking the Administration's next major step following President Trump's December 2025 executive order
  • On workforce and education, the Framework calls for integrating AI training into existing education and workforce programs, studying AI-driven labor-market impacts, and supporting land-grant institutions in developing AI-related skills — rather than creating new standalone federal programs
  • The Framework explicitly urges Congress to "protect children" and "empower parents" to manage their children's AI use — signaling that child safety is emerging as a central axis of federal AI education policy alongside the Administration's push to accelerate AI adoption
  • Congress is called on to bolster capabilities at land-grant institutions to provide technical assistance, launch demonstration projects, and develop AI youth development programs — reflecting a rural and tribal education equity dimension to the federal AI agenda
  • The Framework arrives simultaneously with the Department of Labor's "Make America AI-Ready" initiative, launched March 24, 2026, aimed at equipping American workers with foundational AI skills needed to succeed in an AI-driven economy

Government Technology — Louisiana Board of Regents

LSU Launches First-in-State AI Bachelor's Degree — Louisiana Also Creates New 3-Year "AccB" Degree Track

New
  • The Louisiana Board of Regents approved a Bachelor of Science in Artificial Intelligence at LSU on March 25, 2026 — the first AI degree of its kind in the state — with a curriculum built on rigorous mathematics, computer science, and AI theory, focused on training AI engineers rather than AI users
  • "It is a program which is not to teach people about AI, but it is a program to teach people how to be AI engineers," said Tristan Denley, deputy commissioner for academic affairs — targeting energy, petrochemical, healthcare, defense, and logistics employers actively integrating AI for predictive analytics and digital transformation
  • The Board simultaneously established a new 90-credit-hour accelerated bachelor's degree (abbreviated "AccB") — a "tall, skinny degree" that maintains depth while reducing breadth, allowing up to 12 general education hours to be replaced by discipline-specific coursework
  • The first two AccB programs approved — information technology and bioinformatics at LSU-Alexandria — are designed to get students into the workforce faster and at lower cost, with Chancellor Paul Coreil noting: "At higher ed, one of the criticisms is we move too slow. You're not moving slow now."
  • The moves align with Louisiana lawmakers' push to mold higher education around industry needs, including pending legislation that would prohibit state funds for degrees with low earning potential — mirroring the federal "do no harm" framework for student loan recipients

Entry-Level Jobs Down 35% in 18 Months — WEF Argues AI Is Redefining, Not Eliminating, Early-Career Work

-35%
  • Postings for entry-level jobs in the US have plummeted 35% in the last 18 months, in large part because of AI, according to research firm Revelio Labs — as leaders across industries embrace AI to handle foundational tasks from data entry to coding to customer support
  • Work previously assumed to be done by AI in early-career roles is simply being pushed upward — leaving middle management and senior talent overextended, burned out, and increasingly disengaged as they absorb junior tasks
  • Cognizant hired 25,000 fresh graduates in 2025 and expects to exceed that number in 2026, arguing that digital-native early-career workers who ramp up quickly with AI represent a significant competitive advantage over more senior colleagues with higher change-management curves
  • New entry-level roles are being redefined around judgment calls and AI oversight rather than task execution — reviewing AI outputs, flagging complex cases, surfacing AI-generated insights to senior teams, and monitoring workflows for breakdowns and inefficiencies
  • Hiring managers must now assess not just AI experience but AI reasoning — identifying candidates who understand when AI is appropriate, how to question AI output, and how to improve AI prompts and workflows — a skill set that universities are only beginning to formally teach

Key Themes Emerging

Policy Scramble: Cities and Federal Government Race to Govern AI in Schools

NYC's "traffic light" AI framework — released nearly three years after its ChatGPT ban — illustrates the governance gap that has opened between AI's rapid classroom adoption and institutional readiness. With the White House simultaneously releasing a National AI Policy Framework urging Congress to protect children and integrate AI training, and the DOL launching "Make America AI-Ready," the federal and local policy landscape is becoming more crowded but not yet coherent. The tension between promoting AI adoption and protecting students from its risks is the defining challenge for education policymakers in 2026.

Student AI Anxiety: Critical Thinking Fears Rise Alongside Adoption

The RAND finding that 70% of students fear AI is eroding their critical thinking skills — even as homework AI use climbs from 30% to 60% among middle and high schoolers — reveals a troubling paradox at the heart of AI's educational integration. Students are using AI more while trusting it less, and worrying about its cognitive effects even as they rely on it for academic work. The UIC survey's finding that 95% of students feel confident using AI responsibly, yet remain deeply anxious about academic integrity, suggests that confidence and concern are not mutually exclusive — and that the education system has not yet provided students with the ethical frameworks they need.

University AI Degree Proliferation Reaches Tipping Point

With 304 U.S. institutions now offering AI degree programs and LSU joining RIT, Northwestern, and UW-Madison in launching new bachelor's degrees, higher education is undergoing a structural transformation in how it prepares students for an AI-transformed workforce. Louisiana's simultaneous creation of the 3-year "AccB" degree track signals that institutions are also rethinking the architecture of degrees themselves — compressing timelines, aligning more tightly with labor market demand, and questioning whether the traditional 4-year, 120-credit model remains appropriate for a rapidly changing economy.

K-12 AI Literacy Becomes a Municipal Priority

Boston's initiative to ensure every high school graduate achieves AI literacy — backed by tech philanthropy and Mayor Michelle Wu — joins Chicago's Alpha School expansion and NYC's policy framework as evidence that AI literacy is becoming a municipal education priority, not just a federal aspiration. The convergence of National AI Literacy Day, the DOL's "Make America AI-Ready" initiative, and city-level programs reflects a recognition that AI fluency is now a civic skill as fundamental as reading and numeracy. The challenge is ensuring that these initiatives reach all students equitably, not just those in well-resourced districts or private schools.

Entry-Level Work Redefined: AI Oversight Becomes the New Junior Role

The WEF analysis of AI's 35% reduction in entry-level job postings offers a more nuanced picture than simple displacement: early-career work is being redefined around judgment, AI oversight, and insight synthesis rather than task execution. Organizations that cut junior talent to capture short-term AI efficiency gains risk weakening succession plans and losing the digital-native workers best positioned to accelerate AI adoption. For universities, this means producing graduates who understand not just how to use AI, but when to question it — a skill set that requires both technical fluency and critical thinking, precisely the combination that the RAND survey suggests students fear AI is undermining.

AI School Models Challenge Public Education's Pace

Alpha School's Chicago expansion highlights the emerging two-track AI education landscape: private schools and ed-tech companies moving rapidly to implement AI-personalized learning, while public school districts navigate governance, equity, and policy constraints that slow adoption. The model's claim that two hours of AI-driven instruction can replace a full academic day raises profound questions about what school is for — and whether the remaining time should be devoted to the human skills AI cannot replicate. As AI-based private schools expand into major cities, public education systems face pressure to demonstrate that their more cautious, equity-conscious approach to AI adoption produces better outcomes for all students.
