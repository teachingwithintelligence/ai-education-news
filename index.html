U.S. Senate Commerce Committee

Bipartisan NSF AI Education Act of 2026 Introduced — Scholarships, K-12 Guidance & 5 Community College AI Hubs

🏛️
  • Senators Cantwell (D-WA) and Moran (R-KS) introduced the bipartisan NSF AI Education Act of 2026 today, authorizing the National Science Foundation to expand AI scholarship and professional development opportunities nationwide
  • The bill would create undergraduate and graduate scholarships for AI study — including AI applications in agriculture, education, and advanced manufacturing — plus fellowships for current STEM and education workers
  • NSF would be directed to collaborate with educators and academics to develop publicly available K-12 AI guidance, with a focus on tools for low-income, rural, and tribal students
  • At least five "Centers of AI Excellence" would be established at community colleges and vocational schools in coordination with the CHIPS & Science Act's Regional Technology and Innovation Hub Program
  • An NSF Grand Challenge would be created to find strategies to educate 1 million or more U.S. workers on AI by 2028, with an explicit mandate not to displace existing teachers or workers
  • The bill also authorizes AI research grants through Land-Grant Universities and the Cooperative Extension Service to bring AI tools to farmers and rural communities
  • Sen. Cantwell: "Demand for AI expertise is already high and will continue to grow — we must meet that demand if we are to out-compete China and make the American AI tech stack the global standard"

NPR

College Students & Professors Are Making Their Own AI Rules — And They Don't Always Agree

85%
  • A new NPR investigation finds that three years after ChatGPT's debut, students and faculty are still navigating deeply divided views on AI use — with no institutional consensus in sight
  • A poll by Inside Higher Ed and the Generation Lab found about 85% of undergraduates were using AI for coursework, including brainstorming, outlining papers, and studying for exams
  • Roughly 19% of students reported using AI to write full essays — raising alarms about academic integrity and the erosion of writing as a cognitive skill-building exercise
  • More than half of students who used AI for coursework had mixed feelings, reporting it helps sometimes but can also make them think less deeply
  • English professor Dan Cryer compares AI essay writing to "bringing a forklift to the gym" — the weights get moved, but the muscles never develop
  • Duke pre-med student Anjali Tatini uses AI as an on-demand tutor for complex concepts, but draws the line at having it write for her: "If I'm putting something out, I want it to be something I'm proud to say is mine"
  • UNC junior Hannah Elder advocates for AI integration in curricula so students learn to see it as a tool rather than a cheat code: "We can't deny it's going to be part of the college experience"

The 74 Million

Two New Reports Warn Against AI Extremes in Schools — Urge 'Human-Centered' Adoption to Avoid 'Worst of Both Worlds'

⚠️
  • Two major new reports released today — one from AI for Education and one from the Center on Reinventing Public Education (CRPE) — warn that schools face a critical fork in the road on AI adoption
  • The AI for Education report uses scenario modeling to show that both extremes — banning AI entirely or going all-in on automated instruction — produce worse outcomes than a balanced, human-centered approach
  • Districts that ban AI see students develop "school brain," using AI at home without guidance and producing shallow understanding; test scores plummet and 40% of graduates land on academic probation in the modeled scenario
  • Districts that fully automate instruction see students gaming algorithms, teachers defending grades they didn't assign, and graduates who struggle with collaboration, creativity, and adaptability
  • The recommended "third path" redesigns learning around a graduate profile emphasizing critical thinking, ethical reasoning, and human-AI collaboration — with students learning to use AI as a tool that supports but doesn't replace their thinking
  • CRPE executive director Robin Lake warns of an "efficiency paradox": AI could make schools faster and cheaper without addressing students' actual needs — like the Scantron reinforcing low-level testing for decades
  • Both reports call for an "ambidextrous" approach: improving current practices with AI while intentionally building toward fundamentally new learning models

College Board Research

College Board: 92% of Faculty Fear AI Plagiarism — 84% Say AI Reduces Critical Thinking in Students

92%
  • College Board survey of 3,000+ U.S. college faculty finds 92% are concerned about plagiarism or dishonesty facilitated by AI — a near-universal level of alarm across institutions and disciplines
  • More than 84% of faculty agree that AI reduces students' critical thinking, originality, and deep engagement with course material; 88% are concerned about overreliance on automation
  • 74% of faculty report students are using AI to write essays or papers; 67% say students use it to paraphrase or rewrite content; nearly half believe at least half of their students use AI for writing tasks
  • Faculty sentiment skews negative: 45% report a negative view of AI in higher education vs. 34% positive — yet 77% have used AI themselves in their professional role
  • Faculty at more selective institutions report higher levels of student AI use and greater concern; faculty at open-enrollment colleges are more likely to view AI as a practical instructional tool
  • Writing-intensive disciplines (English, history, humanities) report the highest classroom disruption; STEM and business faculty are more likely to use AI themselves and view it positively
  • Only 21% of faculty feel "very confident" guiding AI use in their classrooms; 79% say they are still beginning to explore what's needed or need more guidance

Fortune / Pew Research Center

Pew Research: 54% of Teens Use AI for Schoolwork — But 64% of Parents Don't Know Their Child Is Using It

54%
  • A new Pew Research Center survey of 1,458 U.S. teens finds 57% use AI to search for information and 54% use it specifically to help with schoolwork — marking a shift from experimentation to routine use
  • Four in 10 teens use chatbots to summarize articles, books, and videos; one in 10 admits to using AI for most or all of their schoolwork
  • A significant "perception gap" exists between teen use and parental awareness: 64% of teens report using chatbots, but only 51% of parents think their teen uses AI — and nearly 30% of parents have no idea
  • Despite widespread use, about 25% of teens are concerned about AI's impact on society, with the most common worry being overreliance and loss of creativity and critical thinking skills
  • A Brookings Institution report calls generative AI the "fast food of education" — providing "frictionless" outcomes that subtract from the learning experience
  • The majority of parents (64%) approve of teens using AI for article and book summaries; 58% approve for homework help — suggesting parents are more permissive than educators
  • About 46% of high school students now use AI tools during their college search process, up sharply from 26% in spring 2025, per a separate EAB survey of 5,000+ students

Key Themes Emerging

Federal Legislation Takes Shape

The bipartisan NSF AI Education Act of 2026, introduced today by Senators Cantwell and Moran, represents the most comprehensive federal AI education bill to date — covering scholarships, K-12 classroom guidance, community college hubs, and a goal of training 1 million workers by 2028. It signals that Congress is moving from debate to action, though implementation timelines remain uncertain.

The AI Policy Vacuum in Schools

With only 13% of districts having communicated clear AI policies to students and teachers, and 44% of educators receiving zero professional development, schools are operating in a dangerous vacuum. The House subcommittee hearing this week underscored bipartisan urgency for federal guidance, while California's new safeguards — prompted by an AI-generated image scandal in a 4th-grade classroom — show what happens when policy lags behind technology.

The 'Human-Centered' AI Imperative

Two major reports released today converge on the same warning: neither banning AI nor fully automating instruction produces good outcomes for students. The emerging consensus is a "third path" that treats AI as a tool supporting — not replacing — human thinking, with school redesigns centered on critical thinking, ethical reasoning, and human-AI collaboration as core graduate competencies.

Student AI Use Outpaces Institutional Response

With 85% of undergraduates and 54% of teens already using AI regularly, and 19% of college students using it to write full essays, the gap between student behavior and institutional policy has never been wider. The NPR investigation published today captures this tension vividly: students are making their own rules, professors are making their own rules, and they don't always agree — leaving academic integrity in a precarious gray zone.

Faculty Alarm Reaches Near-Universal Levels

The College Board's survey of 3,000+ faculty reveals a profession in crisis: 92% fear AI plagiarism, 84% believe AI undermines critical thinking, yet 77% use AI themselves. The divide between writing-intensive and STEM disciplines is sharpening, with humanities faculty experiencing the most disruption while remaining the least equipped with institutional support or clear guidance from administrators.

Teen AI Use Outpaces Parental Awareness

The Pew Research finding that nearly 30% of parents have no idea whether their teen uses AI — while 54% of teens use it for schoolwork — reveals a generational awareness gap with serious implications. As AI becomes embedded in homework, college searches, and daily learning, the absence of family conversations about responsible use mirrors the early days of social media, when parental oversight lagged dangerously behind adolescent adoption.
