Inside Higher Ed — Cornell University College of Agriculture and Life Sciences

Cornell Launches 75-Minute Critical Thinking Module to Counter AI Dependency — 7,000 Students Enrolled So Far

7,000
  • Cornell University researchers developed an asynchronous, 75-minute online module to help students build critical thinking skills in the AI era — piloted in six introductory courses since 2022 and now used across multiple classes, with roughly 7,000 students having completed it
  • A faculty survey found that approximately 83% of first-year students lacked sufficient critical thinking skills — prompting the module's development to give students a shared language and foundational framework for evaluating information, AI outputs, and competing viewpoints
  • The module breaks critical thinking into specific, learnable skills: accessing relevant information, evaluating different points of view, considering evidence that challenges a line of reasoning, and accepting ambiguity — all of which are essential for evaluating AI-generated content
  • Before completing the module, students described critical thinking using fixed, innate concepts like "brain," "intelligence," and "hard." After completing it, words like "curiosity," "perception," and "bias" became most popular — demonstrating a shift from viewing CT as innate to seeing it as developable
  • Christina Schmidt, co-developer of the module: "We're at a pivotal moment where AI is effectively forcing higher ed to examine the unique value we provide. The module gets the conversation started and gives students the foundational language to distinguish what's artificial from what's human intelligence"
  • A recent Inside Higher Ed Student Voice survey found that about 35% of students are seeking roles that emphasize uniquely human skills — while a Stanford Learning Society study found AI is "transforming demand for human capabilities, reshaping work and workers as much as it is replacing tasks"

Fast Company — K-12 AI Adoption Survey 2025–2026

85% of K-12 Teachers Now Use AI — But Only 35% of Districts Have Provided Students Any AI Training

85%
  • An estimated 85% of K-12 public school teachers reported using AI during the 2024–2025 school year, primarily for curriculum and content development — a dramatic rise from prior years that signals AI has become a mainstream professional tool for educators
  • 86% of K-12 students have used AI in general, and 50% report using it specifically for schoolwork — including tutoring, homework help, research, and college advice — while the share of teens using ChatGPT for schoolwork doubled from 13% in 2023 to 26% in 2025
  • Despite surging adoption, policies and training have not kept pace: only 35% of school district leaders reported providing students with any AI training, and only 45% of principals reported having school or district AI policies in place (RAND Corporation, 2025)
  • A critical safety gap has emerged alongside academic concerns: students are turning to AI chatbots for mental health support, with a 2025 study finding that chatbots responding to 60 simulated mental health scenarios sometimes made harmful proposals — including cutting off all human contact or dropping out of school
  • The gap between AI adoption and AI governance is widening fastest in under-resourced districts, where neither teachers nor students have received structured guidance on responsible AI use — creating uneven outcomes that risk deepening existing educational inequities

NSF Selects 25 Faculty as Inaugural NAIRR AI Education Fellows — Spanning 23 States to Advance Undergraduate AI Teaching

25

AI-Native

ETHRWorld / Stanford University — Graduate Employment Study

Stanford Study: 13% Drop in Employment for 22–25-Year-Olds in AI-Exposed Roles — Entry-Level Postings Fall by a Third

13%
  • A Stanford University study reports a 13% fall in employment among 22–25-year-olds in roles heavily exposed to AI — while entry-level job postings have fallen by nearly a third in the past year, and graduate unemployment has overtaken overall unemployment for the first time in decades
  • The "diamond organization" is replacing the traditional pyramid: a thinner base of entry-level employees, a wider middle of experienced professionals who interpret AI outputs, and a slim top of strategists — eliminating the entry-level roles that once served as the training ground for future leaders
  • The "pipeline paradox" is emerging: cutting entry-level roles today eliminates the pathway that produces mid-level managers in five years — risking a leadership drought, wage inflation, aggressive poaching, and long-term capability loss for organizations that prioritize short-term AI efficiency gains
  • Universities are being called to respond: programs need more experiential learning, industry projects, and AI literacy to ensure graduates are genuinely work-ready — shifting from information transfer to application, and building the human capabilities (critical thinking, empathy, creativity) that AI cannot replicate
  • Microsoft is investing heavily in AI literacy for employees; Amazon has committed over $1 billion to reskilling; Pfizer's Breakthrough Fellowship blends internships, full-time roles, and graduate study — signaling that companies building talent pipelines today will shape leadership tomorrow

2026

Key Themes Emerging

Critical Thinking as the Core Competency

Cornell's 75-minute module — which has shifted 7,000 students' understanding of critical thinking from an innate trait to a learnable skill — represents a broader institutional recognition that AI's greatest educational risk is not cheating but cognitive displacement. When 83% of first-year students arrive without sufficient critical thinking skills, and AI tools offer frictionless shortcuts around the very tasks that build those skills, higher education faces an urgent mandate: teach thinking explicitly, or watch it erode. The module's discipline-independent design signals that this is not a problem for any single department — it is a foundational challenge for the entire university.

The AI-Native Graduate Paradox

Reddit CEO Steve Huffman's declaration that his company will "go heavy" on hiring AI-native graduates — even as entry-level postings fall by a third and graduate unemployment hits a 37-year high — captures a profound paradox at the heart of the AI economy. Gen Z graduates are simultaneously the most capable AI users and the most displaced workers, as the very tasks that once gave them their professional start are automated away. The companies that recognize AI fluency as a competitive advantage rather than a threat are positioning themselves to win the talent wars of the next decade — while those cutting entry-level hiring risk the pipeline paradox: no juniors today means no mid-level managers in five years.

The Policy Gap Widens

The simultaneous introduction of the NSF AI Education Act of 2026 and the HBCU AI Research Leadership Act signals growing congressional recognition that federal AI education policy has lagged dangerously behind adoption. With only 35% of school districts providing students any AI training and only 45% of principals having AI policies in place, the governance vacuum is most acute at the K-12 level — precisely where the White House's National AI Policy Framework left the largest gap. The Cantwell-Moran bill's focus on community college AI Centers of Excellence is particularly significant: it acknowledges that AI education equity requires infrastructure investment at the institutions serving the broadest and most economically diverse student populations.

Colleges Compete with AI for Students' Attention

The decline in tutoring requests at Flagler College since 2023, combined with reports that Anthropic pays campus ambassadors to promote Claude and Google offers free AI tools throughout the academic year, reveals a new competitive dynamic in higher education: AI companies are actively recruiting students as users, often during their most stressed and vulnerable moments. Columbia VP Matthew Connelly's characterization of this as targeting "a strapped customer base to hook" raises urgent questions about the commercial interests shaping AI adoption in education. The institutional response — redesigning assessments around process rather than product, building AI literacy into curricula, and clarifying what human instruction uniquely provides — represents higher education's attempt to define its irreplaceable value in an AI-saturated world.

Academic Reorientation: From CS to AI Majors

The first decline in CS enrollment at UC campuses since the dot-com crash, paired with the proliferation of AI-specific majors at dozens of universities, marks a structural shift in how students and parents are reading the future of work. The emergence of interdisciplinary AI programs — combining AI with healthcare, finance, law, and the social sciences — reflects a sophisticated understanding that the most durable careers will not be in AI alone, but in the application of AI expertise to domain-specific problems. MIT's "AI and decision-making" major is emblematic: the future belongs not to those who build AI systems, but to those who know when, how, and whether to deploy them.

Federal Investment in AI Teacher Training Scales Up

The selection of 25 NSF NAIRR AI Education Fellows across 23 states — following the NSF's $11 million K-12 teacher training grant announced last week — signals a coordinated federal strategy to build AI education capacity from the ground up. By targeting both K-12 teachers (through CSTA's professional development initiative) and undergraduate faculty (through the NAIRR fellowship), NSF is attempting to close the AI literacy gap at every level of the education pipeline simultaneously. The geographic breadth of the fellowship cohort — spanning public universities, private colleges, and community colleges across 23 states — reflects a deliberate effort to ensure that AI education capacity is not concentrated at a handful of elite research institutions.
